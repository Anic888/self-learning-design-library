# 1980-2000 — Postmodernism Era

*Grid is shattered, hierarchy destabilized, legibility deprioritized — designers treat the page as expressive material rather than a neutral container for communication.*

## Context
The Macintosh (1984) plus PostScript plus desktop laser printers (LaserWriter, 1985) collapsed the production pipeline into a single authoring tool, letting designers bypass typesetters and phototypesetters. Digital type foundries (Emigre, FontShop, FUSE) put type design itself in the hands of the same people who laid out the page. This era's poster is defined by the tension between (a) rebellion against Swiss/International Style rationalism and (b) the new technical permission that the Mac granted to disassemble it.

## Key movements
- **New Wave / Swiss Punk** (late 70s-80s, Basel) — Weingart's typographic rebellion from inside the Swiss school itself.
- **Memphis Group** (Milan, 1981-1988) — Ettore Sottsass's postmodern product/pattern design influences graphic surface.
- **Émigré** (Berkeley, 1984-2005) — magazine + type foundry that theorized and prototyped the new digital typography.
- **Deconstruction / Cranbrook** (Cranbrook Academy, Bloomfield Hills MI, mid-80s-90s) — Katherine McCoy's program produced a generation of layered, text-as-image designers.
- **The Face / Brody era** (London, 1981-1990) — Neville Brody's art direction at *The Face* made editorial layout into a performance.
- **Grunge / Ray Gun** (1992-2000) — David Carson's anti-design; rule violation as expressive vocabulary.
- **Factory Records / Peter Saville** (Manchester, 1978-1990s) — austere postmodern quotation; album art referencing classical painting, modernist architecture.
- **Paula Scher's Public Theater identity** (1994-) — word-as-image, overlapping wood type, Russian constructivist revival in NYC commercial practice.
- **Barbara Kruger / text-over-image political work** (1981-) — Futura Bold Oblique on red over black-and-white photography; feminist critique of advertising.
- **Stefan Sagmeister early career** (NYC, late 90s) — handmade, body-as-medium, provocatively illustrative.

## Masters of the era
- **Wolfgang Weingart** (1941-2021, German-Swiss) — Basel teacher; deconstructed the Swiss grid from within; staircase and overprint experiments.
- **April Greiman** (1948-, American) — Weingart student; introduced Mac and video aesthetics to graphic design; *Design Quarterly* 133 "Does It Make Sense?" 1986.
- **Dan Friedman** (1945-1995, American) — another Basel-trained American; brought postmodern typography into US commercial work.
- **Rudy VanderLans** (1955-, Dutch-American) — co-founder and designer of *Emigre* magazine.
- **Zuzana Licko** (1961-, Slovak-American) — Emigre type foundry co-founder; designed Emperor, Oakland, Variex, Mrs Eaves.
- **Neville Brody** (1957-, British) — *The Face* art director 1981-86; built custom typefaces (Industria, Insignia, Arcadia) that defined a decade.
- **David Carson** (1955-, American) — *Ray Gun* magazine 1992-95; canonical "anti-grid" practitioner.
- **Paula Scher** (1948-, American) — Pentagram partner from 1991; Public Theater identity; Citibank logo.
- **Peter Saville** (1955-, British) — Factory Records designer; Joy Division *Unknown Pleasures*, New Order *Power, Corruption & Lies*.
- **Stefan Sagmeister** (1962-, Austrian) — NYC studio from 1993; AIGA Detroit self-inflicted poster (1999).
- **Barbara Kruger** (1945-, American) — artist using graphic design language; former *Mademoiselle* art director.
- **Tibor Kalman** (1949-1999, Hungarian-American) — M&Co then *Colors* magazine; vernacular photography + provocative editorial.
- **Jonathan Barnbrook** (1966-, British) — Virus foundry; David Bowie's later album covers (from 2002 but practice begins here).
- **Edward Fella** (1938-, American) — Detroit-based vernacular and hand-lettering explorer; taught at CalArts.
- **Katherine McCoy** (1945-, American) — co-director Cranbrook Academy design program; pedagogy of layered typography.
- **P. Scott Makela** (1960-1999, American) — Cranbrook graduate, digital collage pioneer.
- **Jeffrey Keedy** (1957-, American) — Emigre typeface designer (Keedy Sans, 1989); CalArts faculty.
- **Mike Mills** (1966-, American) — Beastie Boys/Yoshitomo Nara era album design; filmmaker.
- **Tomato collective** (London, 1991-) — John Warwicker, Karl Hyde (Underworld); moving-image and typographic experimentation.
- **Designers Republic** (Sheffield, 1986-2009) — Ian Anderson's studio; Warp Records covers, Pho-Ku aesthetic.
- **Me Company / Paul White** (London, 1985-) — Björk *Post* and *Homogenic* covers; early 3D digital compositing.
- **Vaughan Oliver** (1957-2020, British) — 23 Envelope / v23 studio; 4AD Records (Cocteau Twins, Pixies) from 1983.
- **Ed Fella** (already mentioned — see above, vernacular explorer and CalArts teacher).
- **Jenny Holzer** (1950-, American) — "Truisms" and LED-scroll text works; graphic language without designing posters per se.
- **April Greiman** (already mentioned).
- **Chip Kidd** (1964-, American) — Knopf book jacket designer from 1986; architectonic photographic cover vocabulary.
- **The Designers Republic pseudonym "P.Ho-Ku Corporation"** (DR's imaginary Japanese corporate client).
- **Reid Miles** (1927-1993, American, late work) — Blue Note Records redesigns continuing from earlier era; his language influenced postmodern music graphics.
- **Jamie Reid** (1947-2023, British) — Sex Pistols sleeves (1976-78); the ransom-note punk aesthetic that grunge inherited.
- **Irma Boom** (1960-, Dutch) — book-as-object experimentation from 1991 onward; SHV Think Book (2136 pages, 1996).
- **Pierre di Sciullo** (1961-, French) — Qui? Résiste; experimental typography and code-based type design.

## Visual signatures

### Palettes
- **New Wave / Weingart**: black and one or two acid colors on white — acid yellow (#F5E800), hot pink (#FF2D95), Yves Klein blue (#002FA7).
- **Memphis**: black, white, and candy pastels — mint (#A0D6B4), peach (#FFB6A0), lavender (#C9B1E7), saffron yellow plus primary color accents; patterns of squiggles, triangles, Terrazzo.
- **Émigré early issues**: black on white, occasional spot colors; disciplined because of Mac display limits.
- **Ray Gun**: photography-driven so palettes follow the photo; often duotones in teal-orange or magenta-black; backgrounds of burnt sienna, mustard, deep teal.
- **Factory Records / Saville**: sophisticated austere — black, off-white, sometimes a single pastel (pale green, dusty pink, mint); never punk primaries.
- **Kruger**: red (#C8102E) + black + white exclusively.
- **Brody / The Face**: early issues high-contrast black-and-white; color arrived with Neville's custom typefaces — brick reds, mustards, forest greens.
- **Designers Republic**: Japanese vending-machine primaries plus metallic fluorescents; flat CMYK overprint.

### Typography
- **Weingart**: wide letter-spacing, line-breaks inside words, staircase baselines, overprinted halftones.
- **Emigre bitmap fonts** (Oakland, Emperor): low-resolution, grid-constrained characters as a virtue, not a limitation.
- **Brody's custom faces** (Industria 1989, Arcadia 1990, FF Blur 1992) — geometric sans with humanist warmth; Arcadia is elongated condensed.
- **Carson**: Dingbats for body text (Ray Gun interview with Bryan Ferry, 1994); "never mistake legibility for communication."
- **Kruger**: Futura Bold Oblique exclusively, white knockout on red or black bars.
- **Scher for Public Theater**: overlapping wood-type vernacular revived; saturated colors stacked.
- **Saville**: classical serifs (Bodoni, Baskerville); often centered; sometimes only credits at tiny size.
- **Grunge**: xerox degradation, scan moiré, distressed Helvetica, collaged fragments of pre-existing type.
- **Émigré era typefaces to know**: Emperor (Licko 1985), Oakland (Licko 1985), Variex (Licko 1988), Matrix (Licko 1986), Template Gothic (Barry Deck 1990), Dead History (P. Scott Makela 1990), Keedy Sans (1989), Mason (Jonathan Barnbrook 1992), Exocet (Barnbrook 1991), Mrs Eaves (Licko 1996).

### Composition conventions
- Grid explicitly shown then violated — Weingart puts a grid on the page, then breaks it.
- Layered text — Cranbrook style has 3-5 typographic voices stacked at different opacities and sizes.
- Photography treated as surface, not window — scratched, duotoned, rotated, tiled.
- Margins collapsed or exploded; no standard page edge behavior.
- Text wrapping around irregular shapes, running off the page, intentionally clipped.
- Deliberate asymmetry; centered compositions are used in Saville but always ironic quotation.
- Collage verbs: overlay, overprint, rotate, distress, tear, xerox, misregister.
- Scale shifts extreme: tiny marginalia next to house-sized headline.
- Baselines staircased, jumbled, rotated 90 degrees.
- Text columns run up the side of the page (editorial magazines especially).
- Information hierarchy inverted — credits at top, title buried mid-composition.

### Scale and format
- Ray Gun and Emigre: magazine format (approx 9×12" or 8.5×10.875") — each spread a self-contained poster.
- The Face: standard magazine but oversized 273×203mm (thicker).
- Factory Records sleeves: 12" LP and 7" single formats determine Saville's square compositions.
- Barbara Kruger: exhibition-scale posters and bus-shelter billboards in public space (Public Art Fund NYC).
- Sagmeister album sleeves: CD jewel case 4.75×4.75" — forces micro-scale precision.

### Production techniques
- Offset lithography still primary output; digital pre-press arrives through the 90s.
- **Mac with QuarkXPress** (1987+), then **FreeHand** and **Illustrator**, **Photoshop** (1990+) — the whole software stack emerges here.
- **PageMaker** (1985) first, displaced by QuarkXPress for professional work.
- **Photocopier** as a creative tool — Carson and grunge designers used xerox distortion as texture.
- **Scanning** at medium resolution introduced moiré, compression artifacts, grain that became aesthetic.
- **Screenprint and Risograph** still used for posters, now with digitally generated separations.
- **Fontographer** (1986) and later **FontLab** (1993) enabled independent type foundries.
- **PostScript Type 1** fonts (Adobe, from 1984) gave foundries distribution.
- **Flatbed scanners** introduced the grainy ink-laden texture that defined grunge print.
- **Dye-sublimation proofing printers** (Iris, Scitex) changed pre-press workflow for high-end designers.

### Subject matter conventions
- Magazines (*The Face*, *Emigre*, *Ray Gun*, *I-D*, *Wired* launch 1993, *Colors*) were the poster's sibling surface.
- Music culture — indie rock, rave, electronic, alternative — dominates album art subject.
- Political critique via typographic appropriation (Kruger, Jenny Holzer, ACT UP "Silence = Death").
- Technology futurism — early *Wired* magazine, Designers Republic for Warp.
- Celebrity portraiture decomposed — Brody, Carson, Sagmeister all used the fame-portrait as their raw material.

## What to apply when asked for this era's work

**For New Wave / Weingart Swiss Punk:**
1. Start from a classical Swiss grid, then violate it — angle one element 4-7 degrees, stairstep baselines, set headline across an unexpected baseline.
2. Black and one acid color (yellow, magenta, or electric blue) on white.
3. Use wide letter-spacing (200-400 units) on a classical grotesque (Akzidenz, Helvetica).
4. Overprint two halftones at different angles for texture.
5. Hierarchy implied by position and spacing, not by size contrast alone.

**For Émigré / digital-type experimentation:**
1. Use a bitmap-like low-resolution face (or a simulation) at large scale.
2. Black ink on bright white stock; body text in a classical serif, display in the digital face.
3. Margins and gutters visibly set by software — let the QuarkXPress grid show.
4. Reference the magazine as object — include subtitle, issue number, barcode.

**For Ray Gun / Carson grunge:**
1. Break all legibility rules — distress the text, set body in Dingbats, reverse type out of a photograph at the wrong size.
2. Duotone the photograph in complementary colors.
3. Use whatever typeface is on hand — willful arbitrariness.
4. Scribble in pen over the top of a printed page and scan it back.

**For Factory Records / Saville:**
1. Restrain to one or two classical typefaces (Bodoni, Baskerville, Gill Sans), centered or upper-left anchored.
2. Use pre-existing art quotation — a Renaissance still life, a modernist architecture photo, a weather map.
3. Do not name the band on the cover (Unknown Pleasures has no type).
4. Monochrome or one muted accent color.

**For Paula Scher Public Theater:**
1. Stack wood-type display in saturated flat color blocks.
2. Use gouache-like solid fills — no gradients.
3. Typography IS the image — no photography, no illustration.
4. Titles should overlap, invert figure-ground between words.

**For Barbara Kruger:**
1. Black-and-white photograph (preferably vintage advertisement or propaganda).
2. Red bar across with white Futura Bold Oblique — second-person pronoun ("You/Your").
3. No other color.
4. Message confronts the viewer's complicity.

**For Memphis graphic:**
1. Mix three loud flat colors with black and white.
2. Overlay triangles, squiggles, terrazzo speckles, and grid patterns at different scales.
3. Geometric sans-serif (Futura, Avenir, even Comic Sans if going full pastiche).
4. No drop shadows, no gradients — Memphis is flat.

**For a Neville Brody / *The Face* editorial cover:**
1. Single studio portrait — high-contrast black-and-white, often with a fashion subject.
2. Display typography designed for the issue (Brody frequently drew a fresh headline by hand).
3. Mix one ornamental display with a classical body setting.
4. Accent color is a single saturated brick red, mustard, or forest green.
5. Page number, issue number, price displayed like a piece of data — cornered, rotated, labeled.

**For a Designers Republic / Warp Records cover:**
1. Imagine a fictional Japanese mega-corporation — invent its logo, mascot, product SKUs.
2. Flat saturated primaries (Tokyo vending-machine palette).
3. Technical schematic overlays — exploded diagrams, grid warnings, arrows.
4. Copious small-type information in English + Katakana.
5. Geometric sans (futuristic but era-1990s).

**For a Sagmeister experimental music-package:**
1. Some body-as-medium gesture — text on skin, objects arranged, photographed.
2. Hand-lettering for title (provocatively unskilled or provocatively skilled).
3. Single accent color against a gray/black/white base.
4. Provocation implicit — nudity, pain, honesty.

## What would be anachronistic
- **Instagram / Twitter handles** — no such thing.
- **QR codes** (invented 1994 but uncommon on posters until 2010s).
- **Emoji** (JP mobile phones late 90s, not Western posters).
- **Variable fonts** (technology arrives 2016).
- **Gradient-mesh illustration** (Illustrator gradient mesh arrives 1998, used widely early 2000s).
- **Bento-grid layouts** (2020s pattern).
- **AI-generated imagery** or "Midjourney" textures.
- **Ultra-high-resolution photography** — era used halftone, grainy scans, offset CMYK.
- **Clean Scandinavian minimalism** — wrong decade, wrong ideology.
- **"Vaporwave" pink/cyan Y2K aesthetic** — that's a 2010s revival, not the actual 90s.
- **Retina-crisp UI mockups** — no retina displays existed.
- **Figma screenshots, dark-mode sensibilities** — designers worked on CRT monitors calibrated for print.
- **Spatial / Vision Pro translucency** — no such register existed.
- **Solarpunk optimistic illustration** — post-2015 movement.
- **Responsive-web thinking** — the web was fixed-width tables in this period.

## Influences in / out

**Fed in from:**
- Swiss International Style (Müller-Brockmann, Hofmann) — to be deconstructed by Weingart.
- Dada and constructivism — appropriated by postmodern layering.
- Russian constructivism (Rodchenko, El Lissitzky) — re-entered via Scher's Public Theater and Designers Republic.
- Vernacular commercial printing (Coke signage, diner menus) — celebrated by Edward Fella and M&Co.
- Punk DIY (Jamie Reid for Sex Pistols, 1977) — xerox / cut-and-paste language feeds grunge.
- Phototypesetting errors (overprints, scratches) — aestheticized deliberately.

**Fed forward to:**
- 2000s flat design pushed back against grunge specifically — minimalism was a rejection of Ray Gun excess.
- Contemporary revival of Memphis patterns (late 2010s-2020s "acid graphic" aesthetic).
- 2020s Gen-Z grunge / scrapbook aesthetic on Instagram directly quotes Carson and *i-D*.
- Peter Saville aesthetic persists in quiet luxury brand design (Acne Studios, Aesop).
- Brody's custom typography ethos feeds every 2010s startup that commissions a bespoke sans.
- Barbara Kruger language is the direct ancestor of 2010s protest and meme culture aesthetic.
