# 1960-1980 — Counterculture Era

*The poster reclaims the wall as a site of protest, hallucination, and handmade illustration — image-making escapes Swiss rationalism into psychedelic distortion, Eastern European surrealism, and revolutionary graphics.*

## Context
Cheap offset lithography and the screenprint press put poster production in the hands of artists, students, and political collectives outside corporate studios. The counterculture (anti-war, civil rights, acid rock, anti-colonial liberation) needed propaganda it could print fast and post overnight. Simultaneously, state-subsidized studios in Poland and Cuba created conditions where designers could be highly idiosyncratic because they were insulated from commercial markets.

## Key movements
- **San Francisco psychedelic rock poster** (1965-1971) — Fillmore/Avalon concert posters; hand-lettered, vibrating complementary colors, Art Nouveau revival.
- **Polish School of Poster** (1950s-1980s) — painterly, surreal, often hand-rendered; film and theatre posters as fine art objects.
- **Cuban revolutionary poster** (1959-1980s) — OSPAAAL solidarity posters for Third World liberation; flat graphic silhouette + bold type.
- **Japanese avant-garde poster** — Tadanori Yokoo's collage pop, Kazumasa Nagai's geometric abstraction, Shigeo Fukuda's visual puns and symmetry.
- **Push Pin Studios** (New York, 1954-late 1970s maturity) — illustrated antidote to Swiss modernism; decorative, historical, whimsical.
- **Protest poster wave** (US 1965-1975) — Atelier Populaire (Paris May '68), Black Panther Party (Emory Douglas), anti-Vietnam silkscreen studios, Art Workers Coalition.
- **Op Art adjacency** — Bridget Riley's black-and-white optical painting enters posters and fashion graphics via mod graphic design.
- **Corita Kent's activist pop** — serigraph prayers against war, using supermarket typography as sacred text.
- **British psychedelic / Hapshash and the Coloured Coat** (London, 1967-1969) — Michael English & Nigel Waymouth; UFO Club posters; UK parallel to Fillmore scene.
- **Chicano movement poster** (US Southwest, 1968-1980s) — Rupert García, Ester Hernández, Royal Chicano Air Force; silkscreen agit-graphics for farmworker and immigrant-rights causes.
- **Swiss late-phase International Style** (continuing background) — Müller-Brockmann, Armin Hofmann, Wolfgang Weingart's early period; the orthodoxy this era was largely reacting against.
- **Environmental / Earth Day poster** (US, 1970-) — first Earth Day April 22, 1970; ecological-activist visual language emerges (Robert Rauschenberg, Milton Glaser Earth Day series).

## Masters of the era
- **Wes Wilson** (1937-2020, American) — inventor of the psychedelic lettering idiom; Fillmore posters 1966-67 for Bill Graham.
- **Victor Moscoso** (1936-, Spanish-American) — Neon Rose series; Color Field color theory applied to vibrating rock posters.
- **Rick Griffin** (1944-1991, American) — surfer/comic roots; Grateful Dead "Aoxomoxoa" 1969; elaborate hand-lettering with eyeballs.
- **Stanley Mouse** (1940-, American) — half of Mouse & Kelley; Grateful Dead skull-and-roses.
- **Alton Kelley** (1940-2008, American) — other half of Mouse & Kelley; Family Dog posters at the Avalon Ballroom.
- **Bonnie MacLean** (1939-2020, American) — succeeded Wilson at Fillmore in 1967; continued the house style.
- **Henryk Tomaszewski** (1914-2005, Polish) — founding father of the Polish School; painterly, gestural, often with hand-lettering inside the image.
- **Jan Lenica** (1928-2001, Polish) — Art Nouveau revivalist; flat color, sinuous contours, literary mood.
- **Roman Cieślewicz** (1930-1996, Polish-French) — photomontage and halftone; worked between Warsaw and Paris, at Elle and Vogue.
- **Waldemar Świerzy** (1931-2013, Polish) — painterly portraiture; thousands of posters including a famous Jimi Hendrix.
- **Felix Beltrán** (1938-2022, Cuban) — graphic identity for Cuban cultural institutions; disciplined flat color revolutionary posters.
- **Tadanori Yokoo** (1936-, Japanese) — psychedelic collage, ukiyo-e references, self-as-subject mythology.
- **Shigeo Fukuda** (1932-2009, Japanese) — visual paradox, figure-ground inversions, perfect symmetry, anti-war posters.
- **Kazumasa Nagai** (1929-, Japanese) — geometric abstraction, animal silhouettes, reductive emblem-making.
- **Seymour Chwast** (1931-, American) — co-founder of Push Pin Studios; illustrated posters, woodcut revival, anti-war "End Bad Breath" 1968.
- **Milton Glaser** (1929-2020, American) — "I ♥ NY" 1977, Dylan silhouette poster 1966; storytelling illustration as mass communication.
- **Emory Douglas** (1943-, American) — Minister of Culture, Black Panther Party; weekly newspaper illustration and poster agitprop.
- **Corita Kent** (1918-1986, American) — Catholic nun, serigraph artist; typography-driven social justice pieces from Immaculate Heart College.
- **Atelier Populaire** (collective, Paris 1968) — anonymous screenprinters at the École des Beaux-Arts during May '68 strike.
- **Michael English** (1941-2009, British) + **Nigel Waymouth** (1941-, British) — Hapshash and the Coloured Coat; UFO Club posters in London with silver-ink metallic overlays.
- **Robert Crumb** (1943-, American) — underground comix (*Zap Comix* 1968); poster spillover; hatched line style.
- **Gary Grimshaw** (1946-2014, American) — Detroit underground poster scene; MC5 and Grande Ballroom posters.
- **Jim Phillips** (1944-, American) — Santa Cruz surf and skate poster illustration; later Santa Cruz Skateboards screaming hand 1985.
- **Rupert García** (1941-, Chicano-American) — silkscreen activist posters; Third World Liberation Front (TWLF) graphics; San Francisco State 1968 strike.
- **Ester Hernández** (1944-, Chicana-American) — "Sun Mad" (1982, late-period) repurposing Sun-Maid raisin logo to protest pesticides.
- **Armando Milani** (1940-, Italian) — humanitarian poster; UN-commissioned peace and AIDS-awareness work.
- **Paul Davis** (1938-, American) — Push Pin illustrator; *Theatre Workshop*, George Nelson.
- **James McMullan** (1934-, American) — Push Pin later, Lincoln Center Theatre posters.
- **Niklaus Troxler** (1947-, Swiss) — Willisau Jazz Festival poster series from 1966; bridged Swiss discipline with jazz expressionism.

## Visual signatures

### Palettes
- **Psychedelic rock (SF)**: hot vibrating complementary pairs — orange (#F15A29) + turquoise (#1DC4C4), magenta (#E91E63) + acid green (#A4D236), purple (#6A1B9A) + yellow (#FDD835). Palettes chosen specifically to vibrate when adjacent (simultaneous contrast).
- **Polish School**: muted, painterly — ochres (#B8862A), burnt sienna (#A13A1E), deep greens (#2E4A2B), warm blacks, ivory paper showing through. Often one saturated spot color against muted field.
- **Cuban/OSPAAAL**: flat revolutionary — red (#C8102E), black, yellow (#FCD116), tropical green (#3B8E3F); inherited from Soviet constructivism but warmer.
- **Push Pin**: broader — Victorian earth tones, Art Deco pastels, comic-book primaries depending on story being told.
- **Japanese avant-garde**: Yokoo favors saturated pop primaries plus gold/silver; Nagai favors reductive 2-3 color palettes often including a single warm red (#E3151C).
- **British psychedelic (Hapshash)**: metallic silver or gold as a fifth color, sitting over hot pink + cobalt + emerald — more precious than SF's matte screenprints.
- **Protest / Atelier Populaire**: single color (oxblood red, black, or French blue) on raw manila or cheap newsprint (#E6D5B8).
- **Chicano**: earthy saturated — adobe red (#A73E2E), sun yellow, terracotta, blue-black; frequent use of indigenous-inspired motifs.
- **Op Art / mod**: strictly black-and-white for Riley-core; dayglo chartreuse and magenta for mod fashion spillover.

### Typography
- **Psychedelic**: custom hand-lettering that melts, expands to fill shape, becomes nearly illegible on purpose (Wes Wilson's "you have to work to read it" principle). Art Nouveau revival sources (Alfons Mucha, Viennese Secession) plus Jugendstil.
- **Polish**: often hand-painted into the illustration; calligraphic, loose, never sans-serif grids.
- **Cuban**: tight sans-serif (Helvetica, Akzidenz) for headlines; sometimes hand-drawn condensed caps.
- **Push Pin**: historically eclectic — they revived Victorian wood type, Art Deco, and Art Nouveau typefaces as deliberate pastiche.
- **Op Art / mod**: Futura, Bauhaus geometric sans, Antique Olive.

### Composition conventions
- Psychedelic: full-bleed, no white space, typography conforming to irregular shapes inside the composition; frame often defined by lettering itself.
- Polish: single painterly image dominates; small title block at top or bottom; white paper margin as breathing room.
- Cuban/OSPAAAL: strong single silhouette or portrait; one headline in sans-serif; flat color blocks; frequently a globe or geometric symbol.
- Push Pin: illustrated scene with Victorian layout sensibility; centered composition with ornamental framing common.
- Japanese: radical asymmetry; Yokoo dense collage, Nagai extreme reduction.
- Op Art: orthogonal or rectilinear grid-based repeating pattern; no figurative element.
- Protest/Atelier Populaire: silhouette centered, slogan running across top or bottom; minimal ornament.

### Scale and format
- Rock posters: 14×20" or 11×14" standard US concert poster sizes; cheap enough to staple to telephone poles.
- Polish posters: typically B1 (707×1000mm) — designed for wall display in theatres and kiosks.
- Cuban OSPAAAL posters: 53×33cm folded into *Tricontinental* magazine; designed to unfold as a gift.
- Protest: 8.5×11" mimeograph or 17×22" silkscreen depending on production line.
- Push Pin Monthly Graphic: 11×17" promotional bulletin mailed to clients.

### Production techniques
- **Screenprint (serigraphy)** dominant for rock posters — 3-6 colors, registered by eye, occasional misregistration as signature.
- **Offset lithography** for Polish film posters, Cuban solidarity posters, most commercial work.
- **Hand-separated color** — each color plate drawn/painted separately onto acetate or stencil.
- **Letterpress** still active in protest posters (wood type + linoleum block).
- **Photolithography / halftone** in Cieślewicz's photomontage.

### Subject matter conventions
- Rock posters: surreal nature imagery (eyeballs, flowers, Native American iconography — often appropriated and now contested), Art Nouveau women, skulls, paisley, peacock feathers, peyote buttons, dancing figures.
- Polish: existential and metaphorical — a man without a face, a chair with teeth, a hand cradling an eye; always for serious cinema or theatre.
- Cuban: clenched fists, rifles, profiles of Che Guevara and African liberation figures, maps of Vietnam and Palestine, doves.
- Protest: stark photographs repurposed (My Lai, Birmingham fire hoses), typewriter type on mimeograph.
- Push Pin: narrative illustration drawing from Victorian advertising, Art Deco, American folk art, pulp covers.
- Japanese: Yokoo's ukiyo-e reworkings, kabuki actors, rising sun, female nudes (provocative in 60s Japan); Fukuda's symbolic-object puns.
- Corita Kent: supermarket slogans and biblical phrases side-by-side; "Come alive" (Pepsi), "enriched bread," "somebody somewhere."

## What to apply when asked for this era's work

**For a 60s-70s psychedelic rock poster:**
1. Build custom hand-lettering that deforms to fit an irregular shape — words stretch, compress, merge.
2. Pick two hot complementary colors that will vibrate at the edges; add a third accent.
3. Fill the entire rectangle — no breathing room, no grid; background pattern (paisley, mandala, peacock eye) often required.
4. Central figure is often an Art Nouveau woman, a skull, an eyeball, or a surreal creature.
5. Include all the information (date, venue, bands) inside the shape — readability is secondary.

**For a Polish School film poster:**
1. Start with a painterly or collaged single image with clear symbolic weight.
2. Leave white paper margin around the image.
3. Hand-letter or set quiet serif/sans title at top or bottom.
4. Limit palette to 3-5 muted colors; one can be saturated as accent.
5. The image should be metaphorical, not literal — the poster is a short poem about the film.

**For Cuban / OSPAAAL solidarity:**
1. Silhouette or portrait in flat color, often with halftone texture.
2. Three-color limit (red, black, one extra) enforces discipline.
3. Sans-serif caps headline in Spanish, English, French, Arabic (the four OSPAAAL languages).
4. Subject is always political solidarity — a liberation movement.

**For 1968 protest / Atelier Populaire:**
1. Single flat color on manila or newsprint.
2. Single image (silhouette, fist, factory, soldier) + stenciled slogan.
3. Hand-cut stencil aesthetic — edges are rough, ink is uneven.
4. Text in French (if reproducing the era exactly) or local protest language.

**For a Push Pin illustrated poster (Chwast/Glaser):**
1. Illustration is dominant — flat color, Victorian or Art Deco reference.
2. Typography is selected from historical specimen sources (Caslon, Cheltenham, or hand-drawn variants).
3. Decorative border or centered composition common.
4. Limited palette (3-4 colors) but warm and saturated, not screaming.
5. A narrative intent — the poster tells a small story, not just promotes.

**For Japanese avant-garde (Yokoo):**
1. Dense collage of appropriated imagery — rising sun, ukiyo-e figures, yourself as the subject.
2. Flat saturated primaries plus at least one metallic.
3. Deliberate asymmetry; composition spills off the edge.
4. Japanese hand-lettering or stencil Kanji layered over the image.
5. Subject matter: death, fame, sex, tradition — one or all at once.

**For Corita Kent's serigraph activist pop:**
1. Appropriate a commercial slogan (Wonder Bread, Pepsi, Sun Maid).
2. Overlay hand-lettered or typed religious / peace text.
3. Thick spot color screenprint, 2-4 layers.
4. Hand-cut letterforms and torn-paper textures.
5. Rectangular composition with text dominating, no photography.

## What would be anachronistic
- Crisp Helvetica used in a rock poster (wrong idiom — Helvetica was Swiss/corporate; psychedelic was anti-Swiss).
- Computer gradients or soft digital vector blends — all color was flat screen-ink.
- Drop shadows from Photoshop.
- Pixel-perfect alignment — screenprint registration was imperfect.
- Glossy photographic renderings — photography in this era was halftone or gravure, not chrome digital.
- Bento-box or grid-based infographic layouts.
- Pastel "Scandinavian" minimalism palettes.
- Emoji or Memphis patterns (wrong decade).
- QR codes, URLs, Instagram handles — no such thing yet.
- Variable-font kinetic typography.
- AI-generated texture or painting quality.
- Razor-sharp CMYK 300dpi digital files — physical production was all hand-separated, inked on paper.
- Vision Pro-style translucency or depth panels.
- "Bauhaus-revival" geometric sans with tight kerning (that's a 2010s trope).

## Influences in / out

**Fed in from:**
- Art Nouveau (Mucha, Toulouse-Lautrec) — re-read by SF scene.
- Viennese Secession and Jugendstil.
- Constructivism (Rodchenko, Stenberg brothers) — entered Cuban and Polish work via Soviet training.
- Dada and Surrealism (Magritte especially) — core to Polish School.
- Pop Art (Warhol, Rauschenberg) — adjacent but commercial posters borrowed the iconography.
- Japanese ukiyo-e woodblock composition — revived by Yokoo.
- American comic book line work — Rick Griffin trained on surf comics.

**Fed forward to:**
- Punk DIY aesthetic (1976-1980) — inherits the silkscreen/protest ethos.
- New Wave / Swiss Punk (Weingart, Greiman) — reacted against Swiss grid.
- Alternative comix (Crumb, Shelton) — direct kin to Rick Griffin.
- Gig poster revival (Frank Kozik, Art Chantry, Coop) 1990s — explicit homage to psychedelic era.
- Mondo Tees movie poster scene (2004+) — screenprint culture inherits from Fillmore era.
- Emory Douglas's flat-color protest language persists in Shepard Fairey's Obey and "Hope" (2008).
- Modern solarpunk illustration owes the optimistic activist register to Corita Kent.
- Bauhaus reverence in Swiss modernism continued to be reset against psychedelic anti-grid; the dialectic resurfaces every 15-20 years.
- 2020s acid graphic / Y2K revival is the third cycle back to vibrating complementary colors (psychedelic → Memphis → acid graphic).
