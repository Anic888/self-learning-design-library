# 1920–1940 — The Avant-Garde Decades

*Twenty years in which every poster convention gets overthrown at least once. Constructivism, Bauhaus, De Stijl, Deco, Futurism — the grammar of modern graphic design is invented in this window.*

## Context
Post-war disillusion, Bolshevik revolution, mass literacy, cinema as a dominant new medium, and the industrialization of photography (35mm cameras, halftone maturity, offset printing) collide with radical art movements that explicitly want to abolish the old order — including the old bourgeois ornamental poster. By 1923 Rodchenko is making photomontages; by 1925 Cassandre has published *L'Intransigeant*; by 1928 Tschichold has published *Die neue Typographie*. Each movement weaponizes the poster for a different politics — Soviet modernization, Weimar pedagogy, Dutch utopianism, French commercial luxury, Italian fascism — but they share a conviction that design is no longer decoration. It is program.

## Key movements
- **Russian Constructivism** (1917–1934) — Rodchenko, El Lissitzky, Klutsis, Stenberg brothers; photomontage, red-black-white, diagonal dynamics, sans-serif Cyrillic.
- **Bauhaus graphics** (Weimar 1919 → Dessau 1925 → Berlin 1932, closed 1933) — Moholy-Nagy, Bayer, Albers, Schmidt; Neue Typographie, primary colors, grid.
- **De Stijl** (Netherlands, 1917–1931) — Mondrian, Theo van Doesburg, Piet Zwart (print applications); primaries + black + white + 90° grid.
- **New Typography / Die neue Typographie** (1925–1935) — Jan Tschichold's synthesis; asymmetric sans-first, left-ragged, photographic illustration.
- **Art Deco / Moderne** (1925–1939) — Cassandre, Carlu, Colin, Loupot; streamlined geometric figuration, luxury travel posters, ocean liners, aircraft.
- **Italian Futurism** (1909–~1944; poster-relevant 1912–1940) — Depero, Balla (marginally), Marinetti's parole in libertà; typographic explosion.
- **German Expressionism & Dada (print)** — Heartfield's photomontage; Hannah Höch; Schwitters *Merz*; Hausmann.
- **Stenberg Brothers / Soviet film poster** (Moscow, 1923–1933) — Vladimir & Georgii Stenberg; ~300 film posters; hand-drawn photomontage.
- **Polish poster origin** (1920s–30s, via Tadeusz Gronowski, later blossoms post-1945 as the "Polish School").
- **WPA and American poster modernism** (1936–1943) — begins in this period and bleeds into the next era.

## Masters of the era

### Constructivism
- **Alexander Rodchenko** (1891–1956, Russian) — Painter → photographer → poster designer; *Books* (1925, Lilya Brik); Mayakovsky collaborations.
- **El Lissitzky (Lazar Lissitzky)** (1890–1941, Russian) — *Beat the Whites with the Red Wedge* (1919); bridge Constructivism↔Bauhaus via his Berlin/Weimar years (1921–25).
- **Gustav Klutsis** (1895–1938, Latvian-Russian) — Photomontage propaganda posters; *Electrification of the Entire Country* (1920). Executed in Stalin's 1938 purge.
- **Vladimir & Georgii Stenberg** (1899–1933 / 1900–1982, Russian) — Film posters; hand-painted photomontage; vertical stretch of figures; dynamic asymmetry.
- **Valentina Kulagina** (1902–1987, Russian) — Klutsis's partner; photomontage posters esp. women workers.
- **Varvara Stepanova** (1894–1958, Russian) — Rodchenko's partner; Constructivist textiles, book design, posters.

### Bauhaus
- **László Moholy-Nagy** (1895–1946, Hungarian) — Preliminary Course + print workshop master 1923–28; Typofoto.
- **Herbert Bayer** (1900–1985, Austrian) — Bauhaus print workshop 1925–28; Universal typeface (1925); the all-lowercase movement.
- **Josef Albers** (1888–1976, German, later American) — Preliminary Course; stencil poster type; color theory (Interaction of Color later).
- **Joost Schmidt** (1893–1948, German) — *Bauhaus-Ausstellung Weimar 1923* poster.
- **Oskar Schlemmer** (1888–1943, German) — *Bauhaus Exhibition 1923* poster (head-profile logo).

### New Typography
- **Jan Tschichold** (1902–1974, German→Swiss) — *Die neue Typographie* (1928); Munich film posters 1927–33; later recants toward classicism (1946 Penguin books).
- **Kurt Schwitters** (1887–1948, German) — *Merz* magazine; Dada typographic experimentation.
- **Paul Renner** (1878–1956, German) — Designs Futura (1927), the defining geometric sans of the era.

### De Stijl
- **Piet Mondrian** (1872–1944, Dutch) — Painter whose grid vocabulary is quoted in De Stijl graphic work.
- **Theo van Doesburg** (1883–1931, Dutch) — *De Stijl* magazine design; alphabet experiments.
- **Piet Zwart** (1885–1977, Dutch) — Self-described "typotect"; NKF cable company advertisements; bold typographic collage.
- **Paul Schuitema** (1897–1973, Dutch) — Functionalist Dutch printer/photographer; Berkel scale advertisements.
- **H.N. Werkman** (1882–1945, Dutch) — *Hot Printing*; letterpress experimental composition. Executed by Nazis in 1945.

### Art Deco
- **A.M. Cassandre (Adolphe Jean-Marie Mouron)** (1901–1968, Ukrainian-French) — The defining Art Deco poster designer; Dubonnet, Normandie, Nord Express; type designer (Bifur 1929, Peignot 1937).
- **Jean Carlu** (1900–1997, French) — Luxury and political Deco; later emigrates for *America's Answer: Production* (1941).
- **Paul Colin** (1892–1985, French) — *La Revue Nègre* with Josephine Baker (1925); jazz and theater.
- **Charles Loupot** (1892–1962, French) — St. Raphaël aperitif campaign; silhouette twins.
- **Roger Broders** (1883–1953, French) — PLM railway travel posters; Côte d'Azur.
- **Edward McKnight Kauffer** (1890–1954, American working in London) — London Underground posters; *Flight* (1919); cubist-Deco hybrid.

### Italian Futurism
- **Fortunato Depero** (1892–1960, Italian) — *Campari Soda* bottle (1932); Futurist typographic posters and advertising.
- **Filippo Tommaso Marinetti** (1876–1944) — Manifesto author; parole in libertà (words in freedom) typographic experiments influencing poster design.

### Magazines & Polish
- **Tadeusz Gronowski** (1894–1990, Polish) — *Radion Sam Pierze* (1926); proto-Polish-School poster.

### Influencers at edges (early work, later main era)
- **Alvin Lustig** (1915–1955, American) — begins practice 1936; book jackets more than posters in this window.
- **Lester Beall** (1903–1969, American) — *Rural Electrification Administration* posters (1937–41); first American graphic designer to unite European modernism with American subject.

## Visual signatures

### Palettes

- **Russian Constructivist** — the dogma palette: red `#D52B1E` or `#E63946`, black `#121212`, white `#F5F5F5`. Occasionally one accent: ochre `#D4A017` or blue `#1E3A8A`. Red is political, not decorative.
- **Bauhaus / Neue Typographie** — Primary red `#E4002B`, primary yellow `#FFCC00`, primary blue `#0057B8`, black, white. Allow grey as neutral. Mondrian primaries are the ceiling.
- **De Stijl** — Same primaries as Bauhaus but with stricter restriction: only red + yellow + blue + black + white; no intermediate mixes.
- **Art Deco / Cassandre** — Muted, luxurious, metallic-adjacent: deep navy `#1B263B`, ivory `#F1E9D2`, dusty rose `#C08497`, gold `#C9A227`, deep maroon `#641220`, bottle green `#2F4F4F`. Cassandre often uses 3–4 color airbrush gradient builds.
- **Italian Futurist** — Aggressive, diagonal: red, black, white, sulfur yellow; occasionally Italian tricolor.
- **Stenberg film poster** — Black + red + one ink color (green, yellow, or blue) + newsprint cream ground.

### Typography
- **Sans-serif as ideological default** — serif now associated with the old/bourgeois/royalist order.
- **Geometric sans dominant** — Futura (Renner, 1927), Erbar (Erbar, 1926), Kabel (Koch, 1927), Bauhaus stencil geometrics.
- **Grotesque sans** — Akzidenz-Grotesk, Venus, in New Typography layouts.
- **Universal / single-alphabet experiments** — Bayer's Universal (1925); Tschichold's attempt at "an alphabet of only one alphabet" (no caps).
- **Custom Deco display** — Broadway (Benton, 1927), Bifur (Cassandre, 1929), Peignot (Cassandre, 1937), Parisian (1928). Narrow-high condensed.
- **Constructivist Cyrillic** — Bold geometric Cyrillic, often stenciled or condensed; sans dominant.
- **Futurist typography** — multiple fonts and sizes on a single page; diagonal and radial placement; breaking the baseline.
- **Left-ragged composition** (Tschichold law) — justification is a bourgeois habit; ragged-right is modern.
- **Asymmetric headline placement** — text as geometric element, not as a paragraph.

### Composition
- **Diagonal dynamic line** — the defining Constructivist move; El Lissitzky's red wedge; Rodchenko's shouting profile diagonal.
- **The grid becomes explicit** — Tschichold and Bauhaus treat the grid as a structural feature, not a hidden armature.
- **Asymmetric balance** — weight distributed by visual tension, not by central-axis symmetry.
- **Overlapping planes of flat color** — typical Bauhaus poster: three overlapping rectangles in red/yellow/blue.
- **Photomontage as primary image** — especially in Soviet and Dada work; NOT drawn, NOT painted — cut-and-pasted photograph with hand-lettered type.
- **Cassandre's airbrush streamline** — volumetric, simplified machine forms (ships, trains, bottles) rendered as geometric archetypes.
- **Full-bleed photographic grounds** in Soviet posters (Klutsis especially).
- **Stenberg vertical stretch** — figures elongated, often split into halves with different colors.

### Printing
- **Offset lithography matures** — by 1930s the dominant process for quality posters.
- **Halftone photomontage** fully integrated — screens as fine as 133 lpi available.
- **Spot color + airbrush** defines Cassandre's surfaces.
- **Screen printing (serigraphy)** emerges in the 1930s, especially for WPA posters.
- **Soviet posters** often cheap 2–3 color offset on newsprint; deliberate or unavoidable.
- **Photogravure** for luxury posters and magazine covers (especially Parisian).

### Subject matter
- **Soviet**: industrialization (tractors, electrification, dams), workers, red army, literacy drives, women in production, Lenin, cinema.
- **Bauhaus**: Bauhaus exhibitions, lectures, school announcements, industrial products (Kandem lamps).
- **De Stijl / Dutch**: industrial products, architecture exhibitions.
- **Art Deco**: luxury travel (Normandie, Orient Express, Côte d'Azur), aperitifs (Dubonnet, Campari, St. Raphaël), petrol brands, automobiles, department stores, film.
- **Futurist**: Italian commerce (Campari), fascism once Marinetti aligns with Mussolini, motor racing.
- **Soviet film poster**: cinema release announcements — Battleship Potemkin, Man with a Movie Camera, Strike, October.

## What to apply when asked for this era's work

### 1920s Constructivist poster (Rodchenko/Lissitzky/Klutsis)
Red + black + white only. Square or slightly vertical format. One diagonal organizing line (30–60°). If photographic, use high-contrast black/white halftone photomontage of a worker, soldier, or industrial machine; figure may be shouting, pointing, or in profile. Bold geometric shapes (circles, wedges, triangles) as typographic framing devices. Cyrillic or Latin sans caps, left-aligned or set at 90°/45°. No decorative borders, no ornament, no curved figuration. Often a slogan runs along the diagonal of a red bar.

### Bauhaus-style poster (1923–1928)
Three primaries + black + white. Asymmetric grid. Overlapping rectangles of flat color. Geometric sans, preferably Futura or Bauhaus stencil, often mixed lowercase (Bayer's Universal influence). Typical headline bottom-left or middle-left, ragged right. Small technical details (lecture date, price, venue) in top-right or running along an edge. Minimal illustration; when illustration exists, it's photographic or geometric diagram.

### De Stijl-style poster
Only the five elements: red, yellow, blue, black, white. Only 90° angles. Grid of rectangles with black lines dividing fields. Sans-serif caps; often the type sits inside one of the rectangles. No figuration — if required, abstract to pure geometric equivalents.

### Art Deco poster (Cassandre 1925–1935)
Muted 4-color palette with gold or metallic accent. Vertical format. Radically simplified geometric object (ship prow, train wheel, bottle silhouette) in dramatic perspective — worm's-eye or airbrushed streamline. Custom Deco display type (Bifur, Broadway, Peignot) as headline, placed as a geometric element with the object. Airbrush gradient shading. Luxury subject — travel, alcohol, fashion. Stylized but never abstract — the object is always recognizable.

### Stenberg film poster (1923–1933)
Vertical format, often quite narrow. Figure or face hand-painted in a photographic style, elongated vertically (1.3–1.6× normal). Bold primary color (red, orange, cyan) as ground. Bold condensed Cyrillic sans title across top or bottom. Small credit text in thin sans. Often 2–3 colors only. A signature move: splitting the face or figure across a color boundary so each half reads different.

### Italian Futurist poster
Multiple typefaces, sizes, and angles on one sheet. Diagonal and radial type arrangement. Sharp angles, speed lines, mechanical fragmentation. Red + black + white primary, with occasional sulfur yellow. Text breaks the baseline; words rotated 45°–90°.

## What would be anachronistic
- **Full-color photography on posters** — color photography exists (Autochrome 1907, Kodachrome 1935) but color-photographic posters are extremely rare pre-1940. Most color in this era is spot-color ink or hand-tinted.
- **Chromed/metallic spray effects of later graphic design** — airbrush is used but the surface is painterly-smooth, not mirror-metallic.
- **Post-war Swiss precision grids (Müller-Brockmann 1950s)** — yes, New Typography is a precursor, but the Swiss grid system's full mathematical rigor is a post-1945 construction. Tschichold's grids are looser.
- **Helvetica** — invented 1957. Use Akzidenz-Grotesk, Futura, Erbar, Kabel, or Universal for authenticity.
- **Psychedelic or curvilinear ornament** — all of that is banned by the movements of this era.
- **Mixed case typographic expressive fonts** — Bauhaus lowercase is a specific ideological choice; don't mix it with ornamental caps.
- **Hand-lettered Art Nouveau whiplash** — explicitly what the 1920s movements killed.
- **CMYK process color builds visible as 4-color rosette** — offset CMYK exists but rosettes are rare until post-war.
- **Sans-serif for a luxury Deco poster** — Cassandre uses custom Deco display with character; pure geometric Futura reads Bauhaus, not Deco.
- **Hitler-era Nazi propaganda aesthetics in pre-1933 Weimar work** — don't mix Mjölnir/Hohlwein wartime Nazi imagery with Bauhaus; they are politically and temporally distinct.

- **Joost Schmidt, *Bauhaus Exhibition Weimar*** (1923) — the head-profile geometric composition; the first Bauhaus exhibition poster.
- **Herbert Bayer, *Kandinsky 60th Birthday Exhibition*** (1926) — asymmetric primary-color composition.
- **László Moholy-Nagy, *Pneumatik / Pneumatik Reifen*** (1923) — the airbrushed diagonal tire advertisement.

- **Jan Tschichold, *Die Frau ohne Namen*** (1927) — Munich film poster; asymmetric photographic poster textbook.
- **Theo van Doesburg & El Lissitzky, *De Stijl* covers** (1922) — magazine design as poster language.

- **Paul Colin, *La Revue Nègre*** (1925) — Josephine Baker poster; Deco meets jazz.
- **Jean Carlu, *Disarm*** (1932) — Deco anti-war poster; dove and skull.
- **Fortunato Depero, *Campari Soda*** (1932) — futurist bottle packaging.
- **Edward McKnight Kauffer, *Flight*** (1919, London Underground) — Vorticist-Deco flight of birds.

## Influences in / out
**In:** Cubism (Picasso, Braque 1908–) gives the geometric fragmentation vocabulary. Suprematism (Malevich, *Black Square* 1915) gives the pure-geometric reduction that Constructivism politicizes. Photography (35mm Leica 1925, rapid halftone) enables photomontage. WW1 propaganda methodology feeds Soviet agitprop via Mayakovsky/ROSTA windows (1919–21). The Werkbund (1907) and Deutscher Werkbund exhibition culture (Cologne 1914, Stuttgart 1927) create the institutional conditions for Bauhaus.

**Out:** Virtually all modernist graphic design to date descends from this era. The Swiss International Style (1940s–1960s) is a direct formalization of Tschichold's New Typography + Bauhaus grid. American corporate modernism (Paul Rand, Saul Bass, Charles Eames) takes Constructivist and Bauhaus reductionism and re-clothes it for capitalism. The Polish Poster School (1945–) adapts Constructivist dynamism with Surrealist imagery. Art Deco's streamlined Cassandre vocabulary feeds mid-century travel and airline design (Pan Am, TWA). Stenberg's film-poster expressiveness seeds the mid-century Polish and Czech poster schools. Even Josef Müller-Brockmann's Zurich tonhalle concert posters (1951–) are unthinkable without El Lissitzky and Tschichold.
