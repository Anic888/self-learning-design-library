# 1940–1960 — Swiss Modernism & the Postwar Poster

*Switzerland codifies the grid. America industrializes the book jacket, the film title, and the corporate logo. The poster becomes a system.*

## Context
Two forces converge after 1945. In Switzerland — neutral, industrialized, with deep typographic trade schools in Basel (Allgemeine Gewerbeschule) and Zurich (Kunstgewerbeschule) — the scattered lessons of Bauhaus, Neue Typographie, and Constructivism get systematized into what becomes the **International Typographic Style**: mathematical grids, sans-serif typography, objective photography, no ornament. In America, refugee modernists (Moholy-Nagy in Chicago, Agha and Brodovitch for magazines, Bayer) and native-born talents (Paul Rand, Saul Bass, Lester Beall) turn modernism into the language of corporate identity, publishing, and Hollywood. By 1960 a single visual grammar — sans-serif, gridded, photographic, objective — dominates global professional design for the first time in history.

## Key movements
- **Swiss International Typographic Style** (1940s–1960s) — Basel and Zurich schools; grid systems, Akzidenz/Univers/Helvetica, objective photography.
- **Basel School** — Armin Hofmann, Emil Ruder; more expressive and hand-lettered than Zurich.
- **Zurich School** — Josef Müller-Brockmann, Carlo Vivarelli, Richard Lohse; strict mathematical grid, concert-hall and social-awareness posters.
- **Swiss Concrete Art** (Zurich) — Max Bill, Richard Lohse; mathematical abstraction feeding into poster geometry.
- **WPA Federal Art Project posters** (US, 1935–1943) — Lester Beall, Anthony Velonis; screenprint public-program posters.
- **American corporate modernism** (1945–1965) — Paul Rand (IBM, Westinghouse, UPS, ABC); Lester Beall (International Paper); Saul Bass (AT&T, Bell, Continental).
- **Push Pin Studios** (New York, founded 1954) — Milton Glaser, Seymour Chwast, Edward Sorel, Reynold Ruffins; eclectic illustrative counter-current to Swiss purity.
- **Magazine modernism** (US, 1940s–50s) — Alexey Brodovitch (*Harper's Bazaar*), Cipe Pineles (*Seventeen*, *Charm*, *Glamour*, *Mademoiselle*), Leo Lionni, Bradbury Thompson (*Westvaco Inspirations*).
- **Saul Bass film titles & posters** (1954–) — *Carmen Jones*, *The Man with the Golden Arm*, *Anatomy of a Murder*, *Vertigo*.
- **Polish Poster School** (Warsaw, 1945–1960s, later peak in 60s) — Tomaszewski, Trepkowski, Lenica (later); surrealist-painterly counter to Swiss.

## Masters of the era

### Swiss / International Style
- **Max Bill** (1908–1994, Swiss) — Bauhaus alumnus; founded Ulm School of Design (HfG Ulm) 1953; Concrete Art painter; posters for Zurich Tonhalle and exhibitions.
- **Josef Müller-Brockmann** (1914–1996, Swiss) — Zurich School anchor; *musica viva* Tonhalle concert posters 1951–72; author *Grid Systems in Graphic Design* (1961) and *The Graphic Artist and his Design Problems* (1961).
- **Armin Hofmann** (1920–2020, Swiss) — Basel School; *Giselle* (1959); author *Graphic Design Manual* (1965); teacher of two generations.
- **Emil Ruder** (1914–1970, Swiss) — Basel typography master; author *Typographie* (1967); proponent of Univers.
- **Carlo Vivarelli** (1919–1986, Swiss) — *Für das Alter* (1949); proto-Swiss-style social poster.
- **Richard Paul Lohse** (1902–1988, Swiss) — Concrete Art painter; systematic color compositions influencing poster grids.
- **Max Huber** (1919–1992, Swiss, working in Italy) — Monza racing posters; Swiss precision meets Italian color.
- **Siegfried Odermatt** (1926–2017, Swiss) — *Univac* and corporate Swiss work.
- **Adrian Frutiger** (1928–2015, Swiss) — Designer of Univers (1957) and later Frutiger, OCR-B.
- **Max Miedinger** (1910–1980, Swiss) — With Eduard Hoffmann at Haas Type Foundry; designer of Neue Haas Grotesk (1957), renamed Helvetica 1960.

### American Corporate Modernism
- **Paul Rand (Peretz Rosenbaum)** (1914–1996, American) — IBM logo (1956), Westinghouse (1960), UPS (1961), ABC (1962); *Thoughts on Design* (1947).
- **Saul Bass** (1920–1996, American) — Film titles and posters for Preminger, Hitchcock, Kubrick; AT&T bell logo (1969, later).
- **Lester Beall** (1903–1969, American) — Rural Electrification posters (1937–41 carry-over); International Paper mark (1960).
- **Alvin Lustig** (1915–1955, American) — New Directions paperback covers; abstract-symbolic book design before dying at 40.
- **Bradbury Thompson** (1911–1995, American) — *Westvaco Inspirations* 1938–62 (61 issues); typographic experimentation.
- **Herb Lubalin** (1918–1981, American) — Advertising and editorial; later founded U&lc magazine (1973); active but pre-peak in this window.

### Push Pin (founded 1954)
- **Milton Glaser** (1929–2020, American) — Co-founder; illustrator; *I ♥ NY* (1977, later); influential 1950s Push Pin illustrative style.
- **Seymour Chwast** (1931–, American) — Co-founder; woodcut-influenced illustration and poster work.
- **Edward Sorel** (1929–, American) — Co-founder; satirical illustration.
- **Reynold Ruffins** (1930–2021, American) — Co-founder.

### Magazine Modernism
- **Alexey Brodovitch** (1898–1971, Russian-American) — *Harper's Bazaar* art director 1934–58; white space, ballet photography, Avedon.
- **Cipe Pineles** (1908–1991, Austrian-American) — First female art director in American mainstream magazines; *Seventeen* 1947–50, *Charm*, *Mademoiselle*; first woman elected to New York Art Directors Club (1948).
- **Leo Lionni** (1910–1999, Italian-American) — *Fortune* art director 1949–60; later children's book author-illustrator.
- **Henry Wolf** (1925–2005, Austrian-American) — *Esquire* (1952–58), *Harper's Bazaar* (1958–61); surreal conceptual magazine covers.

### Polish
- **Henryk Tomaszewski** (1914–2005, Polish) — Warsaw Academy poster program; founder of the Polish Poster School; *Henry Moore* (1959).
- **Tadeusz Trepkowski** (1914–1954, Polish) — *NIE!* (1952); wartime memory poster; poetic reduction.
- **Jan Lenica** (1928–2001, Polish) — Early work in this era, matures 1960s.

## Visual signatures

### Palettes

- **Swiss strict** — Black + one color + white. Typical one-color: signal red `#E30613`, cobalt blue `#004B87`, cadmium yellow `#FFD100`. Grounds always clean white (`#FFFFFF`) or off-white (`#F5F5F2`). No halftone gradation in color fields.
- **Müller-Brockmann Tonhalle** — Red or black on white; occasional duotone photograph. Severely restricted.
- **Armin Hofmann (Basel)** — Mostly black and white; very rare second color. Charcoal photographic greys + solid black.
- **WPA screen prints** — Limited 3–5 color palette: deep red, mustard, blue, olive, cream. Hand-cut stencils produce slightly irregular edges.
- **Paul Rand** — Primary-color friendly: red, yellow, blue, black, white; plus the occasional earth green or tan for tactile balance.
- **Saul Bass** — 2–3 colors per poster, usually black + one bold color + one accent (orange + black, red + black, pink + black).
- **Push Pin** — Wider illustrative palette; muted earths with saturated accents; moving against Swiss purity by 1958.
- **Polish poster** — Desaturated painterly palette: browns, slates, mustards, with one saturated accent.

### Typography
- **Sans-serif as non-negotiable default** for Swiss work.
- **Akzidenz-Grotesk** (Berthold, 1898) is the Swiss workhorse through the early 1950s.
- **Univers** (Adrian Frutiger, 1957) — released with 21 numbered weights; adopted by Ruder and the Basel school.
- **Neue Haas Grotesk / Helvetica** (Miedinger + Hoffmann 1957, renamed Helvetica 1960) — becomes the default by the 1960s.
- **Futura** still used by Rand, Beall, Bass in America for Bauhaus-inflected geometry.
- **Flush-left, ragged-right** (Tschichold's rule, now formalized).
- **Strict alignment to grid** — every line starts on a grid column.
- **Weight contrast for hierarchy**, not size contrast — one size, two weights, is a common Swiss move.
- **Typesetting as primary content** — sometimes the poster is only typography (no image).
- **Push Pin exception** — revives 19th-century wood type, Art Nouveau lettering, custom illustrative type; explicit Swiss rejection.
- **Polish exception** — hand-painted Cyrillic/Polish letterforms integrated with painted image.
- **American magazine modernism** — Didot revivals (Bodoni Book especially for *Harper's Bazaar* under Brodovitch); serif stays respectable in editorial.

### Composition
- **Mathematical modular grid** — 6, 8, 12 columns; strict baseline; every element placed on intersection.
- **Golden section and modular ratios** drawn from Le Corbusier's Modulor, Bill's concrete art, Müller-Brockmann's grid studies.
- **Objective photography** — straightforward single-subject product photography, often silhouetted on white or high-key.
- **Duotone photograph** — common Swiss move: single hero photo treated as duotone with the accent color.
- **Asymmetric balance, mathematically justified** — not Tschichold's looser asymmetry, but Lohse-Bill mathematical systems.
- **Absence of ornament** — ornament is ideologically taboo in Swiss work (Adolf Loos *Ornament and Crime* 1908 finally institutionalized).
- **Concert and exhibition posters** often pure typographic: all date/title/performer information composed as the image.
- **Paul Rand's American move** — more playful asymmetry, visual puns (IBM stripe, UPS bow, Westinghouse circle).
- **Saul Bass's cut-paper silhouette** — single symbolic cut-paper icon (arm in *Golden Arm*, fragmented corpse in *Anatomy of a Murder*).
- **Push Pin's eclecticism** — illustrative centerpiece, historical typographic framing, decorative borders.
- **Polish poster's poetic metaphor** — one central painterly image, often surreal; title as hand-lettering.

### Printing
- **Offset lithography is the default.** Full CMYK process color is now standard.
- **Silkscreen / serigraphy** for limited-edition posters, especially WPA, exhibition posters, Saul Bass.
- **Photolithography** enables objective photographic posters.
- **Halftone resolutions** now 150–175 lpi; fine enough to disappear at reading distance.
- **International paper sizes** — ISO 216 (DIN) becomes standard in Switzerland (1922 formalized; widespread adoption post-WW2); A0 and B0 formats for Swiss posters.
- **World Format Poster (128×90.5cm)** — Swiss standard city-wall poster size throughout the era.

### Subject matter
- **Swiss**: Tonhalle and orchestra concerts, museum exhibitions (Kunstgewerbemuseum Zurich, Gewerbemuseum Basel), social campaigns (for-the-aged, road safety), industrial products.
- **American corporate**: annual reports, brand identity launches, trade show booths, corporate campaigns for IBM, Westinghouse, Olivetti (via Pintori in Italy).
- **Saul Bass**: Hollywood film one-sheets for Preminger and Hitchcock.
- **WPA**: public health, travel (see America first), national parks, theater (Federal Theatre Project), community services.
- **Magazine editorial**: fashion photography, lifestyle, emerging youth market (Pineles's *Seventeen*), food and home.
- **Polish**: theater, cinema, circus, jazz — heavily cultural, since commercial advertising under state socialism is limited.

## What to apply when asked for this era's work

### 1950s Swiss International Style poster (Zurich, Müller-Brockmann lineage)
Vertical B0 or A0 proportions. Pure white ground. One saturated flat color (red, blue, or yellow) + black. Grid-based layout with explicit mathematical structure — columns divide the sheet; every element aligned to grid intersections. Akzidenz-Grotesk or (if after 1957) Helvetica/Univers, medium or bold weight, flush left, ragged right. One typographic headline, often set at a single size with hierarchy via weight contrast. If image, either (a) a duotone objective photograph of a single subject silhouetted on white, or (b) pure geometric form (circle, line, arc) as concert-related metaphor. No ornament, no illustration, no decorative edges, no gradients.

### 1960s Basel School poster (Hofmann lineage)
Black and white only. One large central image — a high-contrast photograph, a cut-paper silhouette, or hand-drawn mark. Helvetica or Univers, typically medium weight. Grid is present but expressive — Hofmann allows gesture, chunkier layout, larger negative space. Subject centered or slightly off-center.

### Paul Rand corporate poster (1947–1960 American)
Playful primary-color palette (red, yellow, blue, black, white). One central visual pun or symbol — stripes, spots, pattern. Sans-serif (Futura or later Helvetica) headline; lowercase often. Asymmetric but balanced. Often a white ground with one saturated color rectangle as visual anchor. Wit and warmth over strict formalism.

### Saul Bass film poster (1954–1960)
Black + one bold color (orange, red, or pink) + white. Cut-paper ragged-edge symbol as central image — fragmented limb, jagged arm, broken form. Hand-drawn or Franklin Gothic title lettering, large, stacked, rough. Title bar usually top or bottom third. Credits in small clean sans below.

### WPA-style screenprint poster (1936–1943)
Muted earth-plus-accent palette: deep red, mustard, cream, blue. 3–5 colors maximum, each screen with a slightly irregular edge from hand-cut stencil. Stylized flat illustration of landscape, public institution, or figure. Title in bold condensed sans caps at top. Subtitle (location or program) below image. Slight oft-stylization of forms — not photorealistic, somewhere between Deco and naive folk.

### Push Pin Studios poster (1954–1960)
Illustrative centerpiece combining Art Nouveau whiplash or 19th-century Victorian wood-type references. Wider color palette than Swiss — muted earths with unexpected bright accents. Custom-drawn illustrative title lettering. Decorative border allowed. Think "Swiss with personality restored."

### Magazine cover (Brodovitch, Pineles, Wolf)
Full-bleed photographic image (often fashion or conceptual); Didone serif (Bodoni) masthead; white space generous; one-line headline at top or bottom; clean sans subheads.

### Polish poster (late 1940s–early 1960s, Tomaszewski)
Painterly central image, often with surreal juxtaposition. Desaturated base palette with one warm accent. Hand-lettered Polish title integrated with the image; poetic rather than commercial voice. No grid — Tomaszewski explicitly rejects Swiss rigidity.

## What would be anachronistic
- **Helvetica before 1957** — Neue Haas Grotesk released 1957, renamed Helvetica in 1960. Any poster dated 1949 with Helvetica is wrong; use Akzidenz-Grotesk instead.
- **Univers before 1957** — Frutiger designed it for Deberny & Peignot 1954–57.
- **Chromalin / photographic prepress effects** from the 1970s.
- **Vector logos with perfect mathematical curves** — all logos in this era were hand-drawn or drafted; slight irregularities are authentic.
- **The word "Helvetica" as a design identity** — the typeface's cultural meaning as "the Swiss font" is a 1970s–80s retrospective construction; in 1957 it was just a new grotesque.
- **Postmodern color schemes (Memphis, 1980s)** — hot pastels and pattern riot belong to the 1980s, not 1950s.
- **Psychedelic poster vocabulary (Wes Wilson 1966, Victor Moscoso)** — 60s counterculture poster is the NEXT era; do not mix.
- **Chinese or Japanese contemporary commercial poster vocabularies** — mid-century Japanese modern poster (Yokoo, Nagai) matures post-1960.
- **CGI gradients, digital effects, photo-manipulation** — obviously anachronistic.
- **Scripts and decorative display type in Swiss work** — ornament was a genuine taboo.
- **Ultra-condensed super-black modern display type** — the Swiss used medium and bold weights; ultra-black display is a 1970s thing.
- **Black-background posters in Swiss work** — overwhelmingly white-ground. Black grounds are rare and should be used sparingly.
- **Overtly political/revolutionary imagery in Swiss commercial work** — Swiss style is associated with neutrality and objectivity; revolutionary iconography belongs to the Constructivist era or Cuban/Polish postwar posters.

## Notable specific posters
- **Carlo Vivarelli, *Für das Alter (For the Aged)*** (1949) — widely cited as the first fully mature Swiss-style poster; black and white, single photographic figure, minimal type.
- **Max Bill, *Die gute Form*** (1949) — touring exhibition poster; pure geometric Swiss-Concrete.
- **Josef Müller-Brockmann, *Beethoven (musica viva)*** (1955) — concentric arcs as musical energy; the Swiss concert poster prototype.
- **Josef Müller-Brockmann, *der Film*** (1960) — grid typographic poster; textbook case for *Grid Systems*.
- **Josef Müller-Brockmann, *Weniger Lärm (Less Noise)*** (1960) — single photographic child + type; Swiss social poster at peak.
- **Armin Hofmann, *Giselle*** (1959) — pure black and white ballet poster; dancer as abstract mark.
- **Armin Hofmann, *Herman Miller*** (1962) — typographic Helvetica composition.
- **Emil Ruder, *die Zeitung (The Newspaper)*** (1958) — pure typographic poster; Univers at its debut.
- **Max Huber, *Monza Gran Premio dell'Autodromo*** (1948) — overlapping transparent arrows; pre-fully-mature Swiss, more colorful.
- **Lester Beall, *Rural Electrification Administration* series** (1937, bleeding into this era) — American modernism's first full application.
- **Paul Rand, *IBM logo and applications*** (1956 on) — striped IBM, consistent across posters and reports.
- **Paul Rand, *Westinghouse identity*** (1960) — the "W" mark.
- **Paul Rand, *No Way Out* poster** (1950) — early cut-paper illustrative poster.
- **Saul Bass, *The Man with the Golden Arm*** (1955) — jagged cut-paper arm; one of the most-imitated film posters ever.
- **Saul Bass, *Vertigo*** (1958) — spiral figure composition, Hitchcock-Preminger pivot.
- **Saul Bass, *Anatomy of a Murder*** (1959) — cut-paper fragmented corpse silhouette.
- **Saul Bass, *Exodus*** (1960) — raised arms and flames.
- **Alvin Lustig, New Directions paperback series** (1945–55) — abstract-symbolic book covers, proto-conceptual design.
- **Cipe Pineles, *Seventeen* covers** (1947–50) — commissioned fine artists (Ben Shahn, Richard Lindner) for mass-market teen magazine; the modernist magazine cover peak for young women.
- **Alexey Brodovitch, *Harper's Bazaar* spreads** (1938–58) — ballet photography with Avedon; white space as compositional element.
- **Leo Lionni, *Fortune* covers** (1949–60) — conceptual collage; business magazine as design platform.
- **Henry Wolf, *Esquire*** (1958) — surreal photographic covers (cigarette lighter as skyscraper etc.).
- **Bradbury Thompson, *Westvaco Inspirations 210*** (1958) — typographic overprint experiment.
- **Tadeusz Trepkowski, *NIE! (No!)*** (1952) — falling bomb-shape containing ruined city; Polish Poster School anti-war poster.
- **Henryk Tomaszewski, *Henry Moore exhibition*** (1959, Warsaw) — painterly abstraction; Polish counter to Swiss.
- **Anthony Velonis / WPA Federal Art Project, *See America*** series (c. 1936–39) — national parks screenprints; American mid-century modernism's folk-adjacent face.

## Influences in / out
**In:** Bauhaus (Bayer, Moholy-Nagy, Albers) — Max Bill was a Bauhaus student and carries the DNA directly into Swiss formation at HfG Ulm. New Typography (Tschichold) — Tschichold himself lives in Basel from 1933, teaches there 1946–49, and though he publicly recants hard modernism for Penguin classicism, his New Typography is the explicit foundation of the Swiss grid. Constructivism (El Lissitzky) — Müller-Brockmann explicitly cites Lissitzky. De Stijl. Concrete Art (Bill, Lohse, Hans Arp). American pragmatism and corporate scale provide the demand for Rand's version. Photographic journalism (*Life*, *Look*, *Harper's Bazaar*) trains the eye to accept objective photography in design.

**Out:** Swiss style becomes the dominant global corporate design language from roughly 1960 onward. Helvetica and the grid become synonymous with corporate legitimacy — see every airline, bank, and government identity from 1960–1985. American corporate identity (Chermayeff & Geismar, Vignelli) directly extends Rand and the Swiss imports. Minimalism in 1960s-70s art (Sol LeWitt, Donald Judd) shares formal vocabulary with Concrete Art. Reaction against Swiss purity drives multiple countermovements: Push Pin (already operating by 1954); psychedelic poster (1965–); punk/DIY (1976–); postmodern graphic design (Cranbrook, Émigré, David Carson — late 1980s onward). The Polish Poster School peaks in the 1960s with Lenica, Cieślewicz, Starowieyski. Saul Bass's cut-paper film poster vocabulary is the DNA of every subsequent film-title sequence including Kyle Cooper's *Se7en*. Paul Rand's corporate mark language becomes the standard of American branding into the 2000s.
