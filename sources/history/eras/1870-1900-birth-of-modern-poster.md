# 1870–1900 — Birth of the Modern Poster

*Chromolithography turns the wall into a printed object. Paris invents the commercial color poster; Art Nouveau ornaments it.*

## Context
The modern color poster becomes possible only when Jules Chéret brings Alois Senefelder's lithography into a three-stone color process around 1866–1870, industrialized by the Chaix press in Paris. Cheap, mass color printing collides with Haussmann's newly widened boulevards and a rising urban consumer class — cabarets, music halls, department stores, bicycles, absinthe — creating both the surface (city walls) and the demand (advertising). By 1900 Paris alone is plastering some 4 million posters a year and the "poster craze" (*l'affichomanie*) has spawned the first collectors, dealers, and critical literature.

## Key movements
- **Parisian cabaret/music-hall poster** — Chéret's dancing girls; the poster as printed theater.
- **Art Nouveau / Style Mucha** — Mucha, Grasset, Privat-Livemont; whiplash curves, ornamental frames, idealized women.
- **Les Nabis / Post-Impressionist poster** — Toulouse-Lautrec, Bonnard, Vallotton; Japonisme, flat color, hard outline.
- **American Arts & Crafts poster** — Will Bradley, Edward Penfield (Harper's), Ethel Reed; Beardsley via *The Chap-Book*.
- **Secessionist poster (early)** — Vienna (Klimt, Moser from 1898), Munich *Jugend* magazine gives its name to Jugendstil.
- **English Beggarstaffs** — James Pryde & William Nicholson; cut-paper flat silhouettes, a proto-modern counter-current.
- **Italian Liberty / Stile Floreale** — Hohenstein, Metlicovitz at the Ricordi press in Milan; operatic Art Nouveau.
- **Belgian Art Nouveau** — Privat-Livemont, Henri Meunier; heavy gold ornament, Brussels decorative peak.
- **British commercial poster** — Dudley Hardy, John Hassall, "Beggarstaffs"; theatrical and resort/seaside advertising.
- **Symbolist poster** — De Feure, Orazi, early Mucha; literary and esoteric subjects, dreamlike femmes fatales.

## Masters of the era
- **Jules Chéret** (1836–1932, French) — "Father of the modern poster"; perfected 3-stone color lithography; over 1,000 posters.
- **Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec** (1864–1901, French) — 31 posters; radical cropping, flat silhouettes, spatter (*crachis*), Montmartre subjects.
- **Alphonse Mucha** (1860–1939, Czech, working in Paris) — "Style Mucha"; Sarah Bernhardt series from *Gismonda* (1894); decorative panels.
- **Eugène Grasset** (1845–1917, Swiss-French) — Medievalist stained-glass treatment; pre-Mucha Art Nouveau figure work; *Salon des Cent*.
- **Théophile Steinlen** (1859–1923, Swiss-French) — *Chat Noir* (1896); cats and proletarian scenes; black-outline lithography.
- **Pierre Bonnard** (1867–1947, French) — *France-Champagne* (1891); the poster that converted Lautrec; Nabis Japonisme.
- **Privat-Livemont** (1861–1936, Belgian) — *Absinthe Robette* (1896); heavy gold ornament, Belgian Art Nouveau.
- **Will Bradley** (1868–1962, American) — "American Beardsley"; *The Chap-Book* posters; black-and-red wood-grain aesthetics.
- **Edward Penfield** (1866–1925, American) — Harper's Magazine monthlies 1893–99; flat areas, calm illustration.
- **Aubrey Beardsley** (1872–1898, British) — Posters and book work for *The Yellow Book*, *Salome*; black/white whiplash line.
- **The Beggarstaffs** (James Pryde 1866–1941 & William Nicholson 1872–1949, British) — Cut-paper flat silhouette posters (*Don Quixote*, *Rowntree's Elect Cocoa*).
- **Ethel Reed** (1874–1912, American) — Rare female star of the American poster boom; Boston book posters.
- **Henri-Gabriel Ibels** (1867–1936, French) — Nabis poster designer; circus and theatre subjects; worked alongside Lautrec.
- **Félix Vallotton** (1865–1925, Swiss-French) — Nabis woodcut-flavored posters and prints; black-area silhouette master.
- **Manuel Orazi** (1860–1934, Italian working in Paris) — *Théodora*, Sarah Bernhardt posters; Byzantine Art Nouveau.
- **Georges de Feure** (1868–1943, French) — Symbolist-decorative; stylized female figures for Maison Bing.
- **Leonetto Cappiello** (1875–1942, Italian, Paris) — first poster 1899; mostly 20th-century career but begins here.
- **Maxfield Parrish** (1870–1966, American) — Late-era magazine covers and book posters; saturated painterly color (peak after 1900).
- **Koloman Moser** (1868–1918, Austrian) — Early Secessionist posters from 1898; bridge into 20th-century Vienna.
- **Gustav Klimt** (1862–1918, Austrian) — Secession Exhibition posters from 1898 (*Theseus and Minotaur*, *1st Secession Exhibition*).
- **Dudley Hardy** (1867–1922, British) — *The Yellow Girl* (1895) and *A Gaiety Girl* (1893); British answer to Chéret.
- **John Hassall** (1868–1948, British) — Commercial British poster; *Skegness Is So Bracing* (1908 but style set in 1890s).
- **Adolfo Hohenstein** (1854–1928, Italian) — "Father of Italian poster art"; Ricordi press; opera posters (*Tosca*, *Iris*).
- **Leopoldo Metlicovitz** (1868–1944, Italian) — Ricordi press; opera and industrial fair posters.

## Visual signatures

### Palettes
- **Chéret standard** — limited 3-stone color: a red/vermilion, a yellow/ochre, a blue/green; over cream paper. Typical hexes: `#E63946` (vermilion), `#F4C430` (saffron), `#2E6F95` (teal-blue), `#F5EFE0` (cream ground), `#1A1A1A` (key black).
- **Art Nouveau / Mucha** — ivory, gold, muted jewel tones: `#F2E8D5` (ivory), `#B8860B` (dark gold), `#556B2F` (olive), `#8B3A3A` (oxblood), `#4B6B5C` (sage).
- **Lautrec** — flat black silhouette + 1–2 bright accents (cadmium yellow `#FFC300`, hot red `#D62828`), muted ground.
- **Absinthe-era Belgian** — acid greens `#7FB069` against warm gold and ivory.
- **American Penfield/Bradley** — earth palette: oxblood, forest, cream, black; often 2-color posters.
- **Vienna Secession** — gold leaf `#C9A227`, deep black `#0E0E0E`, ivory `#F1EAD4`, occasional Pompeian red `#A4343A` or muted teal `#3E6F7F`.
- **Italian opera (Hohenstein, Metlicovitz)** — warmer, more tonal than French: deep burgundy, amber, smoky teal, ivory; closer to Symbolist painting.
- **British music-hall (Dudley Hardy)** — primary-leaning: royal blue, poppy red, primrose yellow, on cream.
- **Beardsley** — black and cream only; occasional single warm accent.

### Typography
- **Hand-drawn display lettering dominates** — letterforms are drawn with the image, not typeset. No photography yet in posters.
- **Art Nouveau display** — elastic, organic, letters that curve with the ornament (the originals later cast as Auriol, Eckmann, Arnold Böcklin).
- **Serif bodies when typeset** — Didones (Bodoni/Didot), Clarendons, and fat-face Victorian woodtype for emphasis.
- **English/American** — Golden Type and Caslon revival from William Morris's Kelmscott (1891) and Arts & Crafts influence.
- **Caps-and-initial** medieval treatment in Grasset and Bradley.
- **Key period typefaces** to quote when going for this era: Auriol (George Auriol, 1901), Eckmann (Otto Eckmann, 1900), Arnold Böcklin (Schriftgiesserei Otto Weisert, 1904), Grasset's own type (1898), Morris's Golden Type (1890), Troy (1892), Chaucer (1892). For roman bodies: Caslon Old Face, ATF Cheltenham (Goodhue, 1896), Bodoni revivals.
- **Ornamental border and drop-cap books** (Kelmscott, Roycroft) bleed into poster composition.
- **Letters as ornament** — Mucha's letterforms are often built from the same whiplash curves as the decorative frame; letter and ornament share a vocabulary. This is different from later modernism where type is neutral architecture.

### Composition
- **Central figure on flat ground** — the Chéret formula: one dancing woman, vertical, filling ~60% of sheet, text above and below.
- **Vertical portrait format dominant** — typical sizes 1 sheet (~30×40in / 76×101cm) up to Mucha's tall decorative panels (~60×20in).
- **Japonisme asymmetry** — Lautrec and Bonnard: hard diagonal cropping, foreground silhouettes cut off by edge (learned from ukiyo-e).
- **Ornamental frame/halo** — Mucha's arch or circle behind the figure; mosaic and byzantine quotations.
- **Flat areas of color, hard contour line** — no Impressionist dissolution; poster discipline requires readability at 30 meters.
- **Text integrated with image** — headline sits on a banderole, ribbon, or cartouche; not a separate layout zone.
- **Whiplash line as organizing principle** — in Mucha, Grasset, Privat-Livemont, the compositional armature is an S-curve or figure-8 that the figure's body, hair, and drapery all trace.
- **Hair as compositional element** — Mucha's hair fills 30–50% of the poster area, functioning as decorative field and compositional binder.
- **Woman as subject archetype** — idealized, allegorical, semi-nude or draped; often representing the product, the season, the virtue.
- **No fourth wall broken** — figures are self-contained, looking away or into middle distance; the direct-to-camera pointing finger (Kitchener, Uncle Sam) is still 15 years off.

### Printing
- **Color lithography from limestone** — 3 to 12 stones, one per color, registered by hand.
- **Crachis / spatter** — Lautrec's toothbrush-spatter technique for tonal grain.
- **Grease-crayon texture** visible in skies and flats; never the smooth flat fills of later offset.
- **Gold and metallic inks** via separate runs (Mucha, Privat-Livemont).
- **Paper** — uncoated wove, often with a cream tone; poor lightfastness (surviving examples are faded from originals).
- **Registration imperfections** visible as slight color halos or misalignments; authentic to the era, never a defect to "correct."
- **Overlap mixing** — printers intentionally overprint 2 inks to create a third color and reduce stone count; produces the characteristic muted Belle-Époque secondary hues.
- **Chaix atelier in Paris** prints for Chéret and many peers; becomes the benchmark for quality Art-Nouveau reproduction.
- **Imprimerie Champenois** prints most of Mucha's work.
- **Rembrandt Press (Munich)** and **Hollerbaum & Schmidt (Berlin)** emerge in the 1890s as the major German poster shops.
- **Typical print runs** — 1,000–3,000 sheets per poster for a cabaret or exhibition commission; up to 10,000+ for major consumer brands.

### Subject matter
- Cabarets, café-concerts, dance halls (Moulin Rouge, Folies-Bergère, Chat Noir).
- Consumer goods: absinthe, champagne, chocolate, bicycles, cigarette papers (JOB), sewing machines.
- Theatrical productions, especially Sarah Bernhardt.
- Magazines and books (*Harper's*, *The Chap-Book*, *The Yellow Book*).
- Exhibitions and salons (*Salon des Cent*, *Exposition Universelle 1900*).
- Opera (especially the Ricordi press in Milan for Puccini, Verdi productions).
- Department stores (Au Bon Marché, Aux Galeries Lafayette — commissioned Mucha, Grasset, Steinlen).
- Early bicycle craze (Cycles Perfecta, Humber, Waverley — commissioned Mucha, Beggarstaffs, Bradley).
- Resort tourism (early Belle Époque rail posters for Normandy, Côte d'Azur — still nascent).

### Format and sheet size
- **Colombier (24×35in / 61×89cm)** and **Grand Aigle (28×41in / 71×104cm)** standard Parisian sizes.
- **Mucha decorative panels** (*Les Saisons*, *Les Arts*) in a distinctive ~60×21in tall-narrow proportion; often sold as sets.
- **American magazine poster** typically 14×19in or 19×24in — much smaller than Parisian; designed for shop-window display, not wall-pasting.
- **British double crown (20×30in)** standard theatrical and music-hall size.
- **Sheet count** — Parisian kiosk posters were often printed in multi-sheet formats (2-sheet, 4-sheet, 8-sheet) and pasted together on the wall.

## What to apply when asked for this era's work
- **For a Chéret-style cabaret poster (1890s Paris):** vertical portrait format; one dancing female figure center-right in mid-stride; cream ground; palette limited to vermilion + saffron + teal-blue + black key; hand-drawn display caps above ("BAL TABARIN"), smaller script/serif below ("TOUS LES SOIRS"); lithographic grease-crayon texture in the sky; text on a flat colored banderole.
- **For a Mucha-style Art Nouveau panel:** tall narrow panel (3:1 ratio or taller); female figure centered inside an arched/circular halo frame; palette ivory + dark gold + muted olive/sage + oxblood; whiplash hair and drapery; mosaic/Byzantine ornament in background; custom drawn display caps with organic terminals; typically hand-lettered product name or brand running as ornament.
- **For a Lautrec-style Montmartre poster:** flat black silhouette as primary shape (May Milton, Jane Avril, Aristide Bruant); one bright accent color (yellow or red) as ground; diagonal composition with foreground figure cut off by edge; visible *crachis* spatter; hand-drawn caps; cramped urban subject.
- **For an American Arts & Crafts poster (Penfield/Bradley):** 2-color print (black + oxblood, or black + olive); flat painterly figure with hard outlines; calm seated or standing subject; magazine/book title in custom Caslon-esque caps; decorative border optional; cream paper.
- **For a Secessionist Vienna poster (1898–1900):** near-square format; gold + black + ivory palette; geometric ornament (squares, checkerboards, circles) rather than floral whiplash; mythological or allegorical figure (Theseus, Pallas Athena); custom drawn caps with geometric Secession lettering; decorative frame built of small repeat modules.
- **For an Italian Ricordi opera poster (Hohenstein/Metlicovitz):** vertical; theatrical scene from the opera's climactic moment painted in half-Symbolist half-Art-Nouveau manner; richer tonal modeling than French posters (Italians kept more Academic painting under the surface); opera title and theatre name in ornamental caps at top or bottom; deep jewel palette with lots of warm amber.
- **For a British music-hall poster (Hardy/Hassall):** brighter primary palette than French; hand-drawn figure in bold silhouette; headline in Victorian display wood-type revival caps or slab-serif; often a white-haloed figure on bright flat ground; more commercial, less Salon-prestige than Paris.

## What would be anachronistic
- **Photography on posters** — photographic reproduction exists but is not yet integrated with letterpress/litho posters; real photo-posters arrive post-1910.
- **Sans-serif body typography** — sans exists (grotesques from 1830s) but is considered uncouth for posters; the sans-first poster is a Bauhaus-era move.
- **Halftone dots** — the halftone screen (Meisenbach 1882, commercial ~1890) is for magazines, not yet posters.
- **Hard geometric abstraction** — no Bauhaus squares, no Constructivist diagonals; the era's geometry is floral/organic.
- **Airbrush gradients** — airbrush exists (Burdick 1893) but is not a poster tool until the 1920s Cassandre generation.
- **Any digital or photo-mechanical feel** — everything must show the hand of the lithographer and the grain of the stone.
- **Neon/electric color vibrancy of later offset** — pre-1910 pigments read slightly muted vs. 1950s poster color.
- **Sachplakat reductionism (product on flat ground, nothing else)** — that's Bernhard 1906+. Until 1900 every poster has a figure, scene, or ornament.
- **Photomontage of any kind** — invented after WW1 (Heartfield, Höch, Rodchenko c. 1918–23).
- **Streamlined Deco figuration** — a Cassandre-era development (mid-1920s).
- **Offset-lithography smooth flats without crayon grain** — offset is invented 1903 and not dominant for posters until the 1910s; pre-1900 posters must show the stone.
- **Kitchener-style direct-address propaganda** (pointing finger, "YOU") — a 1914+ WW1 invention.
- **"White space as element"** as a self-conscious design move — negative-space discipline is a 1920s modernist idea; Art Nouveau fills the sheet.

## Notable specific posters
- **Jules Chéret, *Bal au Moulin Rouge*** (1889) — the prototype Parisian cabaret poster; spinning dancer silhouette.
- **Jules Chéret, *Folies-Bergère / La Loïe Fuller*** (1893) — the "Chérette" floating on yellow ground.
- **Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec, *Moulin Rouge: La Goulue*** (1891) — foreground black silhouette of Valentin le Désossé; the poster that made Lautrec famous overnight.
- **Toulouse-Lautrec, *Aristide Bruant dans son Cabaret*** (1893) — black cape + red scarf + yellow ground; the absolute Montmartre icon.
- **Toulouse-Lautrec, *Divan Japonais*** (1893) — Jane Avril seated, cropped Yvette Guilbert gloves top-right; Japonisme at its peak.
- **Toulouse-Lautrec, *May Milton*** (1895) — white-gowned figure on cyan ground; Picasso hung a copy in his studio.
- **Alphonse Mucha, *Gismonda*** (1894) — the overnight-print job for Sarah Bernhardt that launched both Mucha and the Style Mucha.
- **Alphonse Mucha, *JOB*** (1896) — cigarette-paper poster; hair curling into smoke.
- **Alphonse Mucha, *The Seasons*** (1896) — 4-panel decorative set; the template for every Art Nouveau panel afterward.
- **Privat-Livemont, *Absinthe Robette*** (1896) — gold-haloed figure holding the absinthe glass; the Belgian peak.
- **Théophile Steinlen, *Tournée du Chat Noir*** (1896) — the cat poster; still in every poster shop today.
- **Pierre Bonnard, *France-Champagne*** (1891) — the Nabis poster that convinced Lautrec to take up the form.
- **Will Bradley, *The Chap-Book / The Twins*** (1894) — the American Beardsley arrives.
- **Edward Penfield, *Harper's March*** (1894) — calm flat figure reading a magazine; the American magazine-poster template.
- **The Beggarstaffs, *Don Quixote*** (1895) — rejected by the theater but adopted by modernists as proto-flat-design.
- **Aubrey Beardsley, *The Yellow Book Prospectus*** (1894) — black-and-yellow whiplash; the British decadent peak.
- **Gustav Klimt, *1st Vienna Secession Exhibition / Theseus and Minotaur*** (1898) — nude hero fighting the Minotaur; censored, reprinted with fig leaf; the Vienna Secession announces itself.
- **Koloman Moser, *Frommes Kalender*** (1899) — Secessionist geometric ornament poster for a calendar maker.
- **Adolfo Hohenstein, *Tosca*** (1899) — Italian opera poster prototype; Tosca mid-scream.
- **Eugène Grasset, *Salon des Cent*** (1894) — woman with fleur-de-lis and palette; the French decorative tradition.
- **Manuel Orazi, *La Maison Moderne*** (1902, carry-over) — Art Nouveau poster for the interior design shop.
- **Jules Chéret, *Saxoléine / Pétrole de Sûreté*** (1891) — woman holding a kerosene lamp; Chéret's commercial range.
- **Henri-Gabriel Ibels, *L'Escarmouche*** (1893) — journal poster; Nabis flat-silhouette peer to Lautrec.

## Influences in / out
**In:** Japanese ukiyo-e woodblock prints (flooded Paris after Japan reopened in 1854; Hokusai, Hiroshige, Utamaro directly cited by Lautrec, Bonnard, Mucha); William Morris Kelmscott and the English Arts & Crafts revival of medieval ornament; Pre-Raphaelite painting; Symbolist poetry and theatre; Byzantine mosaic and Gothic stained glass (Grasset, Mucha).

**Out:** Every subsequent poster tradition starts here. Direct descendants: German Sachplakat rejects Mucha's ornament (1905); Viennese Secession (1898–1905) takes the decorative frame toward geometry; American illustration (Leyendecker, Parrish) inherits the flat-area figure; Art Deco (1920s) is the next-generation synthesis of Mucha's elegance with geometric reduction; the entire 20th-century commercial poster takes Chéret's format (vertical sheet, central figure, flat color) as its default.
