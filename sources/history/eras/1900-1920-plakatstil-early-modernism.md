# 1900–1920 — Plakatstil & Early Modernism

*Germany strips the poster down to object + word + color field. The first world war makes the poster a weapon.*

## Context
Between Mucha's 1900 peak and Bauhaus's 1919 founding, the poster loses its ornament. German advertising agents (especially the Berlin agency Hollerbaum & Schmidt) commission a new reductive style — **Sachplakat** ("object poster") — where a single product floats on a flat color ground with nothing else but the brand name. Meanwhile the 1914–18 war industrializes the poster as mass-persuasion: Britain, Germany, the United States, and Russia each print tens of millions of sheets. By 1920 the poster has learned two things it never forgets: radical reduction and propaganda scale.

## Key movements
- **Sachplakat / Object Poster** (Berlin, 1905–1918) — Bernhard, Erdt, Gipkens, Klinger; product + word, nothing else.
- **Münchner Plakat / Munich school** — Ludwig Hohlwein; flat painted silhouettes, tonal flats, English-influenced.
- **Beggarstaff legacy into Europe** — The 1894–99 Beggarstaffs cut-paper flat silhouette approach finally lands on the continent via Bernhard.
- **Viennese Secession poster** (Klimt, Moser, Roller, 1898–1905) — geometric ornament as bridge between Art Nouveau and modernism.
- **Wiener Werkstätte poster** (1903–1932) — Moser, Hoffmann, Löffler; checkerboard ornament, geometric sans, Secessionist discipline.
- **Italian-French exuberance** — Leonetto Cappiello's caricatural figure-on-black; the counter-current to German reduction.
- **WW1 propaganda poster** (1914–1918) — mass recruitment, war bonds, rationing; the poster as state instrument.
- **Early American magazine modernism** — Leyendecker (Arrow Collar), Coles Phillips, Edward Penfield continuing.
- **Russian pre-revolutionary poster** (1905–1917) — transition from lubok folk-prints to modern political lithography.
- **ROSTA windows (1919–1921)** — Mayakovsky-led stencil-printed propaganda for Moscow shop windows; proto-agitprop.
- **London Underground programme** (Frank Pick as commissioner from 1908) — modernist transit posters, Gregory Brown, Edward Johnston's Underground typeface (1916).

## Masters of the era
- **Lucian Bernhard** (1883–1972, German, later American) — Inventor of Sachplakat with *Priester Matches* (1906); typographer (Bernhard Antiqua, Bernhard Fashion).
- **Ludwig Hohlwein** (1874–1949, German) — Munich painted-poster master; flat tonal areas; later compromised by Nazi propaganda work.
- **Hans Rudi Erdt** (1883–1925, German) — Berlin Sachplakat peer of Bernhard; *Opel* and war posters.
- **Julius Klinger** (1876–1942, Austrian) — Vienna-Berlin; witty geometric reduction; killed at Minsk 1942.
- **Julius Gipkens** (1883–1970, German) — Sachplakat portraits and wartime posters for the agency Hollerbaum & Schmidt.
- **Leonetto Cappiello** (1875–1942, Italian working in Paris) — *Chocolat Klaus* (1903), *Bitter Campari* (1921); figure-on-black caricature.
- **Koloman Moser** (1868–1918, Austrian) — Wiener Werkstätte co-founder; geometric Secessionist poster work.
- **Alfred Roller** (1864–1935, Austrian) — 14th Secession Exhibition poster (1902); the Klimt-circle printmaker.
- **James Montgomery Flagg** (1877–1960, American) — *I Want You / Uncle Sam* (1917) — the most-reprinted poster in American history.
- **Alfred Leete** (1882–1933, British) — *Lord Kitchener Wants You* (1914) — the prototype for Flagg's Uncle Sam.
- **Dmitri Moor (Dmitri Orlov)** (1883–1946, Russian) — *Have You Volunteered?* (1920); Russian Civil War Bolshevik posters; bridge to Constructivism.
- **Jean Carlu** (1900–1997, French) — early career begins c. 1918; will mature in the Deco era.
- **Howard Chandler Christy** (1872–1952, American) — *Gee! I Wish I Were a Man* (1917), *I Want You for the Navy* (1917); "Christy Girl" recruitment imagery.
- **Joseph Christian Leyendecker** (1874–1951, American) — *Saturday Evening Post* covers; Arrow Collar Man; war poster work (*USA Bonds — Weapons for Liberty*).
- **Howard Pyle** (1853–1911, American) — Pirate and historical illustration; indirect poster influence via Brandywine School.
- **Abram Games** (1914–1996, British) — begins too late for this era (core work is WW2+); listed here only as the "what comes next" reference.
- **Frank Brangwyn** (1867–1956, British) — British war posters; painterly figure work.
- **Theodor Zasche** (1862–1922, Austrian) — Austro-Hungarian war posters; classical-illustration school.
- **Joseph Pennell** (1857–1926, American) — WW1 propaganda; *That Liberty Shall Not Perish from the Earth* (1918, burning Statue of Liberty).
- **Fred Spear** (1878–?, American) — *Enlist* (1915); drowned mother and child from the Lusitania.
- **Edward Johnston** (1872–1944, British) — Designed Johnston Sans (1916) for London Underground — the first modern corporate typeface; blocky humanist sans.
- **Edward McKnight Kauffer** (1890–1954, American in London) — Early London Underground posters from 1915; transitions into full Deco by the 1920s.
- **Gregory Brown** (1887–1941, British) — London Underground poster programme; painterly English commercial modernism.
- **Berthold Löffler** (1874–1960, Austrian) — Wiener Werkstätte poster and cabaret work (*Fledermaus* cabaret).
- **Carl Otto Czeschka** (1878–1960, Austrian) — Wiener Werkstätte; geometric illustrative work.
- **Plinio Codognato** (1878–1940, Italian) — Fiat and automotive posters.
- **Marcello Dudovich** (1878–1962, Italian) — Milan commercial poster master; *La Rinascente* department store; Liberty-style elegance into the 1920s.

*(Note: Lester Beall (1903–1969) is not active in this era — his first posters are the 1937 Rural Electrification series. Including him would be anachronistic.)*

## Visual signatures

### Palettes
- **Sachplakat** — 2 to 4 flat colors maximum. Typical: saturated red `#C8102E` ground with black `#121212` object and ivory `#F3E9D2` text. Or mustard `#D4A017` ground, deep brown `#3E2C1C` product, black text.
- **Hohlwein** — tonal earth palette: olive drab `#6B6B3A`, burnt sienna `#8B4513`, cream `#E8DEC8`, warm black `#1F1A15`. Often monochromatic with one accent.
- **Cappiello** — black ground `#0A0A0A` with saturated figure colors: hot red, cadmium yellow, cobalt.
- **WW1 British/American** — patriotic red `#BF0A30`, navy `#002868`, off-white `#F5F0E1`; occasionally khaki.
- **Russian Civil War** — red `#D72638`, black, and newsprint cream; very limited palettes from shortages.
- **Secessionist Vienna** — gold leaf, black, ivory, muted green; Klimt-decorative.

### Typography
- **Sans-serif emerges as acceptable** — grotesque and geometric sans gain legitimacy as "modern industrial" voice.
- **Custom-drawn Sachplakat lettering** — typically the brand name as a 2–4 word block in custom bold slab or grotesque, placed above or beside the object.
- **Bernhard's own types** — Bernhard Antiqua (1913, roman), Bernhard Fashion, Bernhard Gothic — all born from his poster lettering.
- **Akzidenz-Grotesk** (Berthold, 1898) becomes default German sans.
- **Hohlwein** often uses hand-drawn blackletter or Roman caps integrated into the painted composition.
- **British/American war posters** — Gothic/sans caps, sometimes with underlines; headline-first hierarchy.
- **Fraktur and blackletter** retain legitimacy in German posters (official, newspaper, cultural) through this entire period; pre-1933 Germany is still a blackletter culture.
- **Russian pre-revolutionary display** — often ornamental Cyrillic with Art Nouveau or folk-lubok inflection.
- **American poster type** — condensed gothics (Alternate Gothic, Franklin Gothic, Benton Gothic) from the newspaper tradition; hand-drawn Cheltenham derivatives for magazine posters.
- **Key period typefaces** to reference for authentic setting: Akzidenz-Grotesk (1898), Berthold Grotesk, Franklin Gothic (Benton, 1902), News Gothic (1908), Souvenir (1914), Copperplate Gothic (Goudy, 1901), Bernhard Antiqua (1913), Behrens-Schrift (Peter Behrens, 1902, the quintessential Jugendstil type). For Russian Cyrillic: Pushkin, Elizabeth, ornamental hand-lettering.
- **Johnston Sans** (Edward Johnston, 1916) for London Underground — the first modern corporate typeface; legitimizes humanist sans for institutional use.

### Composition
- **Radical subtraction** — Sachplakat rule: product + brand + ground, nothing else. No scene, no figure, no ornament.
- **Flat color field as background** — the ground is a solid color, not a rendered environment.
- **Single object centered or slightly off-center** on the field.
- **Brand name as block**, usually above or below the object, typically in the same bold weight.
- **Hohlwein variant** — single figure or group as flat tonal silhouette with hard edges; product logo integrated as label.
- **Cappiello exception** — caricatured figure leaping on black ground; diagonal energy; product held as prop.
- **WW1 posters** — typically one central figure (soldier, Uncle Sam, Kitchener pointing), full-bleed headline, secondary line below.
- **Format** — large format widely adopted (double-crown, 30×40, grand aigle); designed to be read at street distance.

### Printing
- **Color lithography still dominant**, but increasingly on zinc plates (faster, cheaper than stone).
- **Flat areas replace grease-crayon texture** — Sachplakat explicitly rejects lithographic grain; it wants smooth fills that read as industrial.
- **Reduced color count** (2–4 colors) is a cost/speed decision as much as an aesthetic.
- **Offset lithography** (Rubel, 1903) begins to appear but is not yet standard for posters.
- **War posters** often printed on cheap pulp paper with 2-color runs.
- **Halftone dot integration** — by 1905–10 halftone screens can be combined with litho color runs for first photo-litho hybrid posters.
- **Hollerbaum & Schmidt (Berlin)** — the premier German poster printer of the era; commissions Sachplakat designers under contract.
- **Chromolithographie Wiener Werkstätte** prints for the Moser-Hoffmann circle.
- **Hatchards, David Allen & Sons, John Waddington** — British poster printers of the era.
- **Shortage conditions in 1917–18 Russia and Germany** reduce color count further; posters of late WW1 often 2 flat colors on newsprint.
- **Edition sizes** — German commercial posters 2,000–5,000 sheets; US Liberty Bond campaigns into the hundreds of thousands; British Kitchener poster ~10,000 printed.

### Subject matter
- Branded consumer goods rendered literally: matches, cigars, shoes, typewriters, automobiles (Opel, Adler, Stiller).
- Theater and exhibition announcements (Klinger, Klimt).
- War recruitment, bond drives, rationing, food conservation, atrocity propaganda, victory celebrations.
- Bolshevik agitprop and White-Russian counter-propaganda (1917–1922).
- Magazine covers and fashion (Leyendecker for *Saturday Evening Post*).
- London Underground promotional posters (Frank Pick's programme begins 1908) — cultural and leisure destination posters commissioned from modernists.
- Early aviation and automobile sport (Michelin Bibendum from 1898 carries on; air meetings at Reims 1909, Monaco).
- Temperance, anti-vice, and women's suffrage posters (especially US/UK, 1908–1918).

### Format and paper
- **Double crown (20×30in / 51×76cm)** standard British street poster size.
- **One sheet (28×42in / 71×107cm)** and **grand aigle (40×54in)** for Parisian walls.
- **German Plakat** typically A0-equivalent (33×47in) on cheap machine-coated poster stock.
- Russian war/revolutionary posters often printed on whatever paper was available — visible fiber texture.

## What to apply when asked for this era's work
- **For a Sachplakat-style product poster (1906–1914 Berlin):** vertical format; single saturated flat color ground (choose from red, mustard, deep blue, forest green); one product object rendered flat with minimal modeling, centered or slightly off-center; brand name as a 2–3 word block of bold custom slab or grotesque caps, positioned above or below the product, same color as product or clean black; NO figures, NO scenes, NO ornament, NO gradients.
- **For a Hohlwein-style painted poster (1908–1925 Munich):** figure or product as flat tonal silhouette; olive/sienna/cream earth palette; hard-edged flat shapes with simple tonal modeling (2 tones per plane, never gradients); hand-lettered caps or blackletter headline integrated into the composition; cropped close to subject.
- **For a Cappiello-style caricature poster (1900s–1910s Paris):** deep black ground; one exuberant caricatured figure leaping or gesturing, painted in flat saturated colors (red, yellow, white); product held as prop or floating next to figure; product name in custom bold caps; energetic diagonal composition.
- **For a WW1 recruitment poster (1914–1918):** full-bleed portrait or symbolic figure (soldier, statesman, Uncle Sam, Britannia); one large bold headline at top or bottom in gothic/sans caps ("YOUR COUNTRY NEEDS YOU", "ENLIST TODAY"); red/white/blue patriotic palette; direct gaze and pointing hand; 2–3 color lithograph on cheap paper.
- **For early Russian agitprop (1917–1920, pre-Rodchenko):** red and black on newsprint cream; simple blocky figure; hand-lettered Cyrillic caps; emblematic imagery (hammer, star, worker silhouette); no photomontage yet.
- **For a ROSTA window (Mayakovsky, 1919–21):** 4–12 panel comic-strip grid on a single sheet; stencil or hand-painted flat color; simple schematic figures with labels; red/black/yellow palette; satirical or instructional captions per panel. Closer to agitprop poster than to propaganda — specifically designed for display in empty Moscow shop windows.
- **For American WW1 Liberty Bond poster:** warmer, more painterly than British; Christy Girl or heroic soldier rendered in academic illustration; red/white/blue; hand-drawn Roman caps headline; often vignette composition fading to white rather than full-bleed.
- **For a Secession-adjacent Vienna / Wiener Werkstätte poster (1903–1914):** near-square or portrait; geometric ornament (Hoffmann checkerboards, Moser squares) rather than German industrial reduction; hand-drawn geometric display lettering; muted colors with black and gold; a bridge between Art Nouveau and modernism.

## What would be anachronistic
- **Photomontage** — Hannah Höch and Rodchenko invent photomontage c. 1919–1923; it is NOT part of the 1900–1918 poster.
- **Constructivist diagonals with geometric blocks** — that is post-1920 Rodchenko/Lissitzky territory.
- **Bauhaus primary-color grid** — the Bauhaus doesn't exist until April 1919 and its poster language isn't mature until 1923.
- **Art Deco streamlining** — Cassandre's streamlined Deco is a mid-to-late 1920s phenomenon.
- **Airbrush gradients** — still not in the poster toolbox.
- **Helvetica or Swiss grids** — forty years early.
- **Full-color photographic posters** — photography appears on propaganda posters (esp. later WW1) but always hand-retouched, and is not yet the dominant mode.
- **Sans-serif still feels radical** — in 1906 using a bold grotesque for a product name is an aesthetic statement; don't present it as neutral/default.
- **"Less is more" as a verbalized principle** — the reduction is happening but no one is saying "form follows function" in this context yet; that's Sullivan 1896 architecture, quoted into design later.

- **Lucian Bernhard, *Stiller Shoes*** (1908) — two brown shoes on yellow ground; the format perfected.
- **Lucian Bernhard, *Adler Typewriters*** (1909) — typewriter on red; pure object + word.
- **Hans Rudi Erdt, *Opel*** (1911) — car on field; German auto advertising born.
- **Hans Rudi Erdt, *U-Boote Heraus!*** (1917) — German war poster; U-boat captain in profile.
- **Ludwig Hohlwein, *Hermann Scherrer Breeches Maker*** (1908) — flat-tonal English hunting gentleman; Hohlwein's breakout.
- **Ludwig Hohlwein, *Marco Polo Tee*** (1910) — tonal cream silhouette; Munich's counter to Berlin.
- **Leonetto Cappiello, *Chocolat Klaus*** (1903) — red-caped girl on black horse; the Italian-French caricature tradition arrives.
- **Leonetto Cappiello, *Bitter Campari*** (1921) — the orange-peel harlequin; Cappiello's most-reproduced image.
- **Koloman Moser, *13th Secession Exhibition*** (1902) — gold + black geometric Vienna.
- **Alfred Roller, *14th Secession Exhibition (Beethoven)*** (1902) — tall narrow format with Klimt-Beethoven ornament.
- **Alfred Leete, *Lord Kitchener Wants You*** (1914, London) — Kitchener's finger pointing from the cover of *London Opinion*.
- **James Montgomery Flagg, *I Want You for U.S. Army / Uncle Sam*** (1917) — direct Kitchener homage; 4 million copies printed.
- **Dmitri Moor, *Have You Volunteered?*** (1920) — red-shirted soldier pointing; early Soviet mobilization.
- **Julius Klinger, *8te Kriegsanleihe*** (1917) — 8 arrows piercing a dragon; Vienna war-loan poster; pre-modernist geometric storytelling.

## Influences in / out
**In:** The Beggarstaffs' cut-paper silhouettes (1894–99) directly seed Sachplakat via Berlin commercial agents aware of the English work. Japanese flat-area composition (via the earlier Lautrec generation). William Morris type revivals feeding into Bernhard's typefaces. Viennese Secession's geometric decoration as a bridge from Art Nouveau. The industrial context of German trade advertising demanding readable, reproducible mass posters.

**Out:** Sachplakat's radical reduction is the direct parent of Bauhaus graphic design (Moholy-Nagy, Bayer all cite Bernhard) and of Swiss Modernism (Müller-Brockmann's product posters of the 1950s are literal updates of the Sachplakat formula). Hohlwein's flat tonal painting influences interwar poster design across Europe and tragically becomes the template for Nazi propaganda. WW1 propaganda methods feed directly into WW2 posters (Ben Shahn, Norman Rockwell on the Allied side; Hohlwein on the Axis). Cappiello's figurative exuberance becomes the French tradition that leads to Savignac and the 1950s French humor-poster school.
