# Hara — Emptiness and Designing Design

*Kenya Hara, "Designing Design" (2007) and "White" (2009). Japanese minimalist philosophy rendered as working design rules — the LLM-actionable distillation.*

## Emptiness (haku 白) — the active container

- **Rule:** White is not absence of content. White is the container that enables content to be perceived.
- A full bowl holds one serving. An empty bowl holds any possibility.
- **Target: 60–80% of layout area should be emptiness** for emptiness-driven work.
- Emptiness must feel intentional — measured, calibrated — not "space left over."
- Never fill emptiness with decorative filler. If you're tempted to fill it, the composition is wrong.

## Ma (間) — the interval that creates relationship

- **Ma = the meaningful gap** between two elements. Not distance, but relational tension.
- Two elements close → they form a pair. Two elements far → they form a tension field. Middle distance → ambiguous; avoid.
- **Rule:** When placing two elements, ask "what is the ma saying?" If nothing, reposition.
- Ma applies to time too: pacing in animation, pauses in a pitch, silence in packaging unveiling.

## Quiet as strategy (loudness is weakness)

- **Rule:** Loud design signals a weak idea trying to compensate. Quiet design signals confidence.
- Reduce saturation, reduce contrast, reduce size until content becomes precious, not noisy.
- A whisper in silence is louder than a shout in noise.
- If your layout could fit on a full-color billboard, it's too loud for Hara's world.

## "Not finding" — design for user discovery

- **Rule:** Don't deliver meaning. Create conditions where the user discovers meaning.
- Hide the brand. Remove the caption. Let the object be itself before being labeled.
- User effort in noticing = user ownership of meaning.
- Example: MUJI packaging — no logo face-out, no marketing copy, product shown as fact.

## Hierarchy through whitespace, not weight

- **Rule:** Most important = most surrounding white space. Least important = densest cluster.
- Do NOT use bold, color, or larger size as primary hierarchy tools.
- **Target ratio for emphasis**: hero element gets 3–5× the white space of secondary elements.
- Size differences stay subtle: 1.2–1.5× ratio between levels, not 3×.

## Packaging as communication

- Packaging is not wrapping — it is the first message.
- **Rule:** Material choice IS the design. Kraft paper says honesty. Rice paper says delicacy. Unbleached cotton says rawness.
- Printing: 1–2 colors maximum. Often just 1.
- Type: small, set once, on one face.
- **Forbidden:** gloss lamination, metallic foils (unless the product literally is metal), any "luxury" signaling that isn't inherent.

## Four seasons principle — continuity through change

- Japanese design embraces seasonal variation as continuity of a fixed frame.
- **Rule:** Design a constant structure (packaging template, poster grid) that changes ONE element with the season/campaign. Structure = identity. Variation = life.
- Do not redesign the system. Renew content within the system.
- Applies to: product lines, editorial, recurring campaigns.

## Signs of the hand (craft visible, not slick)

- **Rule:** Let the material show its making. Brush strokes visible. Fiber visible in paper. Slight irregularity in hand-letterform.
- Anti-slick: avoid the CGI-clean, pixel-perfect, vector-sharp aesthetic.
- Texture: matte over gloss. Tactile over smooth. Subtle imperfection over mechanical uniformity.
- Digital work can still signal hand: slightly off-kilter placement, hand-drawn element, raw photo without retouch.

## Color discipline — the almost-absent palette

- **Primary palette:**
  - Warm white (#F8F5F0 – #FFFFFF) — off-whites, cream, rice paper
  - Kraft / unbleached (#D7C4A9 – #E8DCC5)
  - Near-black (#1A1A1A – #2B2B2B) — never pure #000
  - One earth accent: sumi ink black, indigo (#1B3A6B), vermilion (#C2362A), moss (#6B7A4A)
- **Rule:** Maximum 3 colors per composition. Often 2. Sometimes 1.
- Saturation: desaturate everything. Chroma 10–40%, not 80–100%.
- No gradients. No shadows. No ambient glow.

## Typography

- Japanese serif (Mincho) for literary / quiet / traditional.
- Japanese sans (Gothic) for functional / modern / product.
- Western pairing: Garamond, Caslon, Adobe Jenson (quiet serifs); Helvetica Neue Light, Univers 45 (quiet sans).
- **Rule:** One typeface, two weights maximum. Small sizes. Generous leading (1.6–2.0×).
- Set type small and let white space give it importance. Never compensate with size.

## Photography and image

- **Rule:** Object isolated against solid off-white. Single light source. Soft shadow. No set dressing.
- Color muted. Grain acceptable. Gloss never.
- Subject fills 20–40% of frame. The rest is emptiness.
- If the image "looks like an ad," kill it and reshoot.

## Anti-patterns — what Hara would reject

- **Reject:** bold gradients, neon colors, high-saturation imagery, glossy finishes.
- **Reject:** crowded layouts, maximized screen real estate, "every pixel counts" density.
- **Reject:** loud logos, wordmark dominance, aggressive callouts ("NEW!" "50% OFF!").
- **Reject:** drop shadows, bevels, skeuomorphic textures, 3D-rendered lux.
- **Reject:** "delight" as clutter — animated confetti, celebratory popups, bouncing microinteractions.
- **Reject:** faux-handmade fonts masquerading as craft; the hand must be real.

## Digital translation of Hara principles

- Dark mode: not pure black background. Use #1A1A1A with #F0EDE6 warm-white text.
- Light mode: off-white #F8F5F0, near-black #1A1A1A text. Pure #FFFFFF is too sterile.
- Line weight: 1px hairlines. 0.5px on Retina.
- Animation: 250–400ms, standard easing. No bounces, no overshoots. Gentle fade preferred over slide.
- Cursor: default arrow. Hover states: subtle opacity shift (0.8–1.0), no color flash.

## Quick validation checklist (apply to any "quiet" composition)

1. Is 60–80% of the layout emptiness? If not → remove elements.
2. Are there ≤ 3 colors, all desaturated, no pure black or white?
3. Is hierarchy driven by surrounding space, not weight/color/size?
4. Is there any gloss, gradient, shadow, bevel? Remove.
5. Is the material/medium choice itself meaningful? If just "default," reconsider.
6. Does the user have to discover something (meaning, logo, context)? Good.
7. Could a competitor shout louder and win? Good — you're not competing on volume.
8. Is there one sign of the hand (texture, asymmetry, slight irregularity)?
