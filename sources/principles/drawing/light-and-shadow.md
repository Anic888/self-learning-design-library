# Light and Shadow

*The form principle and the physics of light, translated to actionable values. Works for illustration, paint, and photography — the rules are the same; the tools differ.*

## The form principle — anatomy of a lit form

On any 3D form under a single light, you see five (sometimes six) distinct value zones. Memorize the order — getting it wrong kills the illusion.

1. **Highlight** — direct specular bounce from the light. Smallest area, brightest value. Position: where surface normal points at light source.
2. **Light / halftone** — the broad lit plane. The "local color" at full intensity minus slight angle falloff.
3. **Form shadow / core shadow** — the terminator. Where surface turns away from light. **Darkest point on the form itself** (not darker than cast shadow — see below).
4. **Reflected light** — bounce from surrounding surfaces into the shadow side. Lifts the shadow slightly. Never as bright as the light side.
5. **Occlusion shadow** — where form meets ground (or itself). No light reaches here — not even ambient. **Darkest value in the image.**
6. **Cast shadow** — shadow thrown onto adjacent surfaces. Sharpest near the form, softer with distance.

**Rule**: core shadow is NOT the darkest part. Occlusion is. Cast shadow is second. Core is third. Violating this flattens the form.

## The 5-value structure (simplified)

For quick thumbnails and poster reads, collapse to 5 values:

- **Value 1** (near-black, ~5%): occlusion, deep cast shadow
- **Value 2** (dark, ~25%): core shadow, form shadow
- **Value 3** (mid, ~50%): reflected light, halftone on shadow side
- **Value 4** (light, ~75%): lit plane, halftone on light side
- **Value 5** (near-white, ~95%): highlight

Poster rule: if your design doesn't read in 5 values (as a grayscale thumbnail), it won't read at scale.

## Lighting styles (named)

### Chiaroscuro (Caravaggio)
- **High contrast** — full value range used, but heavy emphasis on the dark end (60–70% dark, 30–40% light).
- Deep blacks, selective bright lights, no middle value on the face.
- Light source usually top-side, 45°+, often single source.
- Emotional register: dramatic, theatrical, sacred, dangerous.
- References: Caravaggio, Artemisia Gentileschi, Rembrandt (softer), La Tour.

### Sfumato (Leonardo)
- **Smoky transitions** — no hard edges anywhere on the form.
- Values blend softly from light to dark; terminator barely visible.
- Overall contrast moderate, often mid-range dominant.
- "Like smoke" (Leonardo): layered thin glazes.
- Register: mystery, contemplation, softness, introspection.
- Mona Lisa's smile, entire Renaissance portraiture tradition.

### Tenebrism
- **Almost all shadow** — a single shaft of light picks out subject from near-black surround.
- ~80%+ of frame is deep shadow; lit area is small and bright.
- Stronger / more extreme than chiaroscuro.
- References: Caravaggio at his darkest, Georges de La Tour (candlelight tenebrism), film noir.

### Rim / backlight
- Light source behind subject. Silhouettes subject; rims edges with bright halo.
- Subject face is in shadow; edges glow.
- Hair, fur, fabric edges glow brightly (translucent materials picked out).
- Register: ethereal, cinematic, heroic, mysterious.
- Use in posters for silhouettes against sky, doorway figures, divine-light effects.

### High-key vs low-key
- **High-key**: mostly light values (valued 3–5 dominant), minimal shadow, soft light. Feels light, happy, clean.
- **Low-key**: mostly dark values (1–3 dominant), deep shadows, hard or directional light. Feels moody, serious, noir.

## Light source types

- **Point source** (bare bulb, candle, distant sun pinhole): sharp shadows, radial falloff (inverse square law — brightness drops as 1/distance²).
- **Directional** (sun, key spotlight): nearly parallel rays, sharp shadows, no visible falloff (distance to sun makes it effectively parallel).
- **Ambient**: fills everywhere, no shadow. Overcast sky or indirect bounce. No form modeling alone → combine with directional.
- **Area source** (softbox, window, overcast sky patch): wraps, softens, blurs shadows proportionally to size.

## Hard vs soft shadows

- **Hard shadow**: light source is small relative to subject (or far enough to be effectively a point). Crisp edges.
- **Soft shadow**: light source is large relative to subject. Penumbra (soft edge) widens with source size and distance from subject to shadow-receiving surface.
- **Rule**: to soften a shadow, either (a) make the source bigger, (b) move source closer to subject, or (c) push the shadow-catching surface further from the subject.
- Sun alone: hard shadow (sun is tiny at that distance).
- Sun through cloud: soft (cloud becomes the effective area source).

## Multiple light sources

- Each source creates its own shadow set. Read as: dominant direction + secondary fill.
- **Key** = brightest, dominant. **Fill** = secondary, lifts shadows, usually opposite side.
- Multiple hard-light sources → multiple crossing shadows — reads "studio" or "theatrical."
- One key + ambient fill = natural default for illustration.
- Avoid: three or more equal sources unless you want the "flat" look. Intersecting shadows confuse form.

## Color of light vs color of shadow

**Core principle**: warm light produces cool shadow. Cool light produces warm shadow. Eye color-adapts; brain exaggerates complement.

- Golden-hour warm light (#FFD49A) → shadows tend blue-violet (#5C6B8A)
- Overcast cool daylight (#C8D4E0) → shadows tend warm neutral (#8A7864)
- Tungsten interior warm (#FFB070) → shadows cool cyan (#5A7890)
- Moonlight cool blue (#AFC4DE) → shadows warm brown
- Firelight warm orange (#FF8040) → shadows cyan-blue (#3070A0)

**Shadow color ≠ local color × dark multiplier.** Shift in complement direction for realism.

## Atmospheric and volumetric light

- **Fog / haze / dust**: scatters light. Reduces contrast in distance. Cools or warms by particle type (water → blue shift, dust → warm).
- **God-rays / volumetric light / crepuscular rays**: visible shafts where beams pass through particulate medium. Essentially, fog made visible by a strong directional source.
- Posters: add volumetric light for mood amplification. Sacred, mysterious, sci-fi, mornings.
- Color of volumetric ray ≈ color of light source, slightly desaturated by scatter.

## Time-of-day character

- **Golden hour** (sunrise ~0:00–1:00, sunset –1:00–0:00): low-angle warm light (#FFC080 to #FF9050), long shadows, soft directional. Low contrast. Warm/cool split maximum.
- **Blue hour** (twilight, 30 min after sunset or before sunrise): ambient cool (#7090B8 to #40607A), no hard shadows, practical lights (windows) pop warm.
- **Midday** (zenith sun): overhead directional, short shadows, high contrast, neutral white balance. Posters avoid this for portraits (under-eye shadow, flat nose, "harsh" look) unless heroic.
- **Twilight / dusk**: progressive value collapse. Sky cool, land dark. Silhouette territory.
- **Night**: local light sources dominate (practical light — streetlamps, windows, neon). Rim lighting natural. Dark values carry the weight.
- **Overcast**: giant softbox. Everything soft, flat, cool. No directional shadow. Good for pure local-color reads.

## Illustration vs photography application

### Illustration
- You invent the light. Use the form principle consciously — decide core shadow, highlight, occlusion for every form.
- Simplify: 3 values minimum, 5 values standard, 7+ only for realism.
- Lean into designed color temperature — warm/cool shadow split even more than nature shows.
- Cast shadows can be stylized shapes (flat graphic shadows, see WPA posters, Saul Bass).

### Photography
- You select/shape existing light. Light direction, size, distance, and modifiers are your variables.
- See `/photography/lighting-setups.md` for crafted setups.
- Golden hour/blue hour timing is the cheapest "good light."
- For illustration-like feel in photo: hard single light + deep shadow + minimal fill.

## Quick validation checklist

1. Single light source: is there ONE clear direction?
2. Is occlusion darker than core shadow? (rule violation kills form)
3. Does reflected light lift shadow without matching light side?
4. Do cast shadows match light direction and soften with distance?
5. Warm light + cool shadow (or inverse)? Not same-hue-just-darker.
6. Is the image readable as 5 values in grayscale thumbnail?
7. Highlight placed where surface normal points at light?
8. Multiple sources: one clearly dominant (key)? Or deliberate theatrical stack?
