# Perspective

*Linear and atmospheric perspective for poster work. The geometry that makes a 2D frame read as 3D space — and the rules you break on purpose when it shouldn't.*

## Horizon line = viewer's eye level

- The horizon line is **always at eye level**, by definition. Not "the ground" — your eyes.
- Low horizon (bottom third): subject looms, viewer feels small, sky dominates. Heroic, monumental.
- High horizon (top third): viewer looks down, ground dominates, intimate or diagrammatic.
- Centered horizon: neutral, documentary, often static. Avoid for dramatic posters.
- **Rule**: decide horizon FIRST, before any vanishing points. Everything else hangs off it.

## 1-point perspective

- One vanishing point (VP) on the horizon line. All depth-lines converge there; verticals stay vertical; horizontals stay horizontal.
- Use cases: interior corridors, train tracks, road-into-distance, facing a wall head-on, stage sets.
- **VP placement**:
  - Dead center → symmetrical, formal, Kubrick one-point shot
  - Off-center (left/right third) → dynamic, asymmetric, "journey" feel
- Works best when subject is **facing you squarely**. The moment the subject rotates, switch to 2-point.
- Classic examples: Kubrick corridors, Van Gogh's *Bedroom*, da Vinci's *Last Supper*.

## 2-point perspective

- Two VPs on the horizon line, typically OFF the picture plane (outside the frame edges).
- Use cases: architectural views, buildings seen corner-on, boxes in space, street scenes where you see two sides of structures.
- **Verticals stay vertical** — this is the cardinal rule. If your verticals tilt in a 2-point setup, you've accidentally drifted into 3-point.
- VP spacing: wider apart → flatter, more natural. Closer together → fish-eye distortion, dramatic.
- **Common trap**: placing both VPs inside the frame. They should sit off-frame unless you want wide-angle warp.
- For neutral architectural feel: VPs ~2× frame-width apart.
- For dramatic looming: VPs ~1× frame-width apart.

## 3-point perspective

- Two VPs on the horizon + a third VP above or below.
- **Worm's-eye** (VP above): looking up. Subject towers. Verticals converge skyward. Skyscrapers, heroic figures, monsters.
- **Bird's-eye** (VP below): looking down. Subject shrinks. Verticals converge to the ground. Maps, crowd scenes, falling subjects.
- The third VP controls how extreme the tilt reads. Close 3rd VP → vertigo. Far 3rd VP → subtle monumentality.
- Rule of thumb: for a poster of a looming figure/tower, put the 3rd VP 1.5–2× frame-height from the horizon.
- Comic-book heroic shots are 95% 3-point worm's-eye. Spidey swinging = worm's-eye 3-point almost always.

## Station point (viewer distance)

- Station point = where the viewer is standing. Controls the **cone of vision** (usually 60° for natural, 90° for wide-angle).
- Short station point (viewer close) → extreme foreshortening, fish-eye feel.
- Long station point (viewer far) → compressed, telephoto feel, flattened planes.
- For posters: long station point flattens the image. Good for iconic, graphic reads.
- Short station point adds drama but distorts — only use when distortion IS the point.

## Atmospheric (aerial) perspective

Distance blues, fades, and desaturates. Atmospheric perspective works alongside linear perspective — often stronger in landscape posters.

- **Desaturation with distance**: foreground 100% chroma → midground 60% → background 30% → horizon 10–15%
- **Blue shift**: distant forms pick up atmospheric blue (cyan-blue haze, ~#A8C4D9 to #D0DCE4)
- **Value compression**: distant forms move toward mid-value. No deep blacks at horizon. No pure whites either.
- **Contrast reduction**: foreground keeps full contrast, distance has 30–50% contrast
- **Edge softening**: crisp foreground edges, soft/lost distant edges
- Dust/smog tints the haze warm (ochre #C8B48A) — not always blue. Mars landscape goes orange-pink.

## When to break perspective — intentionally

- **Fauvist / expressionist** (Matisse, Derain): tilt the plane for emotion. Room floors rise up; table angles don't match.
- **Japanese woodblock** (Hokusai): isometric-ish, no vanishing points, parallel depth lines. Still reads clearly.
- **Medieval / Byzantine**: hierarchical scale — important figures are LARGER regardless of distance. Perspective ignored.
- **Cubism** (Picasso, Braque): multiple viewpoints in one frame. Breaks perspective as subject.
- **De Chirico / Magritte surrealism**: correct perspective but impossible subjects/shadows — the perspective stays sharp so the wrongness reads.
- **Flat design 2010s**: no perspective at all, pure elevation. Reads as diagram.

**Rule**: break it on purpose or not at all. Accidental perspective errors look amateur; deliberate ones look stylized.

## Common mistakes

1. **Tilted verticals in 2-point** — verticals must stay vertical. Tilting means you've slipped into accidental 3-point.
2. **Horizon inconsistency** — if one object's horizon is at eye-level, all objects' horizons must match. Can't have two horizons in one frame.
3. **VPs too close in 2-point** — creates unintentional fish-eye. Push them further off-frame.
4. **Foreshortening errors** — objects pointed at viewer compress. A 10m stretched arm should shrink to 30–40% visible length, not stay full.
5. **Shadow directions that don't match light source** — shadows are a perspective system too. Shadow vanishing points tie back to the light source.
6. **Eye-level mismatch to figures** — if horizon is at 1.7m (standing adult eye level), standing adults' eyes should sit ON the horizon. Crowd scenes reveal this instantly.
7. **Reflecting without a ground plane** — reflections in puddles/windows follow their own perspective rules tied to the surface.
8. **Forcing 3-point when 2-point would do** — tilting verticals adds drama but can make posters feel queasy. Reserve for looming/falling subjects.

## Applied to poster work

- **Movie posters**: heroic shot = 3-point worm's-eye; intimate drama = 1-point frontal; action = 2-point with diagonal.
- **Travel posters**: 1-point down a road/canal/street reads as "journey." WPA/Cassandre classics are almost all 1-point.
- **Architectural/real-estate**: 2-point with VPs far apart. Keep verticals vertical. This is neutral and flattering.
- **Horror / monster**: 3-point worm's-eye, third VP close, distorted.
- **Editorial/infographic**: isometric or axonometric (no vanishing points, parallel projection). Reads as diagram, depth-encoded by overlap.
- **Fashion / portrait**: telephoto-ish, long station point, compressed. Backgrounds flatten, figure pops.
- **Atmospheric / mood**: layer aerial perspective — 3–5 depth planes each lighter/bluer/less contrasty.

## Quick validation checklist

1. Where is the horizon? Does it match viewer eye-level implied by the subject?
2. Are all eye-levels of standing figures on the horizon line?
3. In 2-point: are all verticals vertical? If not → switch to 3-point or fix.
4. Are VPs far enough off-frame to avoid fish-eye (unless intentional)?
5. Do distant elements desaturate, blue-shift, lose contrast?
6. Do shadows share a consistent light source / vanishing point?
7. Is foreshortening applied where limbs/objects point at viewer?
8. If perspective is broken — is it stylistically deliberate and consistent, or accidental?
