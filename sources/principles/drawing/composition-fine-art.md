# Composition — Fine Art Fundamentals

*The geometry of where things go. Rules that shape visual hierarchy, balance, and eye movement — in paintings and by direct transfer, in posters.*

## Rule of thirds

- Divide the frame into 3 × 3 grid. Four intersection points.
- Focal points land on intersections, not dead center.
- Horizons along the upper or lower third line — never cut frame in half (unless symmetry is the point).
- Vertical subjects (figures, trees, towers) along the left or right third.
- **Strength**: 70% of usable poster layouts work on rule of thirds. Safe default.
- **Weakness**: predictable. Can feel formulaic when overused.

## Golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618)

- Divide so longer part / shorter part = whole / longer part.
- For a 1000px frame: primary division at 618px (golden section).
- **Golden rectangle**: sides in 1 : 1.618 ratio. Can be subdivided recursively into smaller golden rectangles.
- **Golden spiral**: logarithmic spiral drawn through recursive golden rectangles. Eye follows it naturally.
- Real usage: renaissance paintings, Nautilus shell, modern logos (Apple, Twitter bird both cite it).
- In practice: golden ratio division ≈ 62%/38% split — use for asymmetric layouts that feel "right" without being 50/50 or rigid 33/67.

## Dynamic symmetry (Jay Hambidge root rectangles)

- Root rectangles built from squares: √2 (1 : 1.414), √3 (1 : 1.732), √5 (1 : 2.236).
- Diagonal + reciprocal lines create armature — grid of intersections for focal points.
- **Key use**: when you want a composition that feels mathematically resolved but not golden-ratio obvious.
- Hambidge's system drove Bellows, Robert Henri, many early-20th-c American painters.
- For poster work: root-2 rectangle (1 : 1.414) is exactly A-series paper ratio. Handy coincidence.

## Leading lines

- Lines (roads, rivers, arms, gazes, fences, beams) that point the eye at a focal subject.
- Curved > straight > zigzag for gentle guidance. Diagonals > horizontals > verticals for energy.
- Converging leading lines = 1-point perspective as composition tool.
- **Gaze is a leading line**. Figure looking off-frame drags viewer's attention off-frame. Figure looking into frame holds attention.
- Multiple competing leading lines = confusion. Pick one dominant path.

## S-curves and C-curves

- **S-curve**: slow, elegant, long. Reads as grace, femininity, journey. Landscape classic: river S-curving into distance.
- **C-curve**: encloses, cradles, holds focal point in a bowl.
- Reverse-S (Z-curve, backwards): energetic, punchy, often seen in battle scenes, advertising hero shots.
- Hogarth called the S-curve the "line of beauty" (1753) — still cited.

## Tangents (avoid)

- A tangent = when two edges just barely touch without overlap or clear separation.
- Creates visual ambiguity, flattens depth, distracts eye.
- Examples: head-top exactly on horizon line; two branches kissing at frame edge; figure's hand exactly at doorframe edge.
- **Fix**: either clearly overlap (near edge clearly in front) OR clearly separate (5–10% frame width between).
- Unavoidable tangent? Reshoot or redraw. They will not go unnoticed.

## Triangulation

- Three-point arrangements form triangles — strongest compositional primitive.
- **Stable triangle** (base at bottom, apex at top): pyramid. Mountain. Monument. Reads solid, permanent.
- **Inverted triangle** (apex down): unstable, tense, falling. Horror, disaster, vertigo.
- **Tilted triangle**: energetic, dynamic. Action poses.
- Classical portraits often build torso-head triangles: shoulders base, head apex = stable pyramid.
- Multi-figure scenes: arrange heads as triangle points. Group photos obey this.

## Rule of odds

- Odd numbers (3, 5, 7) feel natural and dynamic.
- Even numbers (2, 4, 6) feel paired, static, adversarial.
- 3 elements: one dominant + two supporting = natural hierarchy.
- 2 elements: immediate tension, the eye ping-pongs.
- Reason: odd groups can't be split evenly → asymmetry built in → dynamic.
- Still-life rule: odd number of objects. Florists: odd number of stems.

## Figure-ground relationships

- Figure = subject; ground = background. Their boundary must read clearly.
- **Value separation**: if figure and ground share the same value range, silhouette vanishes.
- **Chroma separation**: warm figure on cool ground (or vice versa) separates even at same value.
- **Edge type**: crisp figure-edge pulls figure forward; soft edge integrates with ground.
- Figure-ground reversal (Rubin's vase, M.C. Escher): intentional ambiguity as subject.

## Positive / negative space

- Positive = subject shapes. Negative = the shape of the space between/around them.
- **Negative space is a shape too** — draw it, balance it, don't leave it accidental.
- Heavy positive + light negative = weighty. Balanced ≈ 60/40 or 70/30 — never 50/50 unless symmetry is the point.
- Japanese *ma* (間): the meaningful empty. Negative space as content.
- Poster rule: give the headline room to breathe. Tight negative space = claustrophobic.

## Rhythm and repetition

- Repeated element at intervals = rhythm. Columns, arches, footsteps, windows.
- **Regular rhythm**: even spacing, metronomic. Calm, formal, architectural.
- **Progressive rhythm**: intervals change (widening, tightening). Builds or releases tension.
- **Syncopation**: broken pattern. One unexpected break in repetition = focal point.
- Repeat 3+ times to establish the rhythm before breaking it.

## Focal hierarchy (primary / secondary / tertiary)

- **Primary focal point (1)**: where the eye lands first. Sharpest contrast, strongest color, sharpest focus, visual center of mass.
- **Secondary (1–2)**: where eye goes next. Supporting elements, less contrast.
- **Tertiary (2–3)**: context, detail, rewards slower viewing.
- Total: 3–6 hierarchy steps. More than that = no hierarchy.
- Poster rule: viewer should grasp the primary in < 1 second. Everything else is reward.

## Unified field

- All compositional elements must belong to the same "world" — consistent light, color temperature, edge treatment, style.
- Breaking the field (one crisp photo element in a painterly field, one isolated saturated spot) either → focal point (deliberate) or → broken composition (accidental).
- Use this: drop ONE thing out of field to force attention to it.

## Gestalt principles

How the brain groups visual elements — composition exploits these:

- **Proximity**: nearby elements read as grouped. Gap > 2× element-size breaks the group.
- **Similarity**: shared color/shape/size = group, even when separated.
- **Closure**: brain completes partial shapes (broken circles read as circles). Lets you imply rather than draw.
- **Continuity**: eye follows smooth lines/curves even across gaps. Letterforms rely on this.
- **Common fate**: elements moving / pointing same direction group (e.g. arrows aligned → one force).
- **Figure-ground** (again): brain assigns figure vs. ground automatically; designer can exploit or subvert.
- **Prägnanz** (good figure): brain prefers simplest interpretation. Clear shapes win over ambiguous.

## Weight and visual mass

- Not physical mass — **perceived weight** based on:
  - **Size**: larger = heavier
  - **Value**: darker = heavier (on light ground); lighter = heavier (on dark ground)
  - **Chroma**: more saturated = heavier
  - **Position**: lower = heavier; further from center = heavier (lever arm)
  - **Isolation**: a lone element in empty space is heavy (attracts eye)
  - **Complexity**: detailed/textured = heavier than flat
  - **Warm colors**: slightly heavier than cool at same value
- Balance: total weight left ≈ total weight right (or top/bottom). Doesn't have to be symmetric — small dark element balances large light mass like a seesaw.
- Deliberate imbalance = tension, motion, drama. Perfect balance = stillness, formality.

## Quick validation checklist

1. Primary focal point identified in < 1 second?
2. Subject placed on thirds / golden section — or centered intentionally?
3. Any accidental tangents? Fix or embrace.
4. Figure-ground: does silhouette read clearly in black-and-white thumbnail?
5. Odd number of major elements (3, 5, 7)?
6. Visual weight balanced across frame (not dumped on one side unless deliberate)?
7. Leading lines point AT the focal, not away?
8. Negative space intentionally shaped — not leftover?
