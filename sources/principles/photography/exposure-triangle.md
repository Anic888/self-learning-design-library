# The Exposure Triangle

*ISO, aperture, shutter speed. Three variables, one exposure. Each affects the image beyond just brightness — they're creative choices, not just technical ones.*

## The three variables

Exposure is determined by how much light hits the sensor and how sensitive it is. Three knobs:

- **ISO** — sensor sensitivity
- **Aperture** — size of the lens opening
- **Shutter speed** — how long the sensor sees the light

Change any one, compensate with another to hold exposure constant. Each also changes a second property (noise, depth of field, motion).

## Stops — the common currency

- **1 stop = double or half the light.**
- ISO: 100 → 200 = +1 stop. 200 → 400 = +1 stop.
- Aperture: f/2 → f/2.8 = –1 stop (smaller hole). Full f-stop progression: f/1.4, f/2, f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8, f/11, f/16, f/22.
- Shutter: 1/60 → 1/125 = –1 stop. 1/60 → 1/30 = +1 stop.
- All three measured in stops — so trades are direct: "–1 stop aperture, +1 stop shutter" = same exposure.

## ISO

### What it does
- Amplifies the sensor signal. Higher ISO → brighter exposure but more noise.
- Modern full-frame sensors (2020+): clean to ~ISO 3200–6400. Usable to ~12800. Noisy past 25600.
- Crop sensors: clean to ~ISO 800–1600. Usable to ~3200. Grainy past that.
- Phone sensors: usable to ~ISO 800 typically; computational stacking helps.

### Typical ranges
- **ISO 100** (base): cleanest image, max dynamic range. Bright light, tripod shots.
- **ISO 400**: everyday default, still clean on modern sensors.
- **ISO 1600**: indoor / overcast, slight grain, totally acceptable.
- **ISO 3200–6400**: low-light / night / concerts, noticeable grain, handle with noise reduction.
- **ISO 12800+**: emergency. Usable if grain is okay stylistically (BW reportage, concert photography).

### Noise character
- Luminance noise: speckled brightness variation. Sharp, fine grain. Often looks organic.
- Chrominance noise: colored speckles (red/green). Ugly. Noise-reduction removes it first.
- Shadow noise worse than highlight noise — shadows get pushed more in post.
- Grain (film) vs digital noise: film grain is tonal, soft; digital noise is pixel-level, hard. That's why film "looks better" at ISO 800.

### Rule of thumb
- Always start at base ISO (usually 100). Raise only when aperture and shutter can't give you correct exposure.
- Exception: shooting moving subjects in low light — raise ISO to enable faster shutter speeds.

## Aperture (f-stop)

### What it does
- Size of lens opening. Smaller number = bigger hole = more light = shallower depth of field.
- f/1.4 = huge opening (fast lens, great low light, razor-thin DOF)
- f/16 = tiny opening (deep DOF, diffraction kicks in)

### Full-stop progression
f/1.0, f/1.4, f/2, f/2.8, f/4, f/5.6, f/8, f/11, f/16, f/22, f/32. Each step doubles/halves light. Memorize.

### Depth of field (DOF)
- **Shallower DOF** (f/1.4, f/2): isolated subject, blurred background. Portraits, bokeh, product isolation.
- **Deeper DOF** (f/11, f/16): everything sharp. Landscapes, architecture, group shots.
- DOF also depends on: focal length (longer = shallower), focus distance (closer = shallower), sensor size (bigger sensor = shallower at same f-stop and framing).

### Sharpness sweet spot
- Most lenses are sharpest at **f/5.6 to f/8** (2–3 stops from wide open).
- Wide open (f/1.4): usable but slightly soft, some fringing.
- f/11: good sharpness, slight diffraction beginning.
- **f/16+**: diffraction softens the whole image. Use only when DOF demand overrides.

### Diffraction limit
- Crop sensor: diffraction bites at f/11.
- Full frame: at f/16.
- Medium format: at f/22.
- Past the limit, every image is softer regardless of focus accuracy.

### Creative DOF choices
- Portrait: f/1.8–f/2.8 (isolate subject, bokeh background)
- Environmental portrait: f/4–f/5.6 (subject + context)
- Event / documentary: f/2.8–f/4 (flexible, usable in varying light)
- Landscape: f/8–f/11 (full sharpness, deep DOF)
- Astrophotography: f/1.4–f/2.8 wide open (max light gathering)
- Product: f/8–f/11 for full product sharp; f/2.8–f/4 for isolation.

## Shutter speed

### What it does
- How long the shutter is open. Longer = more light + motion blur. Shorter = less light + frozen motion.

### Typical values
- **1/8000 s**: freezes bullets, sports, extreme action.
- **1/1000 s**: freezes fast sports, flight, water drops.
- **1/500 s**: freezes running, cars at moderate speed.
- **1/250 s**: everyday action, people walking.
- **1/125 s**: standard handheld daylight.
- **1/60 s**: minimum handheld with image stabilization; base indoor.
- **1/30 s**: handheld only steady-handed + IS; intentional slight motion.
- **1/4 s – 1 s**: blur, water silkening, need tripod.
- **Long exposure (1–30 s)**: night, light trails, star trails, architecture evening.
- **Bulb (>30 s)**: astro, extremely dim scenes, fireworks.

### Handheld reciprocal rule
- Minimum shutter speed handheld (no stabilization) = 1 / focal length (full-frame equivalent).
- 50mm lens → at least 1/50 s.
- 200mm lens → at least 1/200 s.
- With image stabilization, can often go 2–4 stops slower.

### Creative motion blur
- Running water silk: 1/4 s–2 s.
- Light trails (cars at night): 15 s–30 s.
- Panning shots (follow subject, blurred background): 1/30–1/60 s, match speed.
- Star trails: 10 min+ (use intervalometer or built-in).
- Freeze dance / sport: 1/1000 s min.

### Sync speed (flash)
- Max shutter speed that works with standard flash: typically 1/200 s or 1/250 s.
- Faster requires high-speed sync (HSS) mode on flashes.
- Shoot past sync speed without HSS = black band on the frame.

## Balancing the triangle

Correct exposure = correct combination. If you change one, compensate with another.

**Example**: you're at ISO 400, f/5.6, 1/250 s (good exposure).
- Want shallower DOF → f/2.8 (+2 stops). Compensate: 1/1000 s (–2 stops). Same exposure.
- Want freeze action → 1/2000 s (–3 stops from 1/250). Compensate: ISO 3200 (+3 stops). Same exposure.

Decide priority first: DOF? Motion? Noise? Then pick the value to optimize, balance the other two.

## Exposure compensation

- +/- EV (exposure value) adjustment in semi-auto modes.
- +1 EV = one stop brighter. –1 EV = one stop darker.
- Use when subject trips the meter: snow scene (meter underexposes, +1 to +2 EV); dark subject (meter overexposes, –1 EV).

## Histogram reading

- X-axis: tonal values, black (left) to white (right).
- Y-axis: count of pixels at that value.
- **Clipped shadows** (spike at far left, mashed against left wall) = lost detail, pure black.
- **Clipped highlights** (spike at far right) = blown out, pure white, unrecoverable.
- Ideal: distribution across range, falling gracefully on both ends, no walls.
- **Expose to the right (ETTR)**: for RAW, pushing exposure right without clipping maximizes dynamic range. Pull down in post.

## Metering modes

- **Matrix / evaluative** (default): camera reads whole frame, averages intelligently. Good for most cases.
- **Center-weighted**: prioritizes center ~60% of frame. Good for portraits where subject is centered.
- **Spot**: tiny circle (~2%) — reads exact point. Used for high-contrast scenes, backlit subjects.
- **Highlight-weighted**: newer; meters to protect highlights. Stage lighting, spot-lit subjects.

## Zone system (Ansel Adams)

11 zones (0–X), each one stop apart. Black to white.

- **Zone 0**: pure black (no detail). Deepest shadow.
- **Zone II**: deep shadow, textured but dark.
- **Zone III**: textured shadow (dark clothing, shaded rocks).
- **Zone V**: mid-grey. 18% reflectance. This is what your meter wants.
- **Zone VII**: fair skin in sunlight.
- **Zone VIII**: textured whites (snow in sun, white cotton shirt).
- **Zone IX**: near white, barely detail.
- **Zone X**: pure white, blown out.

**Usage**: meter the scene; decide which zone the key subject belongs in; expose to place it there. "Expose for the shadows, develop for the highlights" (film era); "expose for the highlights" (digital — blown pixels are unrecoverable).

## Expose for highlights vs shadows

- **Digital RAW**: expose for highlights. Shadows can be lifted; highlights once clipped are gone.
- **Film negative**: expose for shadows. Film holds highlight detail very well; shadow detail is what's at risk.
- **High dynamic range scene**: bracket 3+ exposures, merge.

## Reciprocity failure (film)

- Long exposures on film don't respond linearly. A 1-second metered exposure might need 2 seconds actual.
- Reciprocity failure starts around 1 s exposure, worsens at 10+ s.
- Different films have different curves. Velvia, Portra, Acros each have charts.
- Digital: no reciprocity failure; straight linear to sensor noise floor.

## Quick validation checklist

1. Base ISO unless low light or motion demands otherwise?
2. Aperture matched to subject — shallow for isolate, deep for context/landscape?
3. Shutter at reciprocal of focal length minimum for handheld?
4. Are you past the diffraction limit (f/16+ on FF)?
5. Histogram clipped on either end? Rebalance.
6. Metering mode correct for scene (matrix default; spot for high contrast)?
7. Exposure compensation applied for snow/dark subjects?
8. If long exposure on film — reciprocity correction calculated?
