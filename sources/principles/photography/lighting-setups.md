# Lighting Setups

*Named portrait and scene lighting patterns — studio, natural, cinematic. Each has a recognizable fingerprint and a specific emotional register.*

## 3-point lighting (foundation)

The canonical setup for video, portrait, product. Three sources:

- **Key light**: primary, brightest, dominant direction. Usually 30–45° off-axis from subject, slightly above eye level. Defines the form.
- **Fill light**: opposite side of key, softens shadow side. Weaker than key.
- **Rim / back light**: behind subject, aimed at back/shoulders/hair. Separates subject from background.

### Lighting ratios (key : fill)
- **1:1** (fill = key): flat, commercial, cosmetic. Zero drama.
- **2:1**: gentle modeling, daytime feel.
- **3:1**: classic portrait. Good dimension, readable shadow side.
- **4:1**: moody, cinematic, shadow side dark but still detailed.
- **8:1**: dramatic, low-key, Rembrandt territory.
- **16:1+**: near-chiaroscuro, shadow side almost black.

### Placement cheat-sheet
- Key: 30–45° side + slightly above
- Fill: opposite side, same height or slightly below, 1–4 stops less than key
- Rim: directly behind or 3/4 behind, aimed down at shoulders/head. Often 1–2 stops brighter than key (to cut through).

## Named portrait lighting patterns

### Rembrandt lighting
- **Fingerprint**: triangle of light on the shadow-side cheek — below the eye, next to the nose.
- Key light at **45° side + 45° above** eye level.
- Nose shadow connects to cheek shadow but leaves a triangle lit.
- Works best on subjects with defined cheekbones / bridge.
- Register: classical, painterly, dignified, intimate.
- Named for Rembrandt van Rijn.

### Butterfly / Paramount lighting
- **Fingerprint**: small butterfly-shaped shadow directly under the nose.
- Key light centered above camera, aimed down at ~45°.
- Often paired with a reflector below to lift chin shadow.
- Register: glamour, Hollywood 1940s, cosmetics, high-fashion.
- Flattering on symmetric faces; exposes nose length and under-eye circles if too high.

### Split lighting
- **Fingerprint**: exactly half the face lit, half in shadow. Vertical split down the nose.
- Key light at **90° side** (directly sideways) to subject.
- No fill, or very minimal fill.
- Register: dramatic, mysterious, menacing, androgynous.
- Good for high-contrast editorial, character portraits.

### Loop lighting
- **Fingerprint**: small shadow loop from nose extending to cheek but NOT touching it.
- Key light at **30–45° side + slightly above** eye level (bit lower than Rembrandt).
- Most common portrait lighting — flattering on most face shapes.
- Register: natural, friendly, "default" portrait.

### Short lighting
- Key light on the side of face AWAY from camera (far side lit, near side shadowed).
- Slims the face. Good for round faces and wider subjects.
- Used with Rembrandt/loop/split to make choice.

### Broad lighting
- Key light on the side of face TOWARD camera (near side lit, far side shadowed).
- Broadens the face. Good for very thin faces.
- Less flattering on full-faced subjects (makes them appear wider).

### Ring light
- Light wraps around lens axis (circular).
- **Fingerprint**: flat even lighting, circular catchlight in each eye, shadow halo behind subject on nearby surface.
- Register: beauty / cosmetics / YouTube / vloggy. Flat, youthful, uncritical.
- Signature: everyone knows a ring-light photo when they see it.

### Clamshell lighting
- Key above + reflector or secondary below, both pointing at subject face.
- Fills everything, minimal shadow, double catchlight (eye + lower).
- Beauty and cosmetic standard — shows every pore = perfect skin tests.

## Natural light

### Window light
- **North-facing window** (northern hemisphere): soft indirect daylight all day. Preferred for portraits.
- **South-facing**: direct sun part of day, harsh. Can diffuse with curtain.
- East: soft morning, direct afternoon. West: reverse.
- **Softness = window size / distance**. Big window close = soft. Small window far = hard.
- Fall off: ~1 stop per meter away from window. Dramatic chiaroscuro possible with subject 1–2m from window, rest of room dark.
- Painters knew this — Vermeer's entire output is left-side window light.

### Golden hour
- **Time**: first hour after sunrise, last hour before sunset.
- **Character**: warm (#FFC080 to #FF9050 color temp ~2500–3500K), low-angle, soft directional.
- Shadows: long, soft-edged.
- Contrast: moderate — key stops to shadow ~2–3 stops.
- Backlit subjects get glowing rim.
- **Why everyone shoots here**: flattering to skin (warm wash), dramatic angle, cinematic.

### Blue hour
- **Time**: 30 min before sunrise / after sunset.
- **Character**: cool ambient (#7090B8 to #40607A, ~6500–10000K), directionless, soft.
- No hard shadows. Practical lights (windows, street lamps) pop warm against cool sky.
- Register: moody, urban, cinematic, melancholy.
- Short window — plan ahead, move fast.

### Overcast
- **Character**: giant softbox (cloud cover = diffuser). Flat, even, cool.
- No hard shadow. Color temp cool (~6500–7500K).
- Low contrast. Skin looks even, slightly flat.
- Best light for: detail / product / texture shots. Nature photography when texture matters more than mood.
- Weakness: no modeling, subjects look dimensionless. Add rim from flash for dimension.

### Direct midday sun
- **Character**: hard overhead, short shadows, high contrast.
- Under-eye shadow ("raccoon eyes"), harsh nose shadow.
- Color temp ~5500K (daylight neutral).
- Generally avoided for portraits. Works for: heroic landscape, documentary, architecture.
- Mitigate with: diffuser overhead, find shade, use fill flash to lift eye sockets, shoot backlit with key from rim.

## Modifiers

### Softbox
- Rectangular diffusion panel. Makes flash/strobe look like a window.
- Larger softbox = softer light.
- Beauty: 4-foot octagonal. Product: 2-foot strip.
- Gridded softbox: controls spill (keeps light from wrapping behind subject).

### Umbrella
- **Shoot-through (white/translucent)**: light fires through, soft. Cheaper, spills everywhere.
- **Reflective (white/silver inner)**: light bounces back. Directional, more control, more specular.
- Large umbrella = softer. 43-inch standard.
- Silver interior = harder/more specular than white.

### Beauty dish
- Large round shallow dish with center deflector. Creates a slightly harder / more specular soft light.
- Signature: crisp rim on jawline, saturated skin tones, pronounced catchlight.
- Beauty and cosmetic staple.

### Scrim
- Translucent fabric panel between light and subject. Turns hard source soft.
- Used to diffuse midday sun on location.

### Flag
- Opaque panel. **Blocks** light from hitting specific area.
- Uses: block lens flare, shadow one side of subject, control spill onto background.

### Gobo
- "Goes between object" — any shape-cut flag creating patterned shadow.
- Uses: fake window blinds pattern, tree shadow, graphic projection.
- In cinema, also called a "cookie" (cucoloris).

### Grid (honeycomb)
- Attached to softbox/dish/strobe. Narrows the beam, reduces spill.
- Tight grids (20°) = spot-like; wide grids (40°) = gentle control.
- Critical for avoiding light spill on backgrounds.

## Color gels

Colored filters over lights to shift color temperature or add creative hue.

### CTO / CTB (conversion)
- **CTO** (color temperature orange): warms flash (5500K) to tungsten (3200K). Match mixed light environment.
- **CTB** (color temperature blue): cools tungsten to daylight.
- **Quarter / half / full** strengths: 250K / 1500K / 2300K shifts approximately.

### Creative color gels
- Magenta / green: complementary clash (Marvel movie lighting, hip-hop editorial).
- Red rim + blue key: cinematic sci-fi / noir.
- Amber fill + cyan rim: 80s neon aesthetic (Stranger Things, Drive).
- Green practical + orange key: cyberpunk, matrix, hacker film.

## Mixing flash with ambient

- Flash adds burst; ambient = existing light.
- **Ambient-dominant**: long shutter (1/60 s+), low flash power. Flash accents, ambient exposes. Common at receptions, golden hour fill.
- **Flash-dominant**: fast shutter (at sync speed), high flash power. Flash freezes motion, ambient recedes. Fashion on location, strobist stuff.
- Sync speed cap (1/200–1/250 s): past this without HSS, black band appears.
- Color: outdoor fill flash usually needs a 1/4 CTO gel to match late sun warmth.

## Chiaroscuro in photography

- Single hard key + minimal fill (key : fill 16:1 or wider).
- Deep shadows, selective bright lights.
- Black negative fill (black flag/panel on shadow side) sucks light OUT — amplifies contrast.
- Reference: Peter Lindbergh black-and-white portraits, Paolo Roversi, Platon closeups.

## Cinema lighting concepts

### Motivated light
- Every light in frame should appear to come from a logical source in the scene (window, lamp, fire, sun).
- Key motivated from a visible or implied source maintains believability.
- Even stylized lighting should imply motivation.

### Practical light
- A light visible in the shot (lamp, candle, computer screen, neon sign). Doubles as set dressing + illumination.
- Classic noir: single practical overhead bulb = source + prop.
- Use practicals to anchor the eye and justify light direction.

### Eye light
- Small source (tiny reflector, dedicated light) placed specifically to create a catchlight in the subject's eyes.
- Without catchlight, eyes go dead.
- Standard portrait / cinema requirement. Double catchlight = twin sources (common with ring lights, umbrella+fill).

### Rembrandt lighting + practical + motivated = cinema noir
Common noir stack: practical bulb overhead (motivated) + Rembrandt triangle on face + hard shadow from venetian blind gobo.

## Quick validation checklist

1. Key direction identified? Is it motivated by a source in-scene?
2. Key-to-fill ratio selected for emotional register (2:1 light, 8:1 dramatic)?
3. Catchlight visible in the eyes?
4. Rim / back light separating subject from background?
5. Source size matched to desired shadow hardness?
6. For outdoor: avoided harsh midday direct sun? Or embraced it deliberately?
7. Color temperature mixing intentional (warm key + cool ambient)?
8. Gobos / flags / grids controlling spill where needed?
