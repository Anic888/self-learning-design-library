# Bienal Internacional del Cartel en México (BICM) — 17th & 18th editions

*Latin America's flagship poster competition — a biennial survey of socially-engaged, commissioned-or-conscience poster design, judged in Mexico and drawing entries from across the Americas, Europe, and Asia.*

## Context

The Bienal Internacional del Cartel en México (International Poster Biennial in Mexico) was founded in 1990 by the Trama Visual organization and a circle of Mexican poster designers led by Germán Montalvo, Xavier Bermúdez, and colleagues to position Mexico City as a permanent venue for the international poster conversation. It became a full member of the International Council of Design (ico-D, now The Icod) and now runs every two years from the Franz Mayer Museum and the Arocena Museum, with exhibitions touring afterward.

The competition is structured around themed social categories rather than discipline categories: each edition, each category is commissioned by a partner institution (UN agencies, Mexico's Secretaría de Cultura, Amnesty International, COFECE, Taiwan's Design Research Institute, etc.) around a specific issue — migration, anti-corruption, sustainability, public health. This gives the BICM a very different character from purely craft-led competitions like D&AD or Tokyo TDC: it rewards posters that say something, not just posters that look good. The biennial's permanent archive — held at carteles.bienalcartel.org — now exceeds 50,000 posters and is a research corpus in its own right.

Inside the global poster-competition hierarchy, BICM sits alongside the Warsaw Poster Biennial, Chaumont, the Trnava Poster Triennial, and the Lahti Poster Biennial as one of the five or six "majors" — with Warsaw being the oldest, Chaumont the most critically watched, and BICM the most politically engaged.

## Notable designers across recent editions

- **Jianping He** — Berlin-based, repeat BICM laureate. Minimal monochrome posters with hand-cut photographic imagery.
- **Annik Troxler** — Swiss heir to the Basel poster school; type + photographic collage for cultural clients.
- **Christoph Stettler** — Swiss; precise geometric poster-making with strong concept-first framing.
- **Neue Gestaltung** — Berlin studio with a characteristic flat, reductive, sans-serif approach.
- **Jun Cai** — Chinese designer who repeatedly wins across the Asia biennial circuit for brush-and-ink / photographic hybrids.
- **Javier Esperanza Jofré** — Chilean designer, often handling complex text-heavy political work.

## Visual patterns observed

1. **Issue-first composition.** Every winning poster reads as a position, not as ornament. The viewer should know the designer's stance within 1.5 seconds. Ambiguity is rare.
2. **High-contrast duotone palettes.** Black + one scream-color (red, yellow, magenta, UNEP-green) dominates. Full photographic color is much rarer than at D&AD or Tokyo TDC.
3. **Hand + photo + type fusion.** The "BICM poster" aesthetic tends to combine a hand-drawn or painted element, a photographic fragment, and a typographic statement — rather than sitting in purely one mode.
4. **Typography as protest.** Condensed sans, hand lettering, and degraded/distressed typography carry emotional weight; precious Swiss-grid type is less common than at European competitions.
5. **Central single-image hit.** Unlike NY/Tokyo TDC, which reward systems, BICM consistently rewards the single striking visual metaphor — one strong image per poster.
6. **Latin American + East Asian + Mid-European axis.** Winning countries cluster heavily around Mexico / Chile / Argentina, China / Taiwan / Japan, and Switzerland / Poland / Germany / France. US designers are under-represented.
7. **Vertical 70×100 canvas.** Most winning posters respect the European portrait poster canvas; square and landscape formats are rare and usually category-mandated.
8. **Quiet typography paired with loud imagery.** A common combination: a restrained, small-scale sans caption below or beside a dominant, emotionally-charged image.

## What this competition teaches the agent

When asked to make work "in the spirit of BICM":

- **Start from the issue, not the brief.** The designer must take a legible position on a specific social or political theme (migration, climate, corruption, public health, etc.).
- **Work the single-image metaphor.** One strong, central visual idea. Collage, simplify, or illustrate — but resolve the meaning into one image.
- **Restrict color to 2–3 inks.** Black + one red/yellow/green + optionally one mid-tone. Treat full color as a deliberate choice, not a default.
- **Portrait 70×100 poster format.** Design for wheat-paste on a Mexico City wall, not for Instagram first.
- **Let the type be auxiliary.** A single line, possibly two — small scale relative to the image. If the poster leads with typography, the typography itself must be the image (distressed, painted, cut).
- **Favor non-Western imagery traditions.** Mexican muralism, Polish poster school, Cuban film-poster vocabulary, Chinese brush work — all are fair game. The aesthetic is international but explicitly non-American.
- **Avoid prettiness as the goal.** BICM rewards posters that have teeth. A beautiful poster that says nothing will not place.
