# Brno International Biennial of Graphic Design, 28th Edition (2018) — last in-person edition

*Central Europe's flagship survey of contemporary graphic design — broader than poster-only, honoring books, typography, identity, and digital alongside posters. Its last full edition was 2018; the 29th (planned 2020/2022) was cancelled and the biennial shifted to a digital-platform model.*

## Context

Founded in 1964 by the Moravian Gallery (Moravská galerie) in Brno, Czechoslovakia, as the "First Czechoslovak Exhibition of Poster and Promotional Graphics," the Brno Biennial became fully international from 1966 onward. For nearly 60 years it operated as one of the world's most significant graphic-design events, alternating its focus across two biennial themes: (1) poster, corporate identity, information and promotional graphics, and (2) graphic design and typography in books, magazines, newspapers, and digital media.

Brno's distinction relative to its peer biennials (Warsaw, Chaumont, Lahti) is its breadth: it is not poster-first. The biennial honors the full discipline of graphic design. Past exhibitors, jurors, and lecturers have included Saul Bass, Roman Cieślewicz, Wim Crouwel, Alan Fletcher, Adrian Frutiger, Milton Glaser, and Herb Lubalin — making Brno historically one of the most influential gathering points for European graphic design. It sits alongside the Prague Quadrennial and Karlovy Vary Film Festival as a Czech cultural institution.

**Current status:** On April 24, 2022, the Moravian Gallery announced it would abandon the regular in-person biennial exhibitions and replace them with a "sustainable permanent digital platform." The 29th edition (already postponed from 2020) was cancelled, partly due to the economic impact of the war in Ukraine. A petition from the design community argued a digital-only platform cannot replicate the biennial's value. **There was no 2024 edition.** The last in-person edition was therefore the 28th (2018).

## This edition — 28th Biennial (2018)

- **Year:** 2018
- **Dates:** May 10 – August 26, 2018
- **Venue:** Moravian Gallery, Brno
- **Curatorial direction:** OKOLO creative group — Matěj Činčera, Jan Kloss, Adam Štěch
- **Curatorial intent:** "Open a democratic platform to represent the complete scope of the discipline" — present contemporary graphic design across diverse opinions and creative approaches, rather than narrow it to a style
- **Jury:**
  - Lars Harmsen (Germany, editor of *Slanted* magazine)
  - Omar Sosa (Spain/USA, art director of *Apartamento*)
  - Jiří Karásek (Czech Republic, Marvil studio)
  - Steven Watson (UK, founder of *Stack*)
  - Pavla Pauknerová (Czech Republic, design theorist)
  - + OKOLO curatorial team (Činčera, Kloss, Štěch)
- **Eligibility:** Works designed between 1 January 2015 and 31 October 2017. Maximum three entries per participant. No fee.
- **Categories:** Corporate identity, book editions, magazines, posters, type design, informational design, digital media, packaging design
- **Participating countries:** 30+ including Germany, Switzerland, USA, Japan, Czech Republic, China, UK, Netherlands, France

## Notable winners — 28th Biennial (2018)

- **Grand Prix of the 28th International Biennial of Graphic Design 2018 — Award of the Minister of Culture of the Czech Republic**
  - **OK-RM** (Oliver Knight & Rory McGrath, UK)
  - London-based studio known for restrained typographic systems, serious editorial/art-book work, and austere identity design. Typical OK-RM: Akzidenz-Grotesk-or-similar grotesque, black-and-one-color systems, generous white space, art-institutional clients.
  - Visual analysis of their canon: this award signals Brno's 2018 preference for editorial/systems-thinking graphic design over expressive-poster work — reinforcing Brno's identity as broader than a poster biennial.

- **Mayor of Brno Award (designers under 35) — Florian Lamm (Germany)**
  - Berlin-based designer; known for book design, editorial, and typography in the German/Dutch reductive tradition.

- **Czech Centres Award (designers under 30) — Petra Dočekalová (Czech Republic)**
  - Czech type designer/hand-lettering specialist known for lettering with Czech vernacular influence; associated with TypeTogether and the Czech lettering revival.

- **International Jury Award — Jakob Kirch, Markus Dreßen, Jan Wenzel, Katharina Köhler (Germany)**
  - Leipzig/Berlin axis, editorial-design lineage (Dreßen long associated with Spector Books).

- **Visitors' Award — Erich Brechbühl (Switzerland)**
  - Lucerne-based poster designer, Mixer studio. Part of the current Swiss poster tradition — heavy type, flat color, generous negative space, frequently theatre/music posters.

### Brno Biennial Laureates (Lifetime Achievement)

- **2014 Laureate: Aleš Najbrt (Czech Republic)**
  - "The most famous Czech graphic designer" — film and theatre posters, *Reflex* magazine design, corporate identity for Prague and Ostrava, 20+ year collaboration with Karlovy Vary film festival. Exhibition curator: Michal Nanoru.
- **2016 Laureate: František Štorm (Czech Republic)**
  - Type-design legend; Storm Type Foundry founder; specializes in typeface design and its application to music packaging. Exhibition curator: Vladimir 518.

## Visual patterns observed in 28th Biennial winners

- **Editorial/systems dominance over expressive posters.** OK-RM's Grand Prix confirmed Brno 2018 as a biennial for disciplined editorial design, not for expressive poster-art. Winners worked in art-book systems, identities, and typography.
- **Restrained, grotesque-led typography.** Akzidenz-Grotesk, Neue Haas Grotesk, modernist grotesques. Minimal type-pairing. No display-type pyrotechnics.
- **Black-plus-one-color palettes.** Winners cluster around black + an accent (often Pantone red or a neutral warm color), with extensive white space.
- **Book and editorial clients dominate.** Art institutions, publishers, museums, cultural magazines — not commercial advertising.
- **Central European aesthetic backbone.** German, Swiss, Czech, UK-on-European-lineage designers took most top prizes. Very little US-commercial work at the podium.
- **Hand-lettering as a distinct current.** The under-30 award (Dočekalová) points to Czech vernacular lettering and hand-drawn type as a parallel track recognized by the biennial.
- **Swiss poster tradition stays visible in the Visitors' Award.** Brechbühl's recognition confirms Brno still values the flat-color, flat-type Lucerne poster school — just not as the top prize.
- **Long-form evaluation window (2+ years of eligible work).** Unlike Warsaw or Lahti, Brno looked at 2015-2017 output — rewarding sustained practice, not single strong posters.

## What this competition teaches the agent

When asked to create work "in the spirit of Brno Biennial winners" — follow these rules:

### Palette conventions
- Black + one accent (Pantone red, forest green, ochre, or warm neutral)
- Uncoated paper aesthetics — matte, not glossy
- Generous white / paper-stock space
- Avoid vibrant gradients; stay flat or subtly textured

### Compositional conventions
- Systems-thinking, not poster-thinking — this is a book, identity, or editorial context
- Grid-respectful but not grid-locked
- Generous margins; work must "breathe"
- Asymmetric modernist layouts over centered poster compositions
- Cover/spread/page-series thinking, not single-image thinking

### Subject-matter preferences
- Art institutions, museums, publishers
- Cultural magazines, architectural monographs, artist books
- Typography releases, specimen books
- Avoid commercial brand work; prefer editorial/cultural clients

### Typography attitudes
- Grotesque sans-serifs (Akzidenz-Grotesk, Neue Haas Grotesk, Theinhardt, GT America, FK Grotesk)
- Editorial serifs for long-form (Plantin, Lyon, Larish Neue, GT Sectra)
- Minimal type-pairing (1-2 families)
- Hand-lettering as an accepted parallel track (Czech vernacular influence)
- Disciplined, systematic type use; not expressive type as decoration

### What NOT to do (anti-patterns)
- Do not design a single expressive poster and call it "Brno style" — Brno rewards systems, books, identity, editorial
- Do not layer, maximalize, or use Y2K/grunge/collage aesthetics
- Do not use display type as the emotional center — the grid and the system are the emotional center
- Do not reference US-commercial design (big-box brand, SaaS marketing) as a base style
- Do not use glossy/polished 3D rendering, photographic compositing, or digital-first expressive work
- Do not treat the brief as "decoration" — Brno is disciplinary-craft-oriented

## Note on recency

This file documents the 28th Biennial (2018) because the 29th was cancelled and no 2024 edition occurred. The agent should treat "Brno 2018" as the canonical reference until the Moravian Gallery resumes in-person biennials or until the announced digital platform produces an equivalent juried result set.
