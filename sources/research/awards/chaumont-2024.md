# Chaumont International Poster & Graphic Design Festival / Biennale (5th Biennale, 2025)

*France's flagship graphic design event — where the Swiss-French axis of contemporary poster design sets the canon for cultural posters, typography, and image-making.*

## Context

The Chaumont Poster Festival (Festival international de l'affiche et du graphisme de Chaumont) was founded in 1990 by Cyril de Rouvre (then Mayor of Chaumont, 1989-1995) and Thierry Simon in Chaumont, a small town in the Haute-Marne département of northeastern France. The festival was born from the town's existing poster collection — Chaumont inherited the personal poster archive of the Dutailly family, one of the richest municipal poster collections in France, which provided the historical backbone for the competition.

Alain Weill served as the first artistic director (1990-2001). The festival originally ran annually, establishing itself as a leading international poster competition endorsed by Icograda (now ico-D / International Council of Design). In 2017 it restructured: Le Signe — centre national du graphisme opened as France's first permanent national institution for graphic design, and the festival became a biennial organized by Le Signe. The current event is the **5th Biennale Internationale de Design Graphique (2025)**, which incorporated the **31st International Poster Competition** — meaning the poster-competition lineage is continuous from 1990.

Chaumont's distinct place in the design-awards hierarchy: it is the poster competition of the French-Swiss design establishment (Helmo, Fanette Mellier, M/M Paris, Ralph Schraivogel, Niklaus Troxler all won here). The jury historically rewards cultural posters for theatres, festivals, and institutions — work that prioritizes concept, typography, and silk-screen / risograph / commercial-print surface. It is the most "continental European design-school" of the big four biennials (with Brno being more editorial/systems, Warsaw more painterly-Polish, and Lahti more thematic-environmental).

## Notable winners — Chaumont lineage (historically verified)

Because 2025 per-poster winners are not publicly indexed, the canon is best grounded in the festival's recent verified editions:

### 26th Edition (2015, last pre-biennale annual festival)

- **1st Prize — Helmo (Thomas Couderc & Clément Vauchez, France)** — confirmed via 25th edition (2014) results in ICOD archive. Helmo is the definitive Chaumont-winning studio: Reims-based partnership working across cultural identities, posters (Jazz sous les pommiers festival, Fflag, La Villette), exhibition design, and book design. Layered screen-printed posters with displaced registration as intentional aesthetic.
- **2nd Prize (25th) — Evelyn Ter Bekke & Dirk Behage / Atelier ter Bekke & Behage (Netherlands)** — Paris-based Dutch studio, cultural client focus.
- **3rd Prize (25th) — Kim Do-hyung (South Korea)** — single-image conceptual posters; his earlier Icograda Excellence Award (2009) was for *"You Are Under CCTV."*
- **Icograda Excellence Award (25th) — Mathias Schweizer (Switzerland)**.
- **Student Competition 1st (25th) — My-Lan Hoang-Thuy (France)**.

### 21st Edition (2010)

- **1st Prize — Ralph Schraivogel (Switzerland)** — Zurich-based poster master; Cinema Kino and Museum Rietberg poster series canonical. Layered, halftone, typography-as-image.
- **2nd Prize — Fanette Mellier (France)** — Strasbourg-trained designer, now Paris; commissions include the Centre Pompidou and Fondation Louis Vuitton. Bold color, risograph aesthetic, typographic wit.
- **3rd Prize — Mikus Belousov**.
- **Icograda Excellence Award — Niklaus Troxler (Switzerland)** — Willisau Jazz Festival posters — hand-drawn jazz-expressionism, canonical.
- **Student 1st — Geoffroy Pithon (Ensad Paris)**.

### 17th Edition (2006)

- **1st Prize — Andrey Logvin (Russia)** — *"Museum Night Taste Of Life"* (2005). Large-type conceptual poster; blends Cyrillic with pictorial minimalism.
- **2nd Prize — Harmen Liemburg (Netherlands)** — *"Fall Lecture Series"* (2005). Silk-screened, Dutch-poster-school color layering.
- **3rd Prize — M/M Paris (Michael Amzalag & Mathias Augustyniak, France)** — *"Los Angeles"* (2005). Cultural fashion/editorial lineage.
- **Icograda Excellence Award — Stefan Sagmeister (USA)** — *"Chaumont Sagmeister"* (2004-2005).
- **Jury (2006):** Shigeo Fukuda (Japan), Anette Lenz (France), Rico Lins (Brazil), Alice Twemlow (USA), Oleg Veklenko (Ukraine).
- **Competition scale:** 1,800+ entries from 46 countries; 122 posters in final selection.

## Visual patterns observed in Chaumont winners (historical canon)

- **Screen-printed / risograph / silk-screen aesthetic reigns.** Posters won here have visible ink layering, registration imperfections treated as feature, and flat-overprint color mixing. Digital "clean vector" aesthetics rarely win at Chaumont.
- **Saturated, high-contrast flat color — often 3-5 color palette.** Not muted earth tones (that's Warsaw). Chaumont winners use bold reds, yellows, blues, greens. Color is celebratory, print-native.
- **Typography as image, not just text.** Schraivogel, Mellier, Helmo — they treat letterforms as formal elements. Large, oversized, often custom, sometimes glitched, often layered.
- **Cultural-institution subject matter.** Theatres, jazz festivals, museums, film institutions, book fairs. Commercial brand work almost never medals here.
- **French-Swiss axis dominates the podium.** French studios (Helmo, M/M Paris, Mellier, ter Bekke & Behage) and Swiss poster designers (Schraivogel, Troxler) constitute the recurring canon. Dutch and Japanese represented in the second tier.
- **Print-first thinking.** These are physical objects — paper stock, ink behavior, folds, poster sizes — not screen-optimized compositions.
- **Hand-drawn letterforms welcomed (Troxler, Helmo).** Expressive lettering is accepted alongside typographic rigor.
- **Layered visual logic — foreground/background often overlap, bleed, interlock.** Flat-graphic-but-compositional-depth is a Chaumont signature.

## What this competition teaches the agent

When asked to create work "in the spirit of Chaumont Biennale winners" — follow these rules:

### Palette conventions
- 3-5 flat, saturated colors — not muted, not pastel
- Color must behave like print inks — overprint logic is welcomed
- High contrast between adjacent fields
- Risograph palette as reference: fluorescent pink, warm red, cobalt blue, bright yellow, teal, forest green
- Avoid gradients unless they are screen-printed-style stepped gradients

### Compositional conventions
- Layered but structured — overprint two or three visual planes
- Typography is the structural backbone (often oversized)
- Letterforms can be images and images can be letterforms
- Asymmetric, grid-informed but not grid-bound
- Registration-mismatch-as-intent is accepted
- Print-first sensibility — think silk-screen layers, not Figma composites

### Subject-matter preferences
- Cultural festivals (jazz, theatre, cinema, literature)
- Museums, galleries, cultural institutions
- Publishers, book fairs, artist monographs
- Music (the 2025 biennial theme) — concerts, albums, festivals
- Avoid corporate/commercial brand work; avoid tech/SaaS; avoid generic "ad"

### Typography attitudes
- Large, confident, structural
- Grotesque sans (Akzidenz-Grotesk, Theinhardt, Suisse, GT, NaN) or custom display
- Hand-drawn lettering welcomed (Troxler jazz tradition)
- Variable/distorted/stacked type as image-object
- Avoid timid, small, "transparent" typography — Chaumont rewards presence

### What NOT to do (anti-patterns)
- Do not create a polished digital illustration poster — Chaumont is print-native
- Do not rely on photography unless screen-printed or halftoned
- Do not use corporate clean-sans + whitespace minimalism (that's Brno or Swiss corporate, not Chaumont's cultural-expressive pole)
- Do not use generic SaaS/product-marketing aesthetics
- Do not design for the phone screen — these are street-scale cultural posters
- Do not avoid color — muted palettes lose here; boldness wins
- Do not treat type as afterthought decoration — type is the poster
