# AIGA 50 Books | 50 Covers 2024

*The longest-running book-design award in America — a juried selection of the 50 best-designed books and the 50 best-designed book covers published that year, chosen by working designers on craft grounds.*

## Context

AIGA (the American Institute of Graphic Arts, founded 1914) launched what was then called the "Fifty Books of the Year" competition in 1923. The 2023 edition marked the contest's centennial; the 2024 edition — the subject of this file — is the 101st. The award was briefly transferred to Design Observer in 2012 after AIGA restructured its competitions program, then returned to AIGA in 2020. It is explicitly a craft competition: books and book covers are judged on originality, typographic quality, the cohesion of the design system, and the piece's ability to draw the viewer in and hold up on a physical object.

In the global design-awards hierarchy, AIGA 50/50 is the US book-design equivalent of D&AD's Graphic Design and Book Design pencils, Germany's Stiftung Buchkunst "Schönste Bücher," or the Tokyo TDC Book Design Prize — but it is much more volume-driven (100 selections per year, not 5). Winners are archived permanently; past selections live in the AIGA Design Archives and the physical collection at Columbia University's Rare Book and Manuscript Library (formerly the Denver Art Museum, 1980–2012). The selection functions as the canonical annual survey of American and American-adjacent book design — editors and rights managers across US publishing read it as a scouting document.

Judging: five jurors (rotating annually), one of whom is named jury chair. Each juror reviews every entry independently, then the group deliberates. For the 2024 competition, jury chair duties went to **Scott Vander Zee** — a New York–based graphic and typeface designer whose own work is in editorial design, systems, and custom typography; he teaches at both Pratt Institute and Parsons.

## This edition (2024)

- **Entries**: 523 books and covers submitted by practitioners working in book design
- **Selections**: 100 total (50 Books + 50 Covers)
- **Jury chair**: Scott Vander Zee (NYC)
- **Eligibility window**: Designs published and used in the marketplace during 2024
- **Submission criteria (self-selected by jury)**: originality and quality; ability to draw attention and generate interest; creative distinction from the field; polish and professionalism of execution; cohesion of the system, typography, alignment, spacing, and readability

## Visual patterns observed

1. **Single-image-single-gesture covers.** A large fraction of recent winners resolves the cover into one graphic motif — a single object, a single mark, a single cut — sized to bleed or near-bleed. Multi-image collages rarely make the final 50.
2. **Type-carried covers are the second lane.** Where there is no image, the type does the entire job: precise custom lettering, historical display faces, or blackletter revivals. The type must be interesting on its own terms, not merely well-set.
3. **Literary-fiction cover palette skews dark + single hit.** Navy / black / deep green ground, one saturated accent (red, yellow, chartreuse). Pastel washes are rare in the fiction subset; more common in nonfiction and self-help.
4. **Nonfiction and memoir lean photographic + typographic hybrid.** The winning covers for essay collections and memoir often pair an unexpected archival photograph with serif display type.
5. **Book-interior winners favor non-standard trims.** Oversized monographs, square-format art books, and folio-form catalogs are over-represented in the 50 Books half of the award. Standard mass-market trims rarely win on the interior track.
6. **Systemic attention, not just cover art.** Winning book interiors are judged on running-head hierarchy, footnote treatment, sidebar logic, image captions, and back-matter — jurors explicitly cite cohesion of the system.
7. **Indie-press and university-press winners over-index.** Small presses (Futurepoem, Open Letter, Letterform Archive, Hat & Beard), art-book publishers (Delmonico, Prestel, Rizzoli Electa), and university presses consistently outperform their market share in the selections, relative to the big-five trade imprints.
8. **Minimal technique fetishism.** Unlike D&AD winners, AIGA 50/50 rarely rewards visible production pyrotechnics (foils, special materials, edge printing). Restraint wins over ornament.

## What this competition teaches the agent

When asked to make work "in the spirit of AIGA 50 Books | 50 Covers":

- **Design for the physical object.** The book is a thing in the hand, not a thumbnail. Think about trim size, paper stock, and the object-level gesture before the cover artwork.
- **Resolve the cover to one idea.** One mark, one image, or one typographic gesture. If two ideas are competing for attention, cut one.
- **Type must be interesting in itself.** Either pick display type with real editorial intelligence, or draw the letters. A generic sans at large scale will not cut through.
- **For fiction: dark ground + one accent.** Default to a deep-ground palette with a single, saturated accent. Pastel washes are a nonfiction move.
- **For interiors: design the system, not the spread.** Running heads, chapter openers, footnotes, captions, back matter — the jury looks at every part. The most neglected page (index, colophon) needs the same attention as the first spread.
- **Indie-press sensibility wins.** Small-press editorial ambition beats major-trade scale. Treat every book like an art book, even the genre fiction.
- **No pyrotechnics without purpose.** Foils, die cuts, and special inks should answer a content question. The AIGA jury punishes decoration.
- **US/UK/European market-aware.** The competition is American but international; favor work that reads equally well in Brooklyn, London, and Berlin bookstores. Avoid overtly region-locked cultural references unless the book is a regional title.
