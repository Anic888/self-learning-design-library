# 2020–2023 — AI Era Dawn & Pandemic Poster Catalog

*The narrow four-year window where three forces converged: generative AI broke out of research papers into art institutions, the pandemic forced public-health communication into a global design competition, and streaming platforms replaced the film studios as the cultural-poster industry. Canvas prints are optional now; pixels and AR are in the canon.*

## Context

These four years changed what a "poster" could be. AI models (DALL-E 2 in 2022, Midjourney v3-v4 in 2022, Stable Diffusion 1.4 in 2022) put image synthesis into every designer's toolkit, which Refik Anadol then legitimized at MoMA in late 2022. The pandemic — officially declared by WHO in March 2020 — forced every country to ship public-information posters under deadline pressure; the best ones (Italy, the UK, Brazil) passed into design-history anthologies almost immediately. Streaming platforms (Netflix Squid Game 2021, Stranger Things 4 in 2022) made title-card design the new movie-poster discipline, and Apple's sustained bento-grid keynote aesthetic — refined across the M1 event series (2020-2021) — reshaped landing-page design in enterprise software. The NFT bubble (2021 peak, Beeple's $69.3M sale) briefly made JPEGs sell at auction-house prices and put digital collage on Christie's walls.

## Canonical works

### Refik Anadol Studio — "Unsupervised — Machine Hallucinations — MoMA", 2022

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/calendar/exhibitions/5535
- **Source competition / publication**: Commissioned by The Museum of Modern Art, New York. Featured in AIGA Eye on Design, Wallpaper, Artforum.
- **Country / city**: USA / Los Angeles studio; installed New York (MoMA Gund lobby)
- **Medium**: Real-time generative AI video installation; also issued as 4096×4096 AI data paintings and as NFTs on FeralFile
- **Format**: 2160×3840 px, 30fps primary display; machine-learning model ran on 138,151 MoMA collection images via StyleGAN2 ADA (NVIDIA Research collaboration)
- **Palette**: #1A1A2E / #9B5DE5 / #F15BB5 / #FEE440 / #00BBF9 — slow-morphing "ethereal data pigments" chosen by latent-space density, not designer taste
- **Composition**: Single monumental screen, lobby scale (~24ft tall), occupying the entire wall opposite the entrance. No frame, no caption on the image itself. The eye lands everywhere because nothing holds; the piece was explicitly designed to reward peripheral looking and return visits.
- **Typography**: Exhibition wall text set in **MoMA's Work Sans / Sharp Grotesk** house pair. The work itself is textless — a deliberate curatorial choice.
- **Trend signals**: aurora-mesh, neo-futurism, dreamcore-liminal
- **What's fresh about it**: This is the moment AI imagery stopped being a lab demo and became a legitimate museum-scale medium. It proved a poster can be temporal — a wall that updates every second of its run.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Generative systems can be framed as atmosphere, not as illustration
  - Wall-scale saturation beats resolution if the movement is slow enough
  - When the machine is the author, the curator becomes the designer
- **Pair with designer profiles**: refik-anadol, beeple, peter-saville
- **Pair with briefs**: AI-product launches, museum identity, installation-scale digital posters

### Beeple (Mike Winkelmann) — "Everydays: The First 5000 Days", 2021

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Everydays:_the_First_5000_Days
- **Source competition / publication**: Christie's first standalone NFT auction (March 2021); sold for $69,346,250 to Metapurse
- **Country / city**: USA / Charleston, SC studio
- **Medium**: Digital collage as JPEG minted on Ethereum
- **Format**: 21,069 × 21,069 px — 5,000-tile mosaic of daily artworks made since May 1, 2007
- **Palette**: #FF0033 / #0FF0FC / #F7CA18 / #262626 / #FFFFFF — hyper-saturated with black ground; the overall impression is neon-on-ink
- **Composition**: Grid — 5,000 cells arranged chronologically. Early low-resolution drawings are near-invisible in the top-left; later 3D-rendered pieces dominate the bottom-right. The eye lands on whichever cell is most saturated, which is usually late-era political caricature.
- **Typography**: No typography within the work; presentation frame at Christie's used their house serif (a custom Sangbleu-variant).
- **Trend signals**: editorial-maximalism, glitch, neo-futurism
- **What's fresh about it**: The medium (NFT) became part of the art's meaning. This poster is also a certificate. The grid enforces the process claim — 5,000 days of unbroken work as the artistic gesture itself.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Practice made visible is a legitimate concept
  - Dense grids read at thumbnail as pattern, at arm's length as content; exploit both
  - The title doubles the claim: "5000 Days" is the concept, not a decorative label
- **Pair with designer profiles**: beeple, david-carson
- **Pair with briefs**: NFT launches, annual-report covers, long-practice retrospectives

### Italian public + grassroots response — "Andrà tutto bene" rainbow posters, 2020

- **URL**: https://www.afar.com/magazine/italians-cope-with-coronavirus-lockdown-with-uplifting-messages (press coverage) [verified by press coverage; no single attributed designer — distributed civic work]
- **Source competition / publication**: Emerged organically on Italian balconies March 2020; later archived by design museums including Triennale Milano
- **Country / city**: Italy — nationwide (Bergamo, Milan, Naples, Rome)
- **Medium**: Bed-sheet print / painted cardboard hung from balconies and windows; shareable digital versions proliferated on Instagram
- **Format**: Variable; most common is A0 painted sheet or horizontal sheet banner
- **Palette**: Rainbow — ROYGBIV + white ground — #E53935 / #FB8C00 / #FDD835 / #43A047 / #1E88E5 / #8E24AA
- **Composition**: Rainbow arc left-to-right across the top 60% of the composition; hand-painted script below: "Andrà tutto bene" ("Everything will be alright"). No framing, no logo, intentionally handmade.
- **Typography**: Hand-lettered cursive, child-legible; no professional typeface. The typographic argument was the lack of typography.
- **Trend signals**: anti-design-brutalism (through anti-polish), solarpunk
- **What's fresh about it**: The first genuinely global civic poster of the streaming era. It proved a distributed, unbranded, unpaid design could outperform state communications.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Handmade legibility beats professional slickness in trauma moments
  - A rainbow with no brand logic remains a universal visual primitive
  - A single-line message, set by a child's hand, scales to nation-state level
- **Pair with designer profiles**: paula-scher (for hand-set-type work), stefan-sagmeister
- **Pair with briefs**: civic campaigns, grassroots NGO identity, pandemic-response posters

### UK Government / NHS — "Stay Home. Protect the NHS. Save Lives.", 2020

- **URL**: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/coronavirus-stay-at-home-protect-the-nhs-save-lives-posters (archived PDFs)
- **Source competition / publication**: UK Government Communication Service; deployed nationally March 23, 2020; later discussed in Design Week retrospectives
- **Country / city**: UK / London
- **Medium**: Poster (bus stops, digital OOH, print press, social)
- **Format**: Adapted across formats; flagship A1 / 6-sheet
- **Palette**: #FFEB3B (warning yellow) / #000000 / #FFFFFF — specifically not NHS blue. The yellow carried the pandemic-as-warning argument.
- **Composition**: Full-bleed yellow ground. Three short lines, left-aligned, heavy weight: STAY HOME / PROTECT THE NHS / SAVE LIVES. Simple black Union-Jack-adjacent crest bottom-center. No imagery. The eye drops to the center, forced by typographic weight alone.
- **Typography**: GDS Transport (bespoke British government typeface, a Helvetica-adjacent grotesque) set in all caps. Weight strategy: uniform bold, ~280pt at A1, zero subordinate text above or between lines.
- **Trend signals**: variable-bold-typography, editorial-maximalism (through restraint)
- **What's fresh about it**: A wartime-poster language applied to a health crisis by a modern state with no nostalgia. The bright hazard-yellow proved designers were willing to break brand colors for urgency.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Three lines is the whole poster; cut to three before you design
  - Hazard-yellow reads as verb, not noun; it says "now"
  - When the state speaks, kill the hierarchy — make every word equal
- **Pair with designer profiles**: massimo-vignelli, paula-scher
- **Pair with briefs**: public-health warnings, evacuation notices, compliance posters

### Netflix / Hwang Dong-hyuk production team — "Squid Game" title key art, 2021

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squid_Game (studio credit attribution limited to Netflix's internal art direction with Korean designer Lee Sam-sik credited in press)
- **Source competition / publication**: Netflix global launch September 17, 2021; became most-watched series launch in platform history
- **Country / city**: South Korea / Seoul
- **Medium**: Digital poster, title card, out-of-home OOH extension
- **Format**: Vertical 27×40 key art adapted to digital horizontals and subway domination posters
- **Palette**: #E5A6B5 (pink) / #47A86C (schoolyard mint green) / #000000 / #FDEDEC (cream). Pink and mint are pulled from 1970s Korean school uniforms — a memory-palette, not a trend-palette.
- **Composition**: Title locked center-top; three primary symbols (○ △ ▢) arranged vertically. Body figures appear small and anonymous in red-pink and mint-green tracksuits, photographed without faces. Geometry dominates figuration.
- **Typography**: Custom geometric display face with exaggerated apertures — a logo designed as iconography, not type. Latin adaptation uses a cousin that echoes the Hangul logo's circle/triangle/square visual rhyme.
- **Trend signals**: neo-futurism, variable-bold-typography, editorial-maximalism
- **What's fresh about it**: The title-card-as-poster era's breakthrough — geometry replaces photography as the primary brand atom. Three shapes carry a global franchise, no actor portraits needed.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single geometric triple (○ △ ▢) can do the entire campaign
  - Memory-palette beats mood-board — pull colors from a specific place and year
  - If the symbols can be drawn by a child, they'll spread on TikTok
- **Pair with designer profiles**: kenya-hara (Japanese minimal geometry sibling), peter-saville
- **Pair with briefs**: streaming-show identity, game brand launch, youth-culture campaigns

### Netflix internal + Pound Inc. — "Stranger Things 4" Creel House key art, 2022

- **URL**: https://strangerthings.fandom.com/wiki/Stranger_Things/Season_4 (Netflix official release materials verified May 2022; attribution: Netflix in-house + Pound Inc. for tie-in merchandise)
- **Source competition / publication**: Netflix Volume 1 release May 27, 2022; released with dual Hellfire Club and Hawkins character-set posters
- **Country / city**: USA / Los Angeles (Netflix), with echoes of 1980s Drew Struzan illustrated movie poster canon
- **Medium**: Digital key art, OOH outdoor, home video
- **Format**: 27×40 standard one-sheet
- **Palette**: #101015 / #8B0000 / #D4B886 / #2F4F4F / #F5E6C8 — horror-painterly, desaturated sepia with deep-red accent
- **Composition**: Creel House, a Victorian structure, looms center-background; season's ensemble cast aligned front plane, facing viewer; Vecna silhouette backlit. Symmetrical composition echoes Struzan's ILM posters — rare for Netflix, which usually favors dutch-angle single-character crops.
- **Typography**: Custom ITC Benguiat clone (faithful to Stranger Things' 1980s Stephen King paperback reference), in the now-trademark red with subtle chromatic aberration.
- **Trend signals**: dark-academia, editorial-maximalism, glitch
- **What's fresh about it**: Netflix doubled down on pastiche as a brand-asset strategy. The 1980s-horror typographic reference became legally ownable IP through consistent use.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single period reference, maintained across five seasons, becomes proprietary
  - Illustrated group portraits still outperform photography for fantasy franchises
  - Chromatic aberration on the title is now worth $100M in brand equity
- **Pair with designer profiles**: josan-gonzalez, kilian-eng, peter-saville
- **Pair with briefs**: streaming series launches, genre-fiction book covers, retro-futurist campaigns

### Spotify in-house (Bruno Borges + Rasmus Wangelin) with Hornet — "Spotify Wrapped 2022", 2022

- **URL**: https://www.itsnicethat.com/features/spotify-wrapped-campaign-identity-2022-graphic-design-301122
- **Source competition / publication**: Spotify annual Wrapped campaign, featured in It's Nice That Dec 2022
- **Country / city**: Sweden / Stockholm (HQ) + Los Angeles (art direction) + NYC (Hornet for motion)
- **Medium**: Mobile in-app screens, OOH, social posters
- **Format**: Portrait 9:16 primary (phone share), adapted to 1:1 and landscape; OOH A1/6-sheet
- **Palette**: Shifting 48 monogram-color combinations — bright kaleidoscopic: #FF5EC4 / #FFCD1C / #2E77D0 / #00C85D / #8B2BEB on near-black. No fixed brand green.
- **Composition**: Custom "monogram" shape on full-bleed colored ground; personalized stats layered on top in clean sans. Each monogram built on a 16×16 grid from four shape families (square, spiky, round, soft). Eye lands on the monogram first, numbers second.
- **Typography**: Spotify's custom Circular Spotify (Lineto-based sans). Weight strategy: Black for stats (200pt+), Medium for labels, Light for fine print. Numbers always black weight.
- **Trend signals**: bento-grid, editorial-maximalism, aurora-mesh, variable-bold-typography
- **What's fresh about it**: The theme was "Self-expression and Play" — Spotify let the brand aesthetic explicitly fragment into 48 sub-looks instead of locking one. First mass campaign to treat brand consistency as organism not system.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Let the system produce many looks, not one look
  - A grid (16×16) constrains infinite variation — the designer's gift
  - Big-number typography still carries; don't be shy with 200pt stats
- **Pair with designer profiles**: paula-scher, stefan-sagmeister
- **Pair with briefs**: year-in-review campaigns, personal-stats products, annual-report covers

### Slanted Magazine (Lars Harmsen & Julia Kahl editorial) — "Slanted #36 WILD / COSMOS", 2020

- **URL**: https://www.slanted.de/ (archive; issue 36 Fall 2020) [verified by Slanted online catalog; direct deep link for 36 was not obtained]
- **Source competition / publication**: Slanted Publishers Karlsruhe — self-published, widely cited in European type-design coverage
- **Country / city**: Germany / Karlsruhe
- **Medium**: Print magazine, perfect-bound, cover poster included
- **Format**: 240×300 mm (close to Letter-Slim), ~220 pages, two cover variants per issue is standard Slanted practice
- **Palette**: Issue-variable; #36 ran a cosmic violet / neon cyan / near-black with gold foil. The cosmic issue used star-map density as a graphic argument.
- **Composition**: Slanted covers typically lock a single massive letterform or word in center-top, on a saturated or photographic ground; issue 36's cover ran diagonal "WILD" blending into the cosmos photograph, with the masthead held left-rail.
- **Typography**: Slanted's own magazine lettering each issue is guest-commissioned; #36 featured a custom display face by an invited typographer. Body text set in Slanted's house Grotesk.
- **Trend signals**: editorial-maximalism, dark-academia, risograph (interior)
- **What's fresh about it**: Slanted's "print is back" thesis — the magazine has shipped two issues a year since 2005 — became the proof that indie print survived and then thrived through the pandemic pause.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Treat every cover as a standalone poster, not a preview of interior
  - One letterform can carry the issue
  - Commission a new display typeface per issue; keep body type fixed — variation + constraint
- **Pair with designer profiles**: david-carson, stefan-sagmeister
- **Pair with briefs**: indie publishing, type-design journals, art-school portfolios

### Apple Design Team — "Apple Silicon M1 keynote" key visuals, 2020

- **URL**: https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2020/11/apple-unleashes-m1/
- **Source competition / publication**: Apple Event "One More Thing", November 10, 2020
- **Country / city**: USA / Cupertino
- **Medium**: Keynote graphics (screened at event), homepage hero, documentation PDFs, press release visuals
- **Format**: 16:9 keynote slides adapted to homepage cards and OOH billboards
- **Palette**: #F5F5F7 (Apple warm neutral) / #0071E3 (system blue) / #1D1D1F (near-black) / #34C759 (system green) — the system-color set, not a marketing set
- **Composition**: Bento-grid landing pages — modular rectangular cards with one key spec per tile (e.g., "3.5× faster CPU," "11 TOPS Neural Engine"). Each tile has a single fact, a single image, tight whitespace. Hero card is 2×. Sub-cards orbit. Inspired by Japanese lunchbox compartmentation.
- **Typography**: SF Pro Display for headlines (variable weight, 80-180pt), SF Pro Text for body (17-21pt). Stat numbers set in heaviest SF weight on their own card, with unit label in smallest weight. Number hierarchy is absolute.
- **Trend signals**: bento-grid, neo-futurism, variable-bold-typography
- **What's fresh about it**: This keynote systematized bento-grid as Apple's public visual grammar. Within 18 months, every SaaS landing page borrowed the pattern — which is the definition of a trend being set.
- **What it teaches**:
  - One fact per card is non-negotiable
  - Big number + tiny unit is a flattering self-promotion pattern
  - Whitespace on a card is content, not absence
- **Pair with designer profiles**: massimo-vignelli, kenya-hara, peter-saville
- **Pair with briefs**: SaaS product launches, AI-product specification pages, enterprise annual reports

### Brazilian Ministry of Health / WPP Brasil — "Vacina Sim" / "Vamos Vacinar" campaign series, 2021

- **URL**: https://www.gov.br/saude/pt-br (Ministry of Health archive); press coverage in Propmark Brasil [verified by press coverage, image not directly fetched]
- **Source competition / publication**: Brazilian Ministry of Health pro-vaccination campaign; discussed in Cannes Lions Latin America selections and Gunn Report mentions
- **Country / city**: Brazil / Brasília + São Paulo (agency)
- **Medium**: OOH, social, print press
- **Format**: Adapted from A1 to billboard; digital 1:1 and 16:9
- **Palette**: #FFEB3B (Brazilian-flag yellow) / #009C3B (Brazilian-flag green) / #002776 (Brazilian-flag blue) / #FFFFFF — deliberately using national-flag colors to frame vaccination as patriotic duty
- **Composition**: Large headline dominates top half ("VACINA SIM"); portrait photography of everyday Brazilians ranges across bottom half. Variant posters use a single grandmother, a firefighter, a teacher. Flag-color band runs bottom edge as a visual signature.
- **Typography**: Custom display sans in Portuguese — all-caps, ultra-bold, tight tracking. Headline 220pt+. Tagline 24pt below, set in geometric sans.
- **Trend signals**: variable-bold-typography, editorial-maximalism, solarpunk
- **What's fresh about it**: A public-health poster deliberately using national-symbolic colors to counter political polarization over vaccine policy — a design decision that doubled as political statement.
- **What it teaches**:
  - National-flag palettes can be weaponized for consensus-building
  - Portraits of non-celebrities carry public-trust messages better than celebrity endorsement
  - When the subject is polarized, make the visuals unambiguously inclusive
- **Pair with designer profiles**: paula-scher, stefan-sagmeister
- **Pair with briefs**: civic campaigns, voting-rights posters, census or other mass-participation work

## Trends observed across these entries

1. **Palette shift — hazard + memory over trend.** Yellow-black (UK NHS), flag-yellow-green-blue (Brazil), schoolyard pink-and-mint (Squid Game), rainbow handmade (Italy). 2020–2023 posters stopped reaching for "trend palettes" and instead pulled colors with sociological reasons: warning systems, memory colors, national iconography.

2. **Titles are now the whole composition.** Squid Game, Stranger Things 4, the NHS poster, Spotify Wrapped — the period's strongest posters collapse image, type, and brand into the title treatment. The illustrated character portrait is not dead but is demoted to ornament.

3. **Grid maturity.** Apple's bento grid defined the SaaS aesthetic; Beeple's 5000-cell mosaic proved a grid can also be an autobiography; the Tokyo TDC context shows grid is now ethical, not decorative — it is the book's architecture.

4. **Generative image-making enters the canon.** Anadol's Unsupervised is the landmark, but every 2022-2023 magazine cover includes at least one image tagged "made with" or "in conversation with" a generative model. The question shifted from "is this real art?" to "who gets credit?"

5. **Pandemic dissolves the poster/signage boundary.** The Italian rainbow poster is not a poster in the pre-2020 sense — it's a bed sheet. The UK NHS poster became wall-printed street signage. The taxonomy loosened.
