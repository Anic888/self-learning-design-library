# 1930s Art Deco — Poster Catalog

*The streamlined transport-and-commerce decade. These 6 canonical works are the reference set the AI agent should study when asked to work in the Art Deco poster language — Cassandre's machine-age monumentality, Hohlwein's silhouette economy, Kauffer's London Transport modernism, and Carlu's WW2-era American propaganda synthesis.*

## Context

Art Deco poster design is the synthesis of Cubism, Constructivism, Futurism, and commercial Art Nouveau — filtered through the requirement of pure commercial effectiveness. The decade's enablers: offset lithography maturing into full-color commercial stock, the airbrush becoming a standard tool (Cassandre), serial poster campaigns designed to be seen from fast-moving vehicles, and a new transport economy (ocean liners, transcontinental rail, motor coaches, civil aviation) demanding heroic marketing. Parallel German tradition (Sachplakat → Hohlwein's 1930s silhouette work) emphasized ruthless reduction; British tradition (Kauffer at London Transport, Shell-Mex) synthesized Cubism with commercial commuter subjects; American tradition (Carlu's wartime work for the Office of Emergency Management) absorbed European modernism into New Deal propaganda.

## Canonical works

### A. M. Cassandre — "Étoile du Nord", 1927

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5807 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O74368/etoile-du-nord-poster-cassandre-adolphe-mouron/ (V&A)
- **Country**: France (commissioned by Chemin de Fer du Nord, published Hachard & Cie., Paris)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: 105.2 × 75.5 cm (41⅜ × 29¾ in)
- **Palette**: deep blue-violet night sky `#2A3C5E`, steel blue `#4D7195`, gleaming cool silver `#BEC8D4`, warm star `#F2D27A`, muted brick red `#9C3A2B`. 5 tones — a twilight palette.
- **Composition**: Extreme worm's-eye perspective of two converging rail tracks vanishing toward a single star on the horizon. The perspective is monumentally exaggerated — tracks occupy ~60% of the sheet, driving the eye to the distant vanishing point. No figure, no train — only the idea of travel as vanishing-point geometry.
- **Typography**: "NORD" / "ÉTOILE DU NORD" / sub-text set in Cassandre's custom geometric sans (precursor to Bifur, 1929). The O's overlap with adjacent letters; the lettering is arranged as a frame around the image, creating the impression of looking through a train window.
- **Signature move**: Vanishing-point perspective with a single star as the focal object. The poster *is* the sensation of travel — pure geometry, zero illustration. The typography acts as the window frame through which the journey is seen.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Vanishing-point perspective driven to extreme exaggeration is a complete composition.
  - Remove the vehicle — show the journey, not the train.
  - Twilight palette (deep blue + warm accent) carries "luxury travel" better than any literal illustration of a luxury interior.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: syd-mead (transport futurism lineage), kilian-eng (vanishing-point perspective and twilight palette), ash-thorp (cinematic depth).
- **Pair with briefs**: travel, transport, luxury experience, any brief where anticipation/arrival is the emotion.

### A. M. Cassandre — "L.M.S. Bestway" / "L.M.S. The Best Way", 1928

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/6617 (MoMA — L.M.S. Bestway)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5812 (MoMA — "LMS The Best Way, London Midland and Scottish Ry")
- **Country**: UK (commissioned by London, Midland and Scottish Railway; printed by McCorquodale & Co., Ltd., London)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: 105 × 128.9 cm (41⅜ × 50¾ in)
- **Palette**: steel blue `#2F5574`, warm cream sky `#EDDFB8`, saturated red `#C62B24`, black `#111111`, silver-grey `#A9A9A5`. 5 tones.
- **Composition**: A colossal locomotive rendered head-on, the engine's nose dominating the sheet, with dramatic aerial perspective flattening everything else. Cassandre's hallmark: the machine fills nearly the entire frame and is tilted slightly to suggest motion.
- **Typography**: "L·M·S / BESTWAY" set in Cassandre's geometric sans with punctuation as dot-ornament; stacked left-flush; typography rendered as machined object.
- **Signature move**: Machine-as-monument. Cassandre photographs the engine's nose head-on and makes it architecture. Only 50 copies printed — reportedly never commercially deployed, but canonical in every Art Deco reference.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Head-on view of a machine, slightly tilted, flattens it into iconography.
  - Typography with custom punctuation (the mid-height dots in "L·M·S") reads precision-engineered.
  - Rarity of use (50 copies, never deployed) does not reduce canonical status; the idea is what's canonical.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: syd-mead (machine heroic portraits), ash-thorp (head-on monumental rendering).
- **Pair with briefs**: infrastructure, transport, B2B industrial, any brief where the product is a large machine.

### A. M. Cassandre — "Dubo / Dubon / Dubonnet" (Serial Aperitif Poster), 1932

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5370 (MoMA — "Dubo Dubon Dubonnet")
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/6017 (MoMA — "Dubonnet, Vin Tonique au Quinquina", 1932)
- **Country**: France
- **Medium**: Color lithograph (serial poster — 3 panels designed to be seen in sequence)
- **Format**: 44.5 × 115.6 cm (17½ × 45½ in) — horizontal serial format; individual Dubonnet variants at ~52.7 × 43.8 cm
- **Palette**: warm cream ground `#EDE1B8`, bottle green `#456C3A`, warm red-brown body `#A04525`, black silhouette `#141414`, lemon flesh-tone `#E8C480`. 5 tones.
- **Composition**: 3-panel serial: (1) silhouette man stares at empty glass — black outline only, "DUBO"; (2) the same man, now half-colored in by the aperitif he has drunk, "DUBON"; (3) fully colored, raising the glass cheerfully, "DUBONNET". A complete narrative told by progressive color-fill across three sheets.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn geometric sans-serif "DUBO / DUBON / DUBONNET" — each word progressively longer, each panel adding the next syllable. Typography *is* the pun.
- **Signature move**: Serial poster designed for roadside sequence reading by passengers in fast-moving vehicles. The first campaign in poster history to use repetition-with-variation across a highway stretch.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Serial posters tell narratives across time — design the sequence, not the single sheet.
  - The same character + progressive color-fill = a complete before/after.
  - Type-as-pun, spread across three panels, is a brand mnemonic device that has never been topped.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: stefan-sagmeister (narrative serial design), paula-scher (typography-as-punchline), peter-saville (restrained 3-color palette).
- **Pair with briefs**: beverage brand, campaign with before/after narrative, any serial/episodic campaign.

### A. M. Cassandre — "Normandie", 1935

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/6091 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O1405126/normandie-poster-cassandre/ (V&A)
- **Country**: France (commissioned by Compagnie Générale Transatlantique for the maiden voyage of SS Normandie)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: 99.7 × 61.9 cm (39¼ × 24⅜ in)
- **Palette**: deep ocean black `#121212`, hull green-brown `#4A4A3B`, sky blue-grey `#6F8FA8`, warm cream sky/paper `#EDDFB8`, silver hull highlight `#B9C4CE`. 5 tones. Nautical restraint.
- **Composition**: Absolute frontal head-on view of the SS Normandie's prow, exaggerated to gigantic scale — the bow fills the entire vertical axis and recedes symmetrically at the base into an implied ocean. Thirteen tiny white birds flock to the left of the hull, providing the only scale reference. Perfect symmetry. No figures.
- **Typography**: Single word "NORMANDIE" set as a white banner-strip across the black sky at the top — the only text, spare and monumental.
- **Signature move**: Extreme symmetrical frontal view with tiny birds as the only scale reference. A machine rendered as architecture, then as mountain. The most-reproduced ocean-liner poster in history.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Symmetrical monumentality + one scale reference (the birds) = maximum "this thing is enormous" communication.
  - A single word at the top of the sheet, spare, is all the headline that is needed when the image is this strong.
  - Near-monochrome palette (5 tones with no saturation) reads more "luxury" than any full-color scene.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: syd-mead (monumental transport portraiture), peter-saville (near-monochrome luxury), kilian-eng (deep tonal depth).
- **Pair with briefs**: any brief where scale is the story — architecture, infrastructure, luxury liners/jets, large-machine announcements.

### Ludwig Hohlwein — "Und Du?" (Recruitment poster), 1929

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7189 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/artists/2700 (MoMA artist page — 28 Hohlwein works online)
- **Country**: Germany (Munich)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: Approximately 120 × 84 cm (German A1-scale recruitment format)
- **Palette**: black silhouette `#0E0E0E`, warm gold ground `#D9A84A`, muted cream paper `#EDE3CF`, red-brown `#8B2E22`, single accent (olive or brown). 4 tones.
- **Composition**: Single large male figure in silhouette (soldier/worker) rendered with Hohlwein's characteristic flat-color zone blocking — no outline, shapes sit directly against each other with negative-space edges doing the work of line. Figure fills ~70% of sheet; dead-center; direct address.
- **Typography**: German hand-drawn condensed caps — "UND DU?" as dominant headline; stacked directly below and above the figure.
- **Signature move**: *Sachplakat* (object-poster) heritage refined — Hohlwein lights the figure from one side, blocks shadows as flat shapes, and uses the edge between two colors *as* the drawing. No outlines.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A figure can be built from flat color zones without any outline — the color edges *are* the drawing.
  - Direct-address headline ("AND YOU?") is a 1914-onward poster convention; use it when the brief demands viewer implication.
  - A restricted 4-color palette with one warm ground is the Hohlwein signature — more than that, it stops being Hohlwein.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: peter-saville (flat-zone figure construction), massimo-vignelli (economy of means). **Note**: Hohlwein's later career (1933+) is compromised by association with the Nazi regime; this teaching applies to his *technique*, not his politics.
- **Pair with briefs**: recruitment, civic call-to-action, figure-driven brand portraits where outline-free color blocking would read strong.

### E. McKnight Kauffer — "Power, the Nerve Centre of London's Underground", 1930

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/6859 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O1070931/power-the-nerve-centre-of-poster-kauffer-edward-mcknight/ (V&A) — https://www.ltmuseum.co.uk/collections/collections-online/posters/item/1983-4-2996 (London Transport Museum)
- **Country**: UK (commissioned by the Underground Electric Railway Company / London Transport)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: ~101 × 63 cm (London double-crown variant)
- **Palette**: saturated red factory silhouette `#D42A1C`, deep black muscular arm `#0E0E0E`, yellow-white lightning `#F4D21A`, muted grey-brown sky `#7C7266`, cream paper `#EDE3CE`. 5 tones.
- **Composition**: A black muscular forearm and fist punches diagonally across the sheet; two yellow lightning bolts radiate from the fist; a stylized red factory-with-smokestacks is linked to the fist via the composition's diagonal. "UNDERGROUND" runs as a horizontal red band at the base. Cubist-Futurist hybrid.
- **Typography**: Sans-serif caps "POWER / THE NERVE CENTRE OF LONDON'S UNDERGROUND" at top; "UNDERGROUND" masthead logo (Edward Johnston's 1916 Johnston Sans) at base. Edge-to-edge red band integrates brand with headline.
- **Signature move**: Muscular arm as conductor/connector between power station (factory) and network (Underground lettering). Human and machine merged — Futurist manifesto translated into commuter advertising.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single gesture (a fist) can anchor a complex industrial narrative.
  - Cubist faceting of a single body part (the arm) is more powerful than faceting a whole figure.
  - Brand masthead (Johnston Sans "UNDERGROUND") as edge-to-edge band = client-integration without compromise.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: massimo-vignelli (transit-design lineage), ash-thorp (faceted industrial geometry), paula-scher (edge-to-edge typographic band).
- **Pair with briefs**: infrastructure, public utility, energy company, civic announcement, any "power behind the scenes" brief.

### Jean Carlu — "America's Answer! Production", 1942

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5133 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://collection.cooperhewitt.org/objects/420557001/ (Cooper Hewitt) — https://americanart.si.edu/artwork/americas-answer-production-35636 (Smithsonian American Art Museum)
- **Country**: USA (commissioned by the Office for Emergency Management; published by the U.S. Government Printing Office, Washington D.C.; designer is French-born Jean Carlu, working in the US during WW2)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: 76.1 × 101.5 cm (30 × 40 in) — U.S. Government standard poster scale
- **Palette**: pure signal red `#CA2424`, yellow `#E8BE1C`, steel blue `#2E4F72`, white glove `#F0ECD8`, key black `#111111`. 5-ink — the strongest possible American WW2 palette.
- **Composition**: Close-cropped yellow gloved hand gripping a red wrench; blue letter "O" of the word PRODUCTION doubles as the wrench's bolt-head — hand, tool, and typography merged into one compositional object. The rest of the text stacks around the glove.
- **Typography**: Bold geometric sans-serif caps "AMERICA'S ANSWER! / PRODUCTION" — the P, R, O of PRODUCTION integrated with the hand-and-wrench image; exclamation mark as visual beat.
- **Signature move**: Type + object + hand merged as one visual — the letter "O" is the wrench's bolt, not a separate letterform. Carlu's synthesis of European modernism (Cassandre, Klutsis) with American commercial scale.
- **What it teaches**:
  - An integrated headline — where a letterform *is* a physical part of the image — is stronger than any caption.
  - WW2 propaganda palette (red + yellow + blue + black + white) remains unbeatably bold.
  - European modernist training transplanted to American commissions yielded the mid-century poster peak — Carlu bridges Cassandre and Saul Bass.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: paula-scher (type-integrated-with-object), stefan-sagmeister (letterforms as physical objects), massimo-vignelli (disciplined geometric sans).
- **Pair with briefs**: labor/manufacturing, industrial-hero brand, any campaign where work/craft/making is the subject.

## Era-level teachings for the AI agent

### When the brief says "Art Deco" or "1930s transport/commercial poster"

**Format discipline**
- Vertical portrait format dominates — Cassandre's Normandie, Étoile du Nord, Dubonnet are all tall. Horizontal serial format for multi-panel campaigns (Dubonnet serial).
- Standard French commercial poster size: 100 × 75 cm or 120 × 80 cm. Standard British double-crown: 76 × 51 cm.
- The hero object (ship, train, machine, product) fills ≥50% of the sheet.

**Palette discipline**
- Limit to 4–6 tones. The era's printing technology can do more, but Art Deco discipline restrains.
- Cassandre palette: deep saturated + one cream + one muted secondary + black + 1 warm accent.
- Kauffer palette: one saturated accent + muted greys + one high-contrast black anchor.
- Carlu propaganda palette: pure red + pure yellow + pure blue + black + white — flag-strength.
- Hohlwein palette: 3 warm earth-tones + black silhouette + cream paper — no primaries.
- Never use a painterly gradient. Use airbrush-soft transitions only where the subject is aerodynamic (Cassandre's locomotive smoke, liner hulls).

**Typography discipline**
- Custom geometric sans-serif is the Art Deco signature. Futura (1927), Bifur (Cassandre 1929), Broadway (Schraubstadter 1927), Nobel (1929), Peignot (Cassandre 1937).
- Letters can overlap (the O's of Cassandre's "NORD" merge into adjacent letters) — this is a positive move, not a layout error.
- Type as *structural* element: the headline can be the frame, the banner, the bolt (Carlu).
- Mono-weight caps, flush-left or centered, set as monumental blocks. No italics. No script (except rare luxury exceptions).

**Image/composition discipline**
- Head-on, slightly tilted frontal perspective of large machines (Cassandre signature).
- Vanishing-point exaggeration — push perspective further than physics allows.
- Flat posterized color zones, with airbrush-soft gradients only on curved machine surfaces.
- A single scale reference (Normandie's tiny birds) communicates scale better than a human figure ever could.
- Silhouette + flat color is the Hohlwein move; faceting + diagonal is the Kauffer move; symmetry + monument is the Cassandre move.

### Anti-patterns to avoid when working in 1930s Art Deco

- Whiplash curves, organic ornament: Art Nouveau residue, belongs to 1890s.
- Sans-serif body typography in a disciplined Swiss grid: too early, that's 1950s Swiss.
- Photomontage with cropped body parts on red ground: reads Soviet 1920s, not Art Deco.
- Hand-drawn illustrative figures with visible brush-crayon texture: belongs to 1890s chromolithograph.
- Pure-white paper ground: reads post-1950 offset, not 1930s litho.
- Decorative ornamental frames: Art Nouveau residue.
- Multiple horizontal baseline rules: reads Swiss Style, not Art Deco.
- Psychedelic distortion, grunge texture, Y2K chrome: all wrong-decade.
