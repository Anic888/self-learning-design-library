# Japanese Post-War & Avant-Garde Posters — Poster Catalog

*From Yusaku Kamekura's Tokyo 1964 Olympics to Ikko Tanaka's 1981 geometric geisha, Japanese post-war poster design fused Bauhaus and Swiss modernism with mon, origami, ukiyo-e, and kabuki — producing a canon where restraint and emblem do the work of noise. These eight posters are the reference set for the "quiet poster" tradition and the avant-garde pop collage that grew beside it.*

## Context

Japan rebuilt its graphic-design identity after WWII by sending young designers to study Swiss modernism, then returning them to work for Olympic committees, department stores (Seibu, Parco, Mazda, Issey Miyake), theater (Noh, Kabuki, Nihon Buyo), and the JAGDA (Japan Graphic Designers Association). Two vectors emerged and often crossed: a geometric-minimalist lineage (Kamekura → Tanaka → Nagai → Sato) and an avant-garde pop collage lineage (Yokoo → Saito). Both prized the silent poster — one emblem, no noise, all meaning packed into a single mark — but approached it from opposite poles. This is where the "quiet poster" of the global design canon comes from, and where pop art learned to handle East-West iconographic tension without becoming kitsch.

Structurally, the Japanese poster industry differs from the American and European ones in two ways worth remembering. First, corporate commissioning from department stores and manufacturers kept top designers continuously funded at a quality level Western freelancers rarely match — Tanaka, Saito, and Nagai made their reputations on what would elsewhere be low-prestige retail advertising. Second, printing standards (Toppan, Dai Nippon) are among the world's most exacting; the physical object itself has higher gravitas than a commercial Western poster. When working in this vocabulary the digital agent should think of the poster as an object, not an image — stock, ink opacity, and binding / mounting all count.

## Canonical works

### Yusaku Kamekura — "Tokyo 1964 Olympics — Torch Runner" (Official Poster #3), 1962

- **URL**: https://www.itsnicethat.com/features/kamekura-yusaku-tokyo-1964-olympics-identity-graphic-design-040821 (It's Nice That feature)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.olympic-museum.de/poster/olympic-games-poster-1964.php (Olympic Museum); https://www.britishmuseum.org/collection/object/A_2009-3026-4 (British Museum — object 2009-3026-4); https://theolympians.co/2017/11/23/yusaku-kamekuras-famous-first-tokyo-olympics-poster-and-its-impact-on-japanese-nationalism/; https://www.olympics.com/en/olympic-games/tokyo-1964/logo-design
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Photogravure, multi-color offset
- **Format**: B1 poster, ~103 × 72 cm
- **Palette**: Black field `#000000`, crimson red `#C3112D` (the hinomaru sun), torch-flame orange `#F39C29`, deep shadow `#0D0D0D`. 2 saturated tones on pure black.
- **Composition**: Extreme-low-angle photograph of a sprinter at the starting line, shot against pure black — body blurring into the void. The Japanese hinomaru sun + gold Olympic rings hover above. Photography (not illustration) as modernism, a deliberate break from Japan's illustrated poster tradition.
- **Typography**: Modernist sans-serif, small footer — "TOKYO 1964." Type is quiet; the photograph and the red disc do all the work.
- **Signature move**: The first major Olympic poster to abandon illustration in favor of staged photography. Shot in collaboration with photographers Osamu Hayasaki and Jo Murakoshi. Announced Japan's post-war technological revival through Kodachrome precision. Subsequent torch-runners throughout Japan wore Kamekura-designed tank tops.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Photography handled like painting (controlled studio + black void) has more weight than illustration.
  - One red disc can carry national identity if context is right.
  - Modernist type at small scale lets the image breathe.
  - The "Kamekura triangle" — pure black ground, a single circular emblem, a single photographic subject — is reusable at any scale.
  - Collaborating with a photographer rather than an illustrator was the modernist move in 1962; it still reads as modernist sixty years later.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli`, `kenya-hara`, `peter-saville`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Sports / Olympic identity, national-event branding, anything needing "technological confidence" mood, automotive and aerospace campaigns.

### Shigeo Fukuda — "Victory 1945" (Warsaw International Poster Biennale, First Prize), 1975

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/1951 (Fukuda at MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.japanculturemagazine.com/products/japan (Japan Avant-Garde publications); Warsaw Poster Biennale archives; https://www.google.com/arts/ (Google Arts & Culture — Warsaw Biennale collection)
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Screen-print / offset lithograph
- **Format**: B1, ~103 × 72 cm
- **Palette**: Yellow field `#F4C92A`, black `#111111`, occasional red spot `#C01F25`. Two inks + paper.
- **Composition**: A cannon barrel on the right aims left; a bullet heads back INTO the barrel. The weapon self-destructs. Flat silhouette, no perspective, no shading — pure icon. The cannon and bullet are at the same height and scale; the eye completes the trajectory before it registers the reversal.
- **Typography**: "VICTORY 1945" set small in sans-serif at the base; the icon carries the message alone. Type is a caption, not an announcement.
- **Signature move**: The conceptual reversal — violence turned against itself — expressed with two flat silhouettes. Fukuda compresses anti-war argument into a visual paradox you understand in under a second. This single move (an impossible-object or self-canceling silhouette) defines Fukuda's entire career; see also his *Amnesty International* fist-with-barbed-wire (1980), his cutlery-shadow *Lunch With a Helmet On* (1987), and *Earth Day* sprouting axe (1982).
- **What it teaches**:
  - A great conceptual poster = one silhouette + one reversal of expectation.
  - Flat yellow + black has higher signal-to-noise than any full-color image.
  - If the audience has to read text to get it, the concept is wrong.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli`, `kenya-hara`, `stefan-sagmeister`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Social-cause campaigns, editorial illustration for opinion pieces, public-health posters, any brief where a single idea must land instantly.

### Tadanori Yokoo — "Koshimaki-Osen" (Osen in Petticoats — poster for Tatsumi Hijikata's Jokyo Gekijo theater troupe), 1966

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7957 (MoMA — object 698.1966)
- **Alt URL**: https://letterformarchive.org/news/tadanori-yokoo/ (Letterform Archive); https://catalogue.swanngalleries.com (Swann Galleries 2510 lot 242); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadanori_Yokoo
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Silkscreen
- **Format**: 105.4 × 74.6 cm (41½ × 29⅜ in)
- **Palette**: Magenta rose `#E8377C`, cobalt blue `#1F3C9A`, chrome yellow `#F6C82B`, kabuki black `#111111`, white. Five pure screen inks, no CMYK mixing.
- **Composition**: Vertical pop-collage with Mt. Fuji, the rising sun, Hokusai's great wave, and three portraits of theater figures (novelist Tatsuhiko Shibusawa, choreographer Hijikata, dancer Kazuo Ohno) arranged along a rose stem — referencing the 1594 French painting *Gabrielle d'Estrées and One of Her Sisters*. Dense, maximalist, brash. Western art history vs. Japanese folk imagery vs. underground theater in one frame.
- **Typography**: Kanji hand-lettering + Western sans serifs crashed together; multiple writing systems collide inside one sheet.
- **Signature move**: Yokoo turned the kabuki / shinjuku counterculture poster into a pop-art collage using colliding iconographies. Andy Warhol meets ukiyo-e. The poster was MoMA's first acquisition of his work (1966).
- **What it teaches**:
  - Assemble multiple iconographic systems in one frame and let the tension carry the composition.
  - Silkscreen's pure unmixed spot colors give pop-art intensity that CMYK cannot fake.
  - Kanji + Latin type together = instant cultural specificity.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `yoji-shinkawa`, `peter-saville`, `david-carson` (maximalism).
- **Pair with briefs**: Album covers, fashion editorial with East-West tension, theater / performance art, art-book jackets, cultural-studies visual essays.

### Tadanori Yokoo — "Having Reached a Climax at the Age of 29, I Was Dead" (self-portrait / "Made in Japan" poster), 1965

- **URL**: https://letterformarchive.org/news/tadanori-yokoo/ (Letterform Archive feature)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/6512 (Yokoo at MoMA); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tadanori_Yokoo; https://www.artnet.com/artists/tadanori-yokoo/
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Silkscreen
- **Format**: Approx 103 × 72.5 cm
- **Palette**: Hinomaru red `#E23A2E`, chrome yellow `#F4C62A`, black `#0B0B0B`, cream `#F3E9C8`. The national-flag palette as pop instrument.
- **Composition**: Self-portrait of Yokoo as a hanged figure, backed by a Shinkansen bullet train bursting through Mt. Fuji. Sunburst rays from the flag behind him. "Made in Japan" caption. Shock pop at its purest.
- **Typography**: Hand-lettered block caps plus kanji plus small dated cartouche. Declarative, billboard-loud, announcement-style.
- **Signature move**: The announcement poster — Yokoo advertising himself as the first Japanese pop artist, declaring a generational break. Equally self-myth and critique of Japan's postwar identity (the "Made in Japan" caption is both boast and accusation).
- **What it teaches**:
  - A designer's own portrait / manifesto poster is a legitimate category.
  - Sunburst rays + hinomaru red = instant "Japan postwar" semiotics you can subvert.
  - Shock works once. Make the one shock land.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `yoji-shinkawa`, `stefan-sagmeister` (self-as-subject), `david-carson`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Artist announcement posters, solo-exhibition identity, personal-brand portrait work, generational-manifesto editorial.

### Ikko Tanaka — "Nihon Buyo" (UCLA Asian Performing Arts Institute), 1981

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7848 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O1161237/nihon-buyo-poster-tanaka-ikko/ (V&A — object O1161237); https://collection.cooperhewitt.org/objects/18645877/ (Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum); https://auctions.posterauctions.com/lots/view/1-HG2ZX/ucla-asian-performing-arts-institutenihon-buyo-1981 (Poster Auctions International)
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph (printed by Toppan Printing Co., Ltd.)
- **Format**: 103 × 72.8 cm (40½ × 28¾ in)
- **Palette**: Cream ground `#F4ECD4`, black `#0A0A0A`, vermilion `#D44637`, warm flesh-pink `#E7B5A4`, gold `#C79A4B`. The traditional Japanese theatrical palette distilled to four tones.
- **Composition**: A geisha's face abstracted into a pure geometric assembly — rectangles for face planes, triangles for the coiffure, circles for eyes and lips. The entire head is built from 7–8 flat shapes. Composed like origami folded into a portrait.
- **Typography**: Quiet hand-drawn "NIHON BUYO" at the base; display spelling in kanji. Type is accent; the geometric face is everything.
- **Signature move**: Tanaka's geometric abstraction of traditional Japanese subjects — geisha, Noh masks, sumo — by reducing them to Bauhaus primitives. Modernism metabolized through mon (family crest) logic. Still the defining template for "minimal Japanese poster." This was the first time Tanaka used this face formula; he reprised it for decades.
- **What it teaches**:
  - The face is a mask; the mask is 7 geometric shapes. Work backwards from that.
  - Vermilion + cream + black is the Japanese primary triad.
  - Geometric reduction works best when the subject is already stylized (geisha, kabuki) rather than realist.
  - When you abstract a face to primitive shapes, use mon (family crest) logic — every shape must be a recognizable figure when isolated, not just a fragment.
  - Restraint at this level only succeeds with impeccable printing; Toppan's ink opacity and register are part of the design.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kenya-hara`, `massimo-vignelli`, `paula-scher`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Cultural institution identity, geometric logo systems, minimal editorial covers, any "quiet / refined" mood, heritage-luxury brand work.

### Kazumasa Nagai — "Ueno Zoo" (Tokyo Metropolitan Ueno Zoological Gardens poster), 1994

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/4198-kazumasa-nagai (Nagai at MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://kalamaja.studio/products/ueno-zoo-1994; https://www.themarginalian.org/2019/12/12/kazumasa-nagai/ (The Marginalian feature); https://peoplesgdarchive.org/creative/1378/kazumasa-nagai (People's Graphic Design Archive); https://sphere-art.com/en/art-&-culture/225/Kazumasa-Nagai--From-Now-To-Eternity-is-a-Retrospective-Showcase-of-Over-100-Selected-Posters-and-Designs-Works-by-Graphic-Design-Master-Kazumasa-Nagai
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Silkscreen / offset lithograph (all work hand-drawn; Nagai famously rejects digital tools)
- **Format**: B1, ~103 × 72.8 cm
- **Palette**: Deep ultramarine ground `#1A2B6B`, vermilion lion `#D4342C`, saffron mane highlights `#E5A52D`, ivory spots `#F1E8D0`. Two saturated complements on near-black.
- **Composition**: A single stylized lion (and throughout the broader *Life* series, a wide bestiary of animals) rendered as flat colored shapes built from folk-art pattern logic — each limb and facial plane filled with tiny repeating dots, triangles, or line motifs. Animal fills most of the sheet against a flat saturated field.
- **Typography**: Quiet sans-serif Latin + kanji caption. The animal is the whole story.
- **Signature move**: Nagai's late-career pivot (from 1980s onward) into painterly-folk animal portraits rendered entirely by hand — no digital tools. A one-animal-one-poster series that turns the zoo into an art gallery. His abstract-geometric period (1960s–70s) preceded this; the pivot from geometry to bestiary is itself the design story.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Pattern inside a silhouette gives a single figure infinite visual depth.
  - One saturated background + one saturated subject + one accent = the "Nagai triad."
  - Hand-made marks survive digital reproduction; vector outlines do not.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kenya-hara`, `yoji-shinkawa`, `massimo-vignelli`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Zoo / museum / nature institution branding, children's books, folk-pattern revival, identity for conservation nonprofits, picture-book publishing.

### Makoto Saito — "Hasegawa Butsudan" altar-supply poster series (with Hitchcock-profile related works), late 1980s–1990s

- **URL**: https://www.sfmoma.org/artist/Makoto_Saito/ (SFMOMA artist page)
- **Alt URL**: https://eyemagazine.com/feature/article/reputations-makoto-saito (Eye Magazine Reputations interview); https://jankuba.com/text/the-art-of-the-poster-interview-with-makoto-saito/; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makoto_Sait%C5%8D_(designer); https://ocula.com/artists/makoto-saito/
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph (often with spot UV / special inks, embossed blind-stamp elements)
- **Format**: B1, ~103 × 72.8 cm
- **Palette**: Desaturated concrete gray `#7A7A74`, bone white `#EFEAE0`, ash black `#1C1C1C`, restricted single saturated accent (red `#A5252B` or blood-ochre `#7A3E27`).
- **Composition**: Single enigmatic subject — a Hasegawa funerary-urn, an X-rayed body, a skull-profile hybrid, a ghosted Hitchcock silhouette — centered against vast empty field. Deliberate ambiguity: is it alive or dead, human or object, memento or icon?
- **Typography**: Minimal. Often just the client name and a date, set in quiet Futura-like sans at near-footer scale.
- **Signature move**: Saito treats the poster as an existential question. The Hasegawa altar-supply series (for a butsudan — Buddhist household-altar — manufacturer) made posters for *Buddhist funeral equipment* into gallery-tier contemporary art. His Hitchcock portraits similarly refuse likeness for mood. A radical reclassification of mundane commercial subject matter.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A poster for a "boring" subject (funeral supplies, office furniture) is an opportunity, not a limit.
  - Emptiness around the subject is the subject.
  - Desaturated palette + one blood accent = maximum gravity with minimum color.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kenya-hara`, `peter-saville`, `stefan-sagmeister`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Luxury brand posters, film / auteur retrospectives, spiritual or memorial brands, fashion editorial with gravitas, museum-quality annual reports.

### Koichi Sato — "The Modern Poster" (poster for MoMA New York exhibition), 1988

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5045 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/5173 (Sato at MoMA); https://a-g-i.org/user/koichisato/ (Alliance Graphique Internationale); https://designreviewed.com/designer/koichi-sato/
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph with airbrush-gradient color separations
- **Format**: B1, ~103 × 72.8 cm
- **Palette**: Lavender-to-peach gradient `#C4A4D8` → `#F3B989`, luminous cyan halo `#7FCDE5`, white `#FDFDFD`, soft violet shadow `#6F5B8C`. The "light itself" as palette.
- **Composition**: A single luminous fan / disc / ovoid shape floats center-sheet, rendered in airbrush-smooth gradients that suggest light rather than ink. Negative space dominates; the form seems to glow on the paper. Derived from the bokashi gradients of ukiyo-e skies.
- **Typography**: "THE MODERN POSTER / THE MUSEUM OF MODERN ART, NEW YORK" set small in quiet sans-serif below. Type is a caption to the light.
- **Signature move**: Sato fused Western airbrush illustration technique with Japanese gradient traditions (sumi-e water tones, ukiyo-e bokashi) — producing posters that seem to emit light rather than reflect it. Won first prize in the MoMA International Poster competition in 1988.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Gradient mastery: no stops, no banding — airbrush discipline learned from hand-spray work, not digital gradient tools.
  - The light source is the subject, even when there is no representational object.
  - Silent typography protects a radiant image.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `refik-anadol` (luminous color fields), `kenya-hara`, `ash-thorp`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Gallery / museum posters, perfume and cosmetic brand work, tech / AI product imagery, meditation and wellness brands, hospitality design.
