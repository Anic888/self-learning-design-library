# 1960s San Francisco Psychedelic — Poster Catalog

*Between 1965 and 1971, a small group of artists working for Bill Graham's Fillmore and the Family Dog's Avalon Ballroom invented a complete visual language — flaming letterforms, vibrating complementary colors, and an Art Nouveau revival — to advertise rock shows at two San Francisco dance halls. These eight posters are the reference set for psychedelic typography and optical-color technique.*

## Context

The Fillmore–Avalon poster was driven by three forces acting at once: LSD (demanding literal visual distortion), Art Nouveau revival (the Mucha and Toulouse-Lautrec boom reprinted in cheap paperbacks in the mid-1960s), and cheap offset printing that suddenly made saturated full-color runs affordable for small print shops. The signature trick — two saturated complementary colors of equal luminance — creates optical vibration that makes the sheet physically appear to move. Readability was not the goal; atmosphere was, and the near-unreadability of the type was a deliberate audience filter. The "Big Five" (Wes Wilson, Victor Moscoso, Rick Griffin, Stanley Mouse, Alton Kelley) plus Bonnie MacLean, Lee Conklin, and David Singer produced roughly 400 posters over six years that define the era. Their catalog numbers (BG-xxx for Bill Graham / Fillmore, FD-xxx for Family Dog / Avalon) remain the standard index.

The production economics also shaped the style. Bill Graham printed posters in small runs on cheap paper to be pasted around the Haight-Ashbury neighborhood; they were ephemeral advertising, not collectible art. That the posters survive at all is because a fan subculture cared enough to peel them off lampposts. This gives the genre its paradoxical status: the first mass-produced American pop-art medium AND a folk art of bootleg distribution. When the AI agent reaches for this vocabulary today it should remember the originals were printed fast, on thin stock, without proofs — imperfection is signal, not noise.

## Canonical works

### Wes Wilson — "The Association / Quicksilver Messenger Service / Grassroots / Sopwith Camel" (Fillmore Auditorium, BG-018), 1966

- **URL**: https://www.sfmoma.org/artwork/95.630/ (SFMOMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/923930 (Metropolitan Museum of Art); https://www.moma.org/collection/artists/8265 (Wilson at MoMA); https://www.bahrgallery.com/band-items/the-association-1966 (Bahr Gallery)
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph
- **Format**: ~50.8 × 35.6 cm (standard Bill Graham Fillmore size, ~20 × 14 in)
- **Palette**: Viridian green field `#0F7F3A`, flame red `#D3241E`, occasional white highlight `#F4F0E6`. Two saturated complementaries at matched luminance + paper.
- **Composition**: Hand-lettered type curls into a flame shape that reads simultaneously as a human head profile. No central image — the typography IS the image. Date, venue, and bands all flow through the flame silhouette, lines of type bending into the contour.
- **Typography**: Wilson's hand-drawn flame-letters — based on Alfred Roller's 1903 Vienna Secession type but pushed into dripping, melting anatomy. Every letter individually drawn; no two the same.
- **Signature move**: The invention of the "psychedelic letter" — forms that deliberately reduce legibility to force a slow decode, mimicking the dilated attention of the acid experience. Set the template for the entire genre.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Letters can be the illustration — no need for separate image and title.
  - Complementary colors of equal luminance (red on green) produce physical vibration.
  - Illegibility is an audience filter: if you can read it, you're in the tribe.
  - Flame / liquid letterforms derive from Vienna Secession (Alfred Roller, 1903), not from drug imagery — the historical lineage is worth studying.
  - A "psychedelic poster" is just one hand-drawn image at full bleed; do not add 1960s ornament on top of a polished photograph.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher` (typographic posters), `david-carson` (anti-grid), `josan-gonzalez` (hand-drawn density).
- **Pair with briefs**: Music festival posters, underground event flyers, counter-cultural magazine covers, anything that should read as "insider art."

### Victor Moscoso — "Neon Rose #12 — The Chambers Brothers at The Matrix" (NR-12), 1967

- **URL**: https://www.classicposters.com/product-category/neon-rose/ (Classic Posters catalog)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.bahrgallery.com/artist-master/victor-moscoso (Bahr Gallery); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Moscoso; https://rockposters.com/products/auction-neon-rose-12-aor-2-128-chambers-brothers-victor-moscoso-signed-1967-poster-the-matrix-near-mint
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph (three-color process, often with fluorescent inks)
- **Format**: ~50 × 35 cm
- **Palette**: Fluorescent magenta `#F2218C`, electric turquoise `#00B2B5`, sulfur yellow `#F4E630`. Deliberately chosen for equal luminance — the colors "beat" against each other at the edge where they meet.
- **Composition**: A central female figure (or abstract organic form) rendered entirely in flat color shapes with no linework. Hand-lettered title arcs over and under the figure. Every edge is a direct color-to-color meeting — no outline, no shadow, no halftone.
- **Typography**: Moscoso's distinctive bulging, sinuous hand letters — more liquid than Wilson's flames and more botanical. Letters interlock like vines.
- **Signature move**: Vibrating complementary colors. Moscoso trained at Yale under Josef Albers and knew exactly what happens when you place a pure red against a pure green at equal value: the edge shimmers. He weaponized the color theory lesson for rock-and-roll.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Luminance-matched complementary colors create involuntary optical motion.
  - No linework: every edge is a color change, which forces the color choices to carry the drawing.
  - Hand-letter the title and make it structurally part of the figure, not floating above it.
  - Fluorescent inks (day-glo magenta, sulfur yellow) were a 1960s technical novelty; their visual punch is why these posters still look "not from now."
  - A business model baked into the design — Moscoso took 200 free posters to the Matrix in exchange for the right to print more and sell them — is worth studying when designing for low-budget clients.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`, `ash-thorp` (color vibration), `kilian-eng`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Music posters, club identity, any brand pivoting around chromatic intensity, Gen-Z retro projects, neon-revival editorial.

### Rick Griffin — "Aoxomoxoa" (Grateful Dead, Avalon Ballroom), 1969

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/189054 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.rickgriffindesigns.com/concert-posters (Rick Griffin Designs official); https://www.bahrgallery.com/band-items/original-aoxomoxoa-1969 (Bahr Gallery); https://www.classicposters.com/poster/224-1
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph (poster version); LP cover used the same art, printed by Warner Bros.
- **Format**: Avalon standard, ~51 × 36 cm (poster); 12 × 12 in (LP sleeve)
- **Palette**: Burnt umber `#5B3B1F`, chrome yellow-orange `#E89822`, ivory `#F1E5C6`, deep claret `#6D1820`. Earth-tone jewel palette — a deliberate departure from Moscoso's electric spectrum.
- **Composition**: A surreal "solar ovum" — a bulbous sphere holding a stylized sun-face — is wrapped in the band's name spelled in mutant gothic type that orbits the form. Palindrome title "AOXOMOXOA" arcs across the sphere. Underground-comix density; every square inch detailed with scarabs, serpents, and hidden letters.
- **Typography**: Griffin's mutant gothic — thick-letter forms grown from blackletter crossbred with comic-book ornament. The title is both title and ambigram; fans claim "Grateful Dead" can also be read as "We Ate The Acid."
- **Signature move**: Comic-book illustrator discipline (Griffin came from surf comics and Zap Comix) applied to a music poster — drawings precise enough to hold up at any size, palindrome architecture, hidden-word puzzles.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Density + detail + palindrome symmetry = a poster the viewer studies for minutes, not seconds.
  - Earth-tone palettes read more "heavy" and mystical than Moscoso's electric spectrum.
  - Make the title a palindrome / visual puzzle and the viewer engages twice.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kilian-eng` (illustrative density), `yoji-shinkawa` (ink detail), `josan-gonzalez`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Album covers, mystical / occult brands, deep-catalog music reissues, illustration-led book jackets, tarot and esoteric decks.

### Stanley Mouse & Alton Kelley — "Skeleton & Roses" (Grateful Dead + Oxford Circle, Avalon Ballroom, FD-26), 1966

- **URL**: https://www.denverartmuseum.org/en/edu/object/skull-and-rosesgrateful-dead-oxford-circle-avalon-ballroom-san-francisco (Denver Art Museum)
- **Alt URL**: https://entertainment.ha.com/itm/music-memorabilia/posters/fd-26-grateful-dead-1966-skeleton-and-roses-first-printing-avalon-concert-poster-signed-by-alton-kelley/a/41195-5064.s (Heritage Auctions first-printing record); https://www.bonhams.com/auction/19227/lot/174/a-grateful-dead-first-printing-concert-poster-signed-by-alton-kelley-and-stanley-mouse-1966/ (Bonhams)
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph (Bindweed Press first printing)
- **Format**: ~51 × 36 cm
- **Palette**: Desaturated cream ground `#EDE5CE`, Victorian-book red `#B4332C`, ink black `#0B0B0B`, olive shadow `#7F6C3E`. An aged-paper palette, deliberately low chromatic pressure.
- **Composition**: Central oval locket with a skeleton in profile picking roses, crowned with a rose wreath — lifted wholesale from E.J. Sullivan's 1913 illustration for *The Rubaiyat of Omar Khayyam*. Typography wraps around the oval in banners. Formal, almost ecclesiastical composition.
- **Typography**: Victorian circus-poster serifs and banners — a deliberate Art Nouveau / Edwardian revival. Typography is subordinate to the locket; it frames rather than competes.
- **Signature move**: Appropriation — Mouse and Kelley found the illustration in a library book and reproduced it. The poster became the Grateful Dead's permanent visual identity (reused for the 1971 live album and countless merchandise). Pre-digital sampling as band brand-building.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Pre-existing public-domain illustration, recontextualized with a new title banner, can become entirely new IP.
  - Victorian and Art Nouveau revival reads "mystical" and "countercultural" at once.
  - A memorable central motif (skeleton + rose crown) outlives any single event it advertised.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`, `yoji-shinkawa`, `stefan-sagmeister`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Band identity / merchandise, occult and tarot-adjacent brands, heritage-tone projects, memento-mori motifs, vintage-revival editorial.

### Bonnie MacLean — "The Yardbirds / The Doors / James Cotton Blues Band / Richie Havens" (Fillmore Auditorium, BG-075), 1967

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5475 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/755589 (Met Museum); https://archive.org/details/fillmore-poster-67 (Internet Archive); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonnie_MacLean; https://rockposters.com/products/auction-bg-75-the-doors-yardbirds-bonnie-maclean-1967-poster-fillmore-auditorium-near-mint
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph
- **Format**: ~50.8 × 35.6 cm (Fillmore standard)
- **Palette**: Orange `#E8732A`, peacock blue-green `#117E84`, ivory `#F0E4C3`, rose highlight `#C44F5E`. Four harmonic tones — warmer and more balanced than Wilson's combative red/green.
- **Composition**: A human face in profile melts into the elaborate tail of a peacock (a sly Yardbirds pun — "bird"). Typography sits in ornate banners threaded through the plumage. MacLean's work is more composed and less chaotic than the male Fillmore contemporaries.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn, Art Nouveau / Mucha revival letters — more disciplined and more legible than Wilson's flames, but in the same family. Letters fit cleanly in their banners rather than oozing.
- **Signature move**: MacLean fused psychedelic chromatic vibration with a craftswoman's ornament — plus a visual pun (peacock / Yardbird) that gave the poster a second discovery moment. Her BG-075 was included in the 2007 Whitney Museum exhibition.
- **What it teaches**:
  - You can keep psychedelic color theory but dial the typography down to craft-legible.
  - A visual pun on the band name (peacock = Yardbird) gives the poster a second discovery moment.
  - Ornament as structure: tendrils and plumes become the layout grid.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`, `kenya-hara` (craft), `kilian-eng`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Music / concert posters, vintage-revival brands, Art Nouveau-inspired identity work, theater posters, boutique-hotel branding.

### Lee Conklin — "Grateful Dead / Quicksilver Messenger Service / Linn County" (Fillmore West, BG-144), 1968

- **URL**: https://exhibitions.lib.udel.edu/rock-posters/exhibition-item/conklin-lee-bg-144-quicksilver-messenger-service-grateful-dead-linn-county-at-the-fillmore-west-1968-november-7-10/ (University of Delaware rock posters exhibition)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.sfmoma.org/read/an-interview-with-poster-artist-lee-conklin/ (SFMOMA feature); https://www.bahrgallery.com/artist-master/lee-conklin; https://rockposters.com/products/auction-bg-144-grateful-dead-lee-conklin-1968-poster-fillmor
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph
- **Format**: ~55.9 × 35.6 cm
- **Palette**: Pen-and-ink black `#000000` on cream `#F1E8D0` with one accent — usually red `#D02727` or green. A deliberately two-color world when every contemporary was printing four.
- **Composition**: Dense monochrome pen-and-ink drawing in which multiple faces, bodies, and animals morph into each other. The longer you look, the more hidden figures appear. Essentially an optical puzzle — figure-ground bistability weaponized.
- **Typography**: Hand-lettered, integrated into the drawing — letters become part of the morphing anatomy. No separation between type and image.
- **Signature move**: Metamorphic drawing — line art where every shape is also a different shape when re-read. Conklin produced 31 Fillmore posters in 1968–69 in this style, essentially one designer's extended experiment in figure-ground transformation.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single accent color against a dense ink drawing reads stronger than a full-color poster.
  - Visual puzzles reward long looking — useful for posters displayed in the same location for days.
  - Linework density replaces color saturation as the intensity driver.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `yoji-shinkawa`, `kilian-eng`, `josan-gonzalez`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Black-and-white film posters, ink-heavy illustration projects, tour posters, limited-edition / two-color print work, book jackets.

### David Singer — "John Mayall / Mother Earth / New York Rock & Roll Ensemble" (Fillmore West, BG-188), 1969

- **URL**: https://oac.cdlib.org/findaid/ark:/13030/c8h41tj1/entire_text/ (Online Archive of California — Singer poster collection)
- **Alt URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O1175088/bill-graham-presents-poster-singer-david/ (V&A); https://www.bahrgallery.com/artist-master/david-singer; https://illustractiongallery.com/bill-graham-fillmore/john-mayall-fillmore-west-bg-188-OP1-2.html
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph from photo-collage original
- **Format**: ~56 × 35 cm (Fillmore West standard)
- **Palette**: Sepia / toned-photo monochrome `#4A3826` plus deep indigo `#1F2656`, muted ivory `#E8DEC5`. A print-library palette.
- **Composition**: Surrealist collage — vintage engravings and magazine photographs cut and reassembled into impossible scenes (a hand holding a moon, figures emerging from architectural frames). Heavily influenced by Max Ernst and René Magritte. The poster was produced just weeks after the Apollo 11 moon landing and incorporates lunar imagery.
- **Typography**: Thin Art Deco-revival sans-serif — a calm counterweight to Wilson's and Griffin's lettering maximalism. Type recedes to almost a caption.
- **Signature move**: Singer turned the Fillmore poster toward sophisticated collage when the market was saturated with flame-letter psychedelia. Cooler, drier, more library-than-ballroom. He designed 66 Fillmore posters for Bill Graham from 1969 to 1971.
- **What it teaches**:
  - The counter-move inside a crowded style — when everyone vibrates, go quiet.
  - Surrealist collage vocabulary (Ernst / Magritte) reads as "adult countercultural" versus LSD-teen.
  - Art Deco typography calms a wild image.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`, `stefan-sagmeister`, `kenya-hara`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Literary jazz posters, indie film, boutique brand identity, classical music / curatorial print, art-book jackets.
