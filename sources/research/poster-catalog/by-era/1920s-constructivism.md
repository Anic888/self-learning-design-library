# 1920s Constructivism & Bauhaus — Poster Catalog

*The revolutionary decade when poster design abandoned the drawn figure for the photograph, the hand-drawn letter for the machine grotesque, and the centered composition for the diagonal. These 8 canonical works are the Russian-Constructivist + Bauhaus + New Typography reference set the AI agent should study when asked to work in the 1920s avant-garde visual language.*

## Context

Two roughly parallel revolutions collided in the 1920s: (1) Russian Constructivism, born out of the Bolshevik revolution (1917), where Rodchenko, El Lissitzky, the Stenberg brothers, and Klutsis weaponized photomontage and geometric abstraction for state propaganda and film promotion; (2) the Bauhaus (Weimar 1919 → Dessau 1925 → Berlin 1932), where Moholy-Nagy, Herbert Bayer, and Jan Tschichold codified "New Typography" (*Die neue Typographie*) — sans-serif, asymmetric, grid-driven, lowercase-only, photography-integrated. The technology enablers: offset lithography (industrialized in the 1900s, dominant by the 1920s), commercial photography on poster stock, photomontage (Heartfield, Höch, Rodchenko c. 1918–1923), and the halftone screen reaching poster-scale fidelity.

## Canonical works

### El Lissitzky — "Beat the Whites with the Red Wedge" (Клином красным бей белых), 1919

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beat_the_Whites_with_the_Red_Wedge (Wikipedia — consolidated bibliography; the physical poster is held by Museum of Fine Arts Boston and multiple European state archives)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.theartstory.org/artist/lissitzky-el/ (The Art Story — contextual scholarship)
- **Country**: RSFSR (pre-USSR) — printed in Vitebsk
- **Medium**: Color lithograph (letterpress + lithography)
- **Format**: ~53 × 71 cm (approx.); multiple editions survive
- **Palette**: pure revolutionary red `#D41E1E`, white paper `#F4EFE1`, black `#141414`. 3 flat elements only — no tonal modeling.
- **Composition**: A vast red triangular wedge pierces a white circle set on a black ground, left-to-right. Smaller geometric fragments (black squares, red lines, typographic fragments) orbit the central action. Pure Suprematist geometry weaponized for political metaphor.
- **Typography**: Cyrillic sans-serif / drawn geometric caps "КЛИНОМ КРАСНЫМ БЕЙ БЕЛЫХ" scattered in fragments — type floats as shape within the composition, not as a headline band.
- **Signature move**: Pure geometric abstraction used as *narrative* — the wedge literally *is* the Red Army attacking; the circle *is* the White Army being pierced. The first poster where geometry alone tells a story.
- **What it teaches**:
  - 3 flat elements (one shape, one counter-shape, one ground) are a complete composition.
  - Type can be dispersed as *shape*, not pinned in a headline band.
  - Red on white with black anchor is a civil-war palette with zero fat — every hue is earning its place.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: massimo-vignelli (geometric reductionism), peter-saville (flat geometric austerity).
- **Pair with briefs**: political campaign, activist poster, any message that can be reduced to "A conquers B".

### Aleksandr Rodchenko — "Kino-Glaz" (Кино-Глаз / Film-Eye), 1924

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7274 (MoMA)
- **Country**: USSR
- **Medium**: Color lithograph, printed Typo-Lithography Goskino, Moscow
- **Format**: 92.7 × 69.9 cm (36½ × 27½ in)
- **Palette**: process red `#D8371C`, pure black `#111111`, off-white paper `#EDE6D6`, occasional blue accent `#0B4E89`. 3 inks.
- **Composition**: Dead-center single child's eye (photograph) inside a white circle on a black field; the white circle is surrounded by radiating red sans-serif caps. The composition is a target — the film poster *is* the camera lens *is* the audience's eye. Circular centering is unusual for Constructivism (which prefers the diagonal).
- **Typography**: Russian sans-serif drawn caps "КИНО-ГЛАЗ" wrapping the eye-disc like petals; smaller grotesque type stacks at bottom. Red on black / black on red reverses.
- **Signature move**: Photograph + circle + radiating type = lens/target/eye. The first poster that uses circular radial composition as its organizing grid. Photomontage used with total legibility.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A photographic eye at center is a direct-address device — more potent than any drawn face.
  - Radial type around a disc is an organizing system; use sparingly but fearlessly.
  - Three-ink Constructivist palette (red + black + paper) carries entire campaigns.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: massimo-vignelli (target/grid precision), peter-saville (photograph-plus-flat-geometry).
- **Pair with briefs**: documentary/independent film, observational media, surveillance/privacy commentary, any "eye on the subject" brief.

### Aleksandr Rodchenko — "Lengiz, Books!" (Ленгиз: книги по всем отраслям знания), 1924

- **URL**: https://www.artsy.net/artwork/alexander-rodchenko-books-please-in-all-branches-of-knowledge (Artsy — the most consolidated reference for the Rodchenko/Lilya Brik poster)
- **Alt URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Rodchenko (Wikipedia — full provenance)
- **Country**: USSR
- **Medium**: Photomontage, color lithograph (halftone photograph + letterpress overprint)
- **Format**: ~63 × 87.7 cm
- **Palette**: pure signal-red `#D61F26`, black `#121212`, off-white paper `#EDE4CF`. 3 elements.
- **Composition**: Halftone photograph of Lilya Brik (profile, left) cupping her mouth to shout; from her mouth explodes the word "КНИГИ" (BOOKS) in bold 3-D red-and-black block lettering, set in a wedge that emerges as if physically shouted. The left third is photograph; the right 2/3 is typography-as-shape.
- **Typography**: Geometric Cyrillic caps, custom-drawn; a triangle-wedge "КНИГИ" slabs at 3D perspective; additional letterpress caps at top and bottom ("ЛЕНГИЗ", product subtitle).
- **Signature move**: Photographed face + literal speech-as-typographic-object. The word leaves the mouth as a physical 3D shape in red. Photomontage and typography merged as one compositional act.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A cropped face (not full figure) is more direct than any drawn figure.
  - Typography as physical object — set in perspective, as if spoken — is a Constructivist innovation that still reads fresh.
  - One person + one word + one red shape = a complete poster.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: paula-scher (speech-as-typography), stefan-sagmeister (voice-as-form).
- **Pair with briefs**: public literacy, educational campaigns, advocacy where a single voice must cut through, announcements.

### Vladimir Stenberg & Georgii Stenberg — "Chelovek s Kinoapparatom" (Man with a Movie Camera), 1929

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/217943 (MoMA)
- **Country**: USSR (produced by VUFKU, printed at SOVKINO Lithography Works, Moscow)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: 110.5 × 71.1 cm (43½ × 28 in)
- **Palette**: bright orange-red `#E63C1E`, process black `#111111`, sky blue `#3E8AC4`, paper `#EDE4CF`. 4-ink.
- **Composition**: Fragmented female figure — legs, arm, head — stacked in a vortex of skyscrapers set on disconcerting oblique angles. Everything spins around a central vanishing point. The typography spirals into the composition like a whirlpool. Pure kinetic energy on a flat sheet.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn Cyrillic caps that bend with the spiral — type obeys the rotation, not gravity. Mixed weights, mixed sizes, multiple angles.
- **Signature move**: A poster that rotates. The Stenbergs invented a way to render cinematic motion on a static sheet — body fragments, leaning buildings, spiral type, vanishing-point vertigo.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Fragmented body parts (not whole figures) read as motion.
  - Architecture at oblique angles = cinematic depth in a flat poster.
  - Type can obey the composition's rotation, not horizontal gravity.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: ash-thorp (cinematic kinetic design), kilian-eng (spiraling composition), david-carson (type breaking its grid).
- **Pair with briefs**: film premieres, documentary, music video posters, anything that has to convey motion on a still surface.

### Vladimir Stenberg & Georgii Stenberg — "Symphony of a Great City" (Berlin: Die Sinfonie der Großstadt), 1928

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7307 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/86691 — "Odinnadstaty (The Eleventh)" (Stenberg brothers, 1928, for Vertov's film)
- **Country**: USSR (poster for Walter Ruttmann's 1927 German documentary)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: ~105 × 71 cm
- **Palette**: yellow `#F2BC1F`, black `#101010`, red `#D93324`, paper `#ECE2CB`. 3 inks + paper.
- **Composition**: Aerial/oblique view of a city reduced to geometric blocks; a central female figure (in motion, cropped) is pasted as photographic fragment into the architectural grid. Diagonal momentum from lower-left to upper-right.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn Cyrillic condensed caps stacked tightly — each word a different size and weight, creating visual rhythm.
- **Signature move**: Photograph + architectural fragment + diagonal type on a flat three-color sheet. The city itself becomes the poster's grid.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A cropped photograph embedded in geometric architecture creates depth without perspective.
  - Diagonal composition carries energy; use diagonal axes when the subject is urban/kinetic.
  - 3-ink palettes (yellow + red + black + paper) define the Constructivist film-poster signature.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: ash-thorp (architectural compositing), james-white-signalnoise (3-ink retro palettes).
- **Pair with briefs**: urban documentary, city tourism (ironic/avant-garde), architectural festival, any "portrait of a place" brief.

### Gustav Klutsis — "Let Us Fulfill the Plan of Great Works" (Выполним план великих работ), 1930

- **URL**: https://www.productivearts.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/08/Klutsis-Poster-Collection.pdf (Productive Arts — primary catalog of Klutsis 1930–37 poster series)
- **Alt URL**: https://monoskop.org/Gustav_Klutsis (Monoskop — full designer archive) — https://www.moma.org/interactives/objectphoto/artists/12501.html (MoMA artist page; multiple Klutsis holdings)
- **Country**: USSR
- **Medium**: Photomontage, color lithograph
- **Format**: ~123 × 87 cm (large propaganda format)
- **Palette**: signal-red `#C92824`, black photograph `#0E0E0E`, paper white `#EDE3CC`. Deep 3-value palette with heavy red ground.
- **Composition**: A dense forest of raised hands (photomontaged, multiplied, scaled up front-to-back) marches toward the viewer; one giant hand front-center towers over smaller hands behind. Red ground dominates; text banded top and bottom.
- **Typography**: Heavy Cyrillic sans-serif caps in black on red, or white on black bands. Typography as banner; typography as mass object.
- **Signature move**: Photomontage multiplied into a *mass* — not one person, but many people made to look like a crowd via repeat-and-scale of the same cropped photograph. The hand becomes a collective symbol.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Repetition of a single photograph at varying scales creates crowd effect without a crowd photograph.
  - Red ground + black photomontage is the Soviet-30s signature that still reads "propaganda power" today.
  - A cropped body part (hand) can stand in for "the people" more powerfully than a whole figure.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: paula-scher (scale-as-emphasis), stefan-sagmeister (body parts as iconography).
- **Pair with briefs**: collective-action campaigns, labor-day, unions, civic mobilization. Use with caution — the visual grammar is powerful and has been historically misused.

### László Moholy-Nagy — "Pneumatik (Advertising Poster)", 1923–26

- **URL**: https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Laszlo-Moholy-Nagy-Pneumatik-Advertising-Poster-Pneumatik-Reklameplakat-1923_fig3_249882464 (academic reference with image)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/4036 (MoMA artist page — Moholy-Nagy has extensive holdings incl. related Bauhaus works)
- **Country**: Germany (Bauhaus)
- **Medium**: Photomontage + letterpress; designed for offset reproduction (Typophoto concept)
- **Format**: Designed for poster-scale reproduction; surviving proofs at book scale
- **Palette**: black-and-white photograph `#111111 / #F2EDD8`, with red accent `#C8301D` in type. Minimal.
- **Composition**: A car races toward the viewer on a curved horizon made from the letters of the word PNEUMATIK; the perspective is from a tire-level worm's-eye view. The type literally *is* the road.
- **Typography**: Single word "PNEUMATIK" in heavy geometric sans-serif, bent onto a perspective curve — letters-as-architecture. Smaller technical type stacks at margins.
- **Signature move**: *Typophoto* — Moholy-Nagy's coined term for the synthesis of photography and typography into one composition. The car, the road, and the word are the same object.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Typography can be the *ground plane* of a photographic scene.
  - Bauhaus is not only geometric abstraction — it is also machine-age photographic speed.
  - Black-and-white + one red accent is a fully-resolved Bauhaus palette.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: ash-thorp (cinematic photographic compositing), massimo-vignelli (disciplined type-as-architecture), peter-saville (B&W + single accent).
- **Pair with briefs**: automotive, tech product, any brief where speed and engineering must read at a glance.

### Herbert Bayer — "Bauhaus Ausstellung Weimar 1923", 1923

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/189319 (MoMA — final lithograph)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/220977 (MoMA — unrealized poster design, gouache/ink on paper) — https://mcbcollection.com/herbert-bayer (Merrill C. Berman Collection)
- **Country**: Germany (Bauhaus Weimar)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph (final), after a postcard/poster design in gouache, ink, pencil
- **Format**: Poster scale; originally produced as a 20-postcard set plus a poster edition
- **Palette**: red `#D12F1D`, blue `#2F4F8F`, black `#111111`, white paper `#F1EAD3`. Primary-color Bauhaus discipline.
- **Composition**: Pure geometric abstraction — red square, blue circle, black bar, numerals "1923" — arranged on the grid with mathematical asymmetry. No figure, no ornament, no curve except the circle. The poster *is* the Bauhaus manifesto.
- **Typography**: Sans-serif geometric caps (a prototype of what would become universal-style Bauhaus lettering) — "BAUHAUS AUSSTELLUNG WEIMAR JULI–SEPT 1923" set in blocks, stacked left-flush, with massive number "1923" as dominant element.
- **Signature move**: Pure primary-color geometry + geometric sans + mathematical grid = the first truly modern poster. Zero drawn imagery, zero ornament, zero figure.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Red + blue + yellow + black = the primary-color modernist palette; every variation for 100 years riffs on this.
  - Numerals can be the composition's dominant element.
  - Asymmetric grid with left-flush type is the opposite of Parisian centering — and reads modern 100 years later.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: massimo-vignelli (pure geometric modernism), peter-saville (primary-color reduction), paula-scher (numerals-as-dominant-element).
- **Pair with briefs**: exhibition announcements, conferences, any cultural event where the event's conceptual frame is the whole message.

## Era-level teachings for the AI agent

### When the brief says "1920s Constructivism" or "Bauhaus / New Typography"

**Format discipline**
- Both vertical and horizontal formats are legitimate. Film posters often vertical; propaganda often large-format; Bauhaus event posters often landscape.
- Scale in the 1920s is usually smaller than 1890s Paris (lithography + offset economy): ~60–90 cm tall for Bauhaus / Stenbergs, 100+ cm for Soviet propaganda.
- Multi-sheet posters are Soviet, not Bauhaus. Bauhaus posters are single-sheet precision objects.

**Palette discipline**
- Red + black + white/paper is the Constructivist 3-ink signature. Master it first.
- Bauhaus adds yellow and blue (primary-color modernism — Bayer's Bauhaus 1923 poster).
- Photographic halftone enters the poster in this decade — embrace the dot screen, do not smooth it.
- No gradients. No painterly modeling. Flat or photographic, never Impressionist.
- Overprint of red on black to create a near-black accent is a Constructivist move; use it.

**Typography discipline**
- Sans-serif (grotesque) is the era's manifesto. Akzidenz-Grotesk, Futura (1927), the Bauhaus's "universal" lettering (Bayer 1925).
- Lowercase-only is a Bauhaus move (Herbert Bayer argued for abolishing uppercase). Use when the subject is manifesto-like.
- Type at angles (15°, 45°, 90° vertical) is legitimate; type on a curve or perspective is a Typophoto move (Moholy-Nagy).
- Headlines can be the composition's dominant shape (Rodchenko "BOOKS!") — not just a pinned caption.
- Letterforms can be scattered/fragmented across the sheet (Lissitzky) when the subject is revolutionary energy.

**Image/composition discipline**
- Asymmetric composition, driven by diagonal axis. Symmetry is pre-1920 academic; centered composition is also out.
- Photomontage over illustration. A cropped photographic face + one pure geometric shape + one strong color = a complete Constructivist poster.
- Architecture at oblique angles = cinematic depth (Stenberg signature).
- Body fragments, not whole figures (Stenberg, Klutsis): one cropped hand, one eye, one profile.
- Photograph + flat geometric shape must both sit on the same flat picture-plane — no receding perspective modeling.

### Anti-patterns to avoid when working in 1920s Constructivism

- Painterly modeling, rendered shadows, soft edges: reads pre-1920.
- Centered symmetrical composition: reads Belle-Époque, not Constructivist.
- Serif body typography: reads editorial, not poster-of-this-era (exception: Bodoni/Didot as a knowing contrast in very late Bauhaus Berlin, Tschichold 1930+).
- Ornamental frames, borders, cartouches: Art Nouveau residue, absolutely forbidden.
- Full-figure realistic illustration: Constructivism uses photomontage OR geometric abstraction, rarely drawn figures.
- Warm muted jewel tones (Mucha palette): wrong era — use red + black + white.
- Hand-lettered organic display type with curved terminals: anachronistic.
- Photographic realism as the whole poster (no typography, no graphic intervention): this is just a photograph, not a Constructivist poster.
