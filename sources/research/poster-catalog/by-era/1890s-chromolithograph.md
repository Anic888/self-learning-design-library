# 1890s Chromolithograph — Poster Catalog

*The foundational decade of the modern poster. These 8 canonical works are the reference set the AI agent should study when asked to work in the 1890s chromolithograph visual language — from the Parisian cabaret Chérette to Art Nouveau ornament, from Montmartre flat-silhouette radicalism to the Beggarstaffs' proto-modern cut-paper style.*

## Context

Chromolithography — multi-stone color lithography industrialized by Jules Chéret and the Chaix press in Paris from ~1866 — made large-format color printing cheap and fast enough to plaster city walls. Combined with Haussmann's widened boulevards, a rising consumer class, and the explosion of cabarets, magazines, and consumer goods, this technology produced the first mass commercial art form. The ten-year window 1890–1900 is when the poster becomes both a street medium *and* a collector's object (*l'affichomanie*). Every poster in this catalog was drawn directly onto the lithographic stone by the designer's hand — no photography, no halftone, no offset.

## Canonical works

### Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec — "Moulin Rouge: La Goulue", 1891

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/188979 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/333990 (Met) — https://www.artic.edu/artworks/82287/moulin-rouge-la-goulue (Art Institute of Chicago)
- **Country**: France
- **Medium**: Color lithograph (4 colors), on 3 sheets of wove paper joined
- **Format**: 195 × 119.5 cm (76¾ × 47 1⁄16 in) — multi-sheet Parisian kiosk format
- **Palette**: warm cream ground `#F4E8C8`, cadmium yellow halo `#F5B800`, vermilion skirt/letters `#D83A1E`, silhouette black `#141414`, flesh `#E8BEA8`. 4 colors printed + paper ground.
- **Composition**: Foreground black silhouette of Valentin le Désossé (cropped hard by the right edge and bottom) frames La Goulue mid-cancan at center; a rhythmic black frieze of top-hat-and-bustle spectators stretches across the upper third, turning the audience into a typographic pattern. Everything is flattened — no modeling, no perspective except the perspectival yellow gas-globes receding behind her.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn display caps "MOULIN ROUGE / CONCERT / BAL / TOUS LES SOIRS" in vermilion, top and bottom, set inside a black banderole-like band. No typeset metal type anywhere.
- **Signature move**: Foreground black silhouette cropped by the edge — Japonisme applied to advertising. The poster is built from two picture-planes (frieze of spectators + dancers) layered flat.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Use *one* saturated accent color (here yellow) and let every other hue be a near-neutral.
  - A cropped foreground silhouette is stronger than a centered figure.
  - Typography can sit *inside* a flat color band without a drop shadow or outline.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: paula-scher (for rhythm-of-typography parallel), peter-saville (for flat-silhouette discipline).
- **Pair with briefs**: nightclub/music-venue posters, cabaret revivals, any brief where a single figure must carry a noisy crowd scene.

### Jules Chéret — "Folies-Bergère, La Loïe Fuller", 1893

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/5615 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O75604/folies-bergere--la-loie-poster-cheret-jules/ (V&A) — https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b9015255s.item (Bibliothèque nationale de France)
- **Country**: France
- **Medium**: Color lithograph, 3-stone (red, yellow, blue/green) + black key, printed Imprimerie Chaix, Paris
- **Format**: ~124 × 88 cm (Grande-Colombier / typical Chéret kiosk size); printed in 4 colour variants
- **Palette**: lemon/saffron ground `#F3C400`, vermilion `#E53728`, teal-blue `#2E6F95`, grease-crayon black `#1E1E1E`, cream paper halo where ink is spared `#F6EFD8`. Classic "Chérette" 3+1.
- **Composition**: Single female figure (Loïe Fuller in her serpentine-dance gown) occupying ~70% of sheet, swept by a helical whip of drapery that functions as a vector from lower-left to upper-right. The figure floats; no ground line, no setting. Text pinned to the top and bottom in banderoles.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn condensed display caps "FOLIES-BERGÈRE" top, "LA LOÏE FULLER" bottom — both in black against yellow ground. Chéret letters have a slight Victorian-fat-face bone but drawn organically.
- **Signature move**: The floating figure on a flat saturated yellow — the Chérette. No perspective, no scene. The body's motion *is* the composition.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Yellow as ground is radioactive at 30 meters — use it when the poster must win a wall.
  - One figure, no background, no horizon = maximum street stopping-power.
  - Grease-crayon texture on skirts and hair is *signal*, not noise — do not smooth it.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: paula-scher (energy of bodies in motion), james-white-signalnoise (saturated retro color theory).
- **Pair with briefs**: performance/dance events, festivals, anything where "joy as spectacle" is the brief.

### Alphonse Mucha — "Gismonda", 1894

- **URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O1026534/poster-mucha-alphonse/ (V&A)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.muchafoundation.org/en/gallery/browse-works/object/21 (Mucha Foundation) — https://wam.org/our-collection/collection/sarah-bernhardt-as-gismonda/ (Wichita Art Museum)
- **Country**: France (Mucha was Czech, working in Paris)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph on 2 joined sheets
- **Format**: ~216 × 74 cm (~7 ft tall × 29 in wide) — the tall narrow Mucha "panel" ratio Mucha invented for Sarah Bernhardt
- **Palette**: ivory `#F2E8D5`, dark gold `#B8860B`, muted olive-sage `#5B6D54`, oxblood `#7D2F2B`, soft violet-grey shadow `#8E8090`. 5 muted jewel tones on cream.
- **Composition**: Near-life-size standing figure inside a tall narrow arch; the top of the arch is a Byzantine mosaic crown "GISMONDA"; hair, drapery, and palm branch are whiplash S-curves binding the whole vertical. The composition's axis is the figure's spine — there is no scene, only ornament and figure.
- **Typography**: Custom drawn display caps ("GISMONDA", "THÉÂTRE DE LA RENAISSANCE") integrated into the mosaic band — letterforms built from the same whiplash vocabulary as the ornament.
- **Signature move**: Life-size vertical panel + arched mosaic frame + whiplash integration of type and ornament. This single poster invents "Style Mucha" and the template for every Art Nouveau decorative panel afterward.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Tall-narrow portrait format (3:1 or taller) is not a constraint — it's a weapon.
  - Type and ornament share one visual vocabulary; letters are drawn from the same curves as the hair.
  - Muted jewel palette reads more "luxury" than any saturated Chéret primary.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: kenya-hara (ornament as discipline), kilian-eng (rich decorative fields).
- **Pair with briefs**: theatre productions, luxury goods, anything operatic/romantic. Wedding & fashion editorials.

### Alphonse Mucha — "Job (Cigarette Papers)", 1896

- **URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O89346/job-poster-mucha/ (V&A — note a later reissue dated c.1896; the original is 1896)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.muchafoundation.org/en/gallery/browse-works/object/44 (Mucha Foundation) — https://art.nelson-atkins.org/objects/24844/job-cigarette-paper-papier-job-cigarette (Nelson-Atkins)
- **Country**: France
- **Medium**: Color lithograph, gold-ink run, printed by F. Champenois, Paris
- **Format**: ~66.7 × 46.4 cm — standard Art Nouveau advertising sheet (not the tall panel size)
- **Palette**: ivory `#F3EAD5`, dark gold `#B38E43`, deep moss green-brown `#4B5040`, oxblood `#803C38`, shadow violet `#6E5B6A`. Gold is printed as a separate run, not painted.
- **Composition**: Half-length female figure centered; an elaborate whiplash mass of hair fills ~45% of the sheet and winds into smoke curls that double as the "O" of JOB. "JOB" as a repeating diamond mosaic pattern fills the background.
- **Typography**: The word JOB is both *the background pattern* and a foreground logo-form in ornamental Art-Nouveau caps. This is the first "brand-as-decorative-pattern" move in poster history.
- **Signature move**: Hair as compositional binder + smoke/hair that morphs into typography + the brand letterform used as a repeating wallpaper ornament behind the figure.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Hair can be ornament, smoke, border, and silhouette all at once.
  - A logo can be both foreground subject *and* repeating wallpaper — using the same mark twice multiplies recall.
  - Art Nouveau is a *restrained* palette — muted olive + oxblood + gold, not bright primaries.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: kenya-hara (pattern-as-field), stefan-sagmeister (brand-as-ornament).
- **Pair with briefs**: luxury brand, beauty/fragrance, tobacco-adjacent, any "one woman one product" portrait format.

### Théophile Steinlen — "Tournée du Chat Noir", 1896

- **URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O74636/cabaret-du-chat-noir-poster-steinlen-theophile-alexandre/ (V&A)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.philamuseum.org/objects/346520 (Philadelphia Museum of Art) — https://www.vangoghmuseum.nl/en/prints/collection/p2361S2003 (Van Gogh Museum)
- **Country**: France (Steinlen was Swiss-born)
- **Medium**: Color lithograph
- **Format**: ~140 × 100 cm (Grand-Colombier)
- **Palette**: warm golden ochre ground `#E8B13B`, vermilion banner `#D93B28`, key black `#151515`, cream paper for the highlight moon/white `#F4ECD2`. 3 inks + paper.
- **Composition**: Single iconic black cat silhouette, frontal, haloed by a gold halo-disc (a pseudo-medieval saint halo) centered on the sheet. A red banderole at top carries the title; a second text block below. The cat is dead-center, staring at the viewer — the rare *direct-address* Belle-Époque poster.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn Roman caps in key black on the ochre ground above ("TOURNÉE DU"), title "CHAT NOIR" on a red banner in negative white inside the red, sub-caption below ("Avec Rodolphe Salis"). Mixed-weight faux-Gothic touches.
- **Signature move**: Full-frontal silhouette with halo, reading as an icon. The cat is a religious relic and a commercial mascot simultaneously.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single black silhouette on warm yellow never fails.
  - Medieval quotation (halo, Gothic lettering) can anchor a poster for a *cabaret*, not just a church — historical register is free to borrow.
  - Direct stare to camera is a rare 1890s move; use it intentionally.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: peter-saville (icon isolated on color field), massimo-vignelli (mascot as system).
- **Pair with briefs**: bar/cabaret, independent music venue, anything needing a 1-character mascot that can also be a logo.

### Will Bradley — "The Chap-Book (The Twins)", 1894

- **URL**: https://www.loc.gov/item/2002721224/ (Library of Congress)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/339042 (Met) — https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O108459/the-chap-book-poster-bradley-will-h/ (V&A) — https://collection.cooperhewitt.org/objects/18697935/ (Cooper Hewitt; The Blue Lady, a related 1894 Chap-Book poster)
- **Country**: USA (Chicago)
- **Medium**: Two-color lithograph / zincograph on cream wove paper
- **Format**: Approximately 51 × 35 cm (20 × 13¾ in) — American magazine-poster scale (shop-window size, not Parisian wall-pasting size)
- **Palette**: black `#121212`, tomato red `#C92A2A`, cream paper `#F2E9D3`. A 2-color print — deliberately restricted.
- **Composition**: Two women in profile, one red one black, interlocked in mirror-symmetry; a flat decorative plant motif frames the bottom. Foreground is flat painterly; no modeling. Heavy influence from Aubrey Beardsley — whiplash but simplified and Americanized.
- **Typography**: "THE CHAP-BOOK" in hand-drawn Caslon-esque caps across the top, set into a red band; date/number in smaller caps below. Arts-&-Crafts / Kelmscott-press typographic sensibility.
- **Signature move**: Two-color American Art Nouveau. Restraint by economic necessity (American presses could not easily match Paris's 12-stone runs) becomes a virtue — Bradley reduces to 2 ink + paper and wins.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A 2-color print with a paper ground can beat a 12-color print on discipline alone.
  - American Art Nouveau is calmer, more frontal, and less whiplash than Parisian.
  - Kelmscott-style Caslon/Golden-Type caps age extraordinarily well; they still look "editorial" today.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: paula-scher (editorial sensibility), peter-saville (reduced palette as signature).
- **Pair with briefs**: magazine launches, literary events, book covers, indie publishing, editorial-adjacent cultural.

### The Beggarstaffs (James Pryde & William Nicholson) — "Don Quixote" (Lyceum Theatre), 1895

- **URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O76330/don-quixote-poster-pryde-james/ (V&A)
- **Alt URL**: https://beggarstaffs.com/catalogue/don-quixote/ (Beggarstaffs catalogue raisonné) — https://www.vandaimages.com/2006AL3867-Design-for-a-poster-of-the-play-Don-Quixote-at-The.html (V&A Images)
- **Country**: UK (England)
- **Medium**: Cut-paper collage (original design); color lithograph reproductions survive in published form (design was rejected by Irving, never used as a printed theater bill; circulated as print in art periodicals and as Maîtres reproduction)
- **Format**: Large poster-scale design (original collage ~2+ meters tall); printed versions ~double-crown 76 × 51 cm
- **Palette**: black silhouette `#0E0E0E`, sand ground `#D9C39A`, single red accent `#9C2B2B`. Paper as negative space. 2 or 3 colors only.
- **Composition**: Don Quixote on horseback reduced to a single black silhouette against a flat sand ground; the windmills are a second flat shape at right. No modeling at all — pure cut-paper flat shapes. Henry Irving famously rejected it declaring "no more mills!"
- **Typography**: Minimal hand-drawn caps ("DON QUIXOTE / LYCEUM") in the corners — type is subordinate to the image in a way most 1890s posters did not attempt.
- **Signature move**: Cut-paper flat silhouette as total composition. No line, no modeling, no ornament. The Beggarstaffs proto-invent flat modernist design 25 years before the Bauhaus.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Silhouette alone can carry a poster; you do not need modeling, line-work, or ornament.
  - Flat shapes with one red accent is a complete color system.
  - A "rejected" radical move becomes canonical for exactly the reason it was rejected — keep the risky version.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: peter-saville (flat silhouette discipline), massimo-vignelli (reductive shape-making), kenya-hara (negative space as subject).
- **Pair with briefs**: theatre, film, literary adaptation, anything that can be reduced to a single iconic silhouette.

### Jules Chéret — "Saxoléine, Pétrole de Sûreté", 1891

- **URL**: https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O74635/saxoleine-petrole-de-surete-extra-poster-cheret-jules/ (V&A) [verify; multiple Saxoléine editions exist]
- **Alt URL**: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ (BnF Gallica — searchable Chéret archive)
- **Country**: France
- **Medium**: Color lithograph, Imprimerie Chaix, Paris
- **Format**: ~124 × 88 cm (Grande-Colombier)
- **Palette**: deep teal-blue night-ground `#2E6F95`, lamp-glow saffron `#F5B800`, vermilion skirt `#D83A1E`, cream flesh `#F5E0CA`, key black `#1B1B1B`. Night palette — rare for Chéret.
- **Composition**: Single woman (the Chérette) holding a kerosene lamp aloft; the lamp's yellow halo illuminates her face and upper body, carving her out of a flat teal ground. Text pinned to top and bottom in banderoles. Unusually moody Chéret — most of his oeuvre is sunny yellow.
- **Typography**: Hand-drawn display caps top ("SAXOLÉINE") and bottom ("PÉTROLE DE SÛRETÉ / EXTRA-BLANC — DÉODORISÉ — INEXPLOSIBLE") in a red and black banderole.
- **Signature move**: The product (a lamp) becomes the compositional light source. The poster's chiaroscuro is generated by the thing being sold.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Product-as-light-source is a compositional device (Chéret did it first, Cassandre will do it again 40 years later with Dubonnet).
  - Chéret does not only do yellow grounds — a dark teal background is as Belle Époque as bright.
  - 4-color tonal night scene is still "poster," not "painting," if the edges stay hard.
- **Pair with designer profiles in this library**: james-white-signalnoise (moody glow palettes), kilian-eng (night-ground illumination).
- **Pair with briefs**: product-with-a-light-source (lamps, screens, candles, phones), evening-event, any brief where a single object becomes the hero light.

## Era-level teachings for the AI agent

### When the brief says "1890s Paris" or "chromolithograph era"

**Format discipline**
- Default to vertical portrait orientation at ~3:2 or taller. Mucha panels go to 3:1 or taller.
- Never produce a landscape-format 1890s poster unless the brief is specifically a Beggarstaffs-style theatrical bill (which was also vertical) or a Penfield-style magazine shop-window ad.
- Figure fills 60–80% of the sheet. Negative "white space" as a modernist design value does not exist yet in this era.

**Palette discipline**
- Use 3 to 5 hues on cream/ivory paper ground — never on pure white.
- Show overprint mixing (the secondary colors produced when two transparent inks overlap) — this is the Belle-Époque secondary palette.
- Never use a full CMYK / 10-color digital gradient. The era's pigment range is narrower and muted.
- For Chéret: saturated vermilion + saffron + teal-blue + black + cream.
- For Mucha: ivory + dark gold + muted olive/sage + oxblood + soft violet.
- For Lautrec: flat black silhouette + 1 saturated accent (yellow or red) on muted ground.
- For Beggarstaffs: black silhouette + 1 earth-tone ground + 1 red accent.

**Typography discipline**
- Every letter is hand-drawn. Typeset metal type on a poster reads "editorial," not "poster."
- For Art Nouveau subjects: Auriol, Eckmann, Arnold Böcklin, Grasset's type, custom organic terminals.
- For Arts-&-Crafts American subjects: Caslon, Golden Type, Troy, Chaucer, Cheltenham.
- For 1890s Vienna Secession: geometric hand-drawn caps anticipating the Wiener-Werkstätte square-based lettering (late-1890s Moser).

**Image/composition discipline**
- Hard-edged flat color zones; grease-crayon texture in skies; no halftone dots, no photographic reproduction on posters.
- Integrate type with image — the headline sits on a colored banderole, ribbon, or cartouche, not in a separate whitespace zone.
- Women as subject archetype: allegorical, idealized, draped, semi-nude; not realistic portraits, not "customers."
- Figures look away or into middle distance — direct-address (the Steinlen Chat Noir stare) is the rare exception, not the rule.

### Anti-patterns to avoid when working in 1890s chromolithograph

- Photography, halftone dots, offset-smooth flats: anachronistic.
- Sans-serif body typography (except as isolated display caps): reads Bauhaus-modernist.
- Pure white paper ground: reads post-2000 digital.
- Centered symmetrical composition: reads academic-Salon, not Belle-Époque commercial.
- Flat vector-look gradients: reads Adobe Illustrator, not stone-ground.
- "Minimal" 2-color design with huge negative-space margins: reads Swiss Style, not 1890s.
- Sachplakat-style single-object-on-blank-ground: reads Bernhard 1906+, not 1890s.
- A logo-centric composition with the brand as dominant element: reads 1960s+ commercial.
