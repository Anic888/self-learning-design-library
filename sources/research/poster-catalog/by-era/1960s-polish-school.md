# 1960s Polish School of Posters — Poster Catalog

*The Szkoła Polska (Polish School of Posters) turned state-commissioned culture posters — for operas, films, circuses, art exhibitions — into high art: painterly, metaphorical, hand-made, and authored. A reference set for any brief where metaphor and gesture must outweigh literal depiction.*

## Context

Under state patronage but largely outside Western commercial pressures, Polish designers working from the mid-1950s through the 1980s treated every film, opera, and exhibition poster as an independent artwork. Typography was almost always hand-drawn. Photomontage, loose brush ink, collage, and symbolic reduction replaced stock portraiture and studio photography. The result: a sixty-year lineage where the poster is read as poetry, not advertising — metaphor over message, emotion over information. The school's methodology (an idea first, then a medium — ink, gouache, photogram, whatever fits) is the direct ancestor of concept-driven editorial design today.

The economic conditions matter. Polish clients paid modestly and demanded little; designers had unusual creative latitude but also produced on cheap paper with limited color runs. That combination — high-concept intent, low production budget — forced visual invention. Every canonical Polish poster shown below was printed on standard B1 offset stock, typically in four spot colors. The power comes entirely from the idea and the mark-making, not from the reproduction.

## Canonical works

### Henryk Tomaszewski — "Henry Moore" (exhibition poster, Zachęta, Warsaw), 1959

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/5894 (Tomaszewski at MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://zacheta.art.pl/pl/mediateka-i-publikacje/henry-moore (Zachęta National Gallery record); https://www.mutualart.com/Artwork/A-poster-for-the-exhibition-of-works-by-/B26CAD3CF7B3467D6AFD3438B8B63C61 (MutualArt); https://a-g-i.org/user/henryktomaszewski/ (Alliance Graphique Internationale)
- **Country**: Poland
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph on paper (originally gouache/ink artwork)
- **Format**: 69 × 98 cm
- **Palette**: Raw off-white background `#F2EDE3`, bold ink black `#111111`, sanguine red accent `#B8383A`, warm ochre shadow `#C8A15A`. 3 colors + paper ground.
- **Composition**: Hand-lettered "MOORE" dominates the sheet — letters drawn as if carved, with the "O"s behaving like Moore's pierced sculptural forms. The name becomes object. No image of the sculptor or his work is needed; the typography IS the sculpture. The poster reportedly hung on Paul Rand's studio wall as the only image he displayed.
- **Typography**: Entirely hand-drawn display letters. No grid. Baseline wanders. Thickness modulates like brush pressure. Letters seem both pressed and pulled from the paper.
- **Signature move**: The letters themselves perform the subject. The holes in the O's are Moore holes; the weight of the M is Moore weight. A subject-as-typography translation that is now a permanent design vocabulary item.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Typography as subject, not container — the word can BE the thing advertised.
  - Strip the image entirely when the letters can carry all the meaning.
  - Hand-drawn scale variations create voice where a typeface cannot.
  - A four-letter subject name ("MOORE", "CAGE", "CASH") is a gift — use it as the whole composition.
  - Offset from vintage paper stock (warm cream, not white) halves chromatic brightness and doubles gravity.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher` (typographic posters), `peter-saville` (restraint + culture), `massimo-vignelli` (reductive discipline).
- **Pair with briefs**: Museum / exhibition posters, literary titles where the name is iconic, typography-first identity work, single-word wordmark systems, artist-retrospective campaigns.

### Jan Lenica — "Wozzeck" (poster for Warsaw Opera production of Alban Berg), 1964

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7203 (MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.cooperhewitt.org/2015/01/12/wozzeck/ (Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum); https://search.scalarchives.com/id/01619248 (Scala Archives); https://www.moma.org/artists/3480 (Lenica at MoMA)
- **Country**: Poland
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph
- **Format**: 96.5 × 67.3 cm (38 × 26½ in)
- **Palette**: Deep black `#050505`, oxblood red `#8B1A1E`, warm cream ground `#F4E8D4`, one pale moon white `#EFE7D1`. 3 inks + paper.
- **Composition**: A screaming head with open mouth merges into concentric rippling water lines that fill the sheet. A blood-red circle (moon / the drowning Wozzeck's disappearing horizon) floats upper-center. Horror and tenderness in the same form. The composition works as both portrait and water-surface — a single image that is literally two views at once.
- **Typography**: "WOZZECK" hand-drawn in dark, uneven caps — arranged along the rippling curve so the title follows the water. No sans-serif rigor; letters behave like ink drops.
- **Signature move**: One figure dissolving into its environment — character and circumstance become the same mark. Won a Gold Medal at the 1966 Warsaw International Poster Biennale and remains Lenica's most-reproduced design.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Marry the subject to its element: the water and the body share the same lineweight.
  - A single red circle + black linework can carry an entire opera's mood.
  - Typography bends to the composition, not the other way around.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`, `massimo-vignelli` (for counter-example of rigor), `yoji-shinkawa` (ink discipline).
- **Pair with briefs**: Theater / opera / ballet posters, dramatic literary adaptations, tragic biographies, mental-health narratives, psychological thrillers.

### Roman Cieślewicz — "Kafka — Proces" ("The Trial"), 1964

- **URL**: https://catalogue.swanngalleries.com/Lots/auction-lot/ROMAN-CIESLEWICZ-(1930-1996)-KAFKA--PROCES-1964-32x21-inches (Swann Galleries record)
- **Alt URL**: https://polishpostergallery.com/gallery/?q=cieslewicz_roman (Polish Poster Gallery); https://polishpostershop.com/en/roman-cieslewicz-potega-znaku-graficznego/ (Pigasus / retrospective); https://mfpostershop.com/en/artist/85-cieslewicz-roman
- **Country**: Poland (later Paris)
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph with photomontage / enlarged halftone
- **Format**: ~81 × 53 cm (32 × 21 in) — Polish theater B2 format
- **Palette**: Saturated inky black `#000000`, desaturated grey photograph `#A8A29A`, paper white `#F5F5F0`. Monochrome with one textural grey.
- **Composition**: A photographic head reduced to extreme contrast halftone is repeated at shrinking scale down the sheet — the protagonist recedes into a white void until he disappears. Absence and repetition deliver Kafka's paranoia more directly than any illustration of courtroom architecture could.
- **Typography**: Sans-serif caps placed quietly at the head — the image does the shouting; the letters are a caption, not a title.
- **Signature move**: Photographic reduction pushed until the face becomes a dot — scale-shift as psychological erosion. The void as antagonist.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Repetition with diminution = narrative of loss compressed into a single static frame.
  - High-contrast halftone photomontage reads emotionally, not journalistically.
  - Leave massive white void — the empty space is the subject.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli` (opposing rigor), `peter-saville`, `stefan-sagmeister` (concept-first).
- **Pair with briefs**: Literary adaptations, psychological thrillers, political protest, editorial covers about surveillance / anxiety / bureaucracy.

### Roman Cieślewicz — "Zoom — Contre la Pollution de l'Œil" (cover/poster for Zoom magazine), 1971

- **URL**: https://www.centrepompidou.fr/en (Centre Pompidou collection entry — search "Cieślewicz Zoom")
- **Alt URL**: http://signum.art.pl/en/wydarzenie/roman-cieslewicz-collages-1961-1972-2/ (Signum Foundation retrospective); https://visualdiplomacyusa.blogspot.com/2018/12/artist-of-day-december-11-roman_11.html
- **Country**: France (by Polish-born designer, settled in Paris 1963)
- **Medium**: Photomontage, offset
- **Format**: Magazine cover / poster, approx 40 × 30 cm cover format
- **Palette**: Bleached pinks `#F4BAB5`, pulp magazine red `#D4293C`, near-black `#111111`, paper `#EFEAE0`. Pop-art intentional chromatic restraint.
- **Composition**: Two halved faces collaged together — stitched vertical seam down the center. Beauty and horror occupy the same head. Cieślewicz repeatedly used this surgical bisection technique across a whole series of "collages centres."
- **Typography**: "ZOOM" in heavy display type as a mast; tagline set small against the face. The magazine's typographic voice surrenders to the image.
- **Signature move**: The halving — two photographs split down a center line to produce a third hybrid identity. A Cieślewicz staple, and arguably the template for every "split-face" editorial cover of the following fifty years.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Surgical collage (clean center seam) reads as critique; loose collage reads as dream.
  - Pull color from the source photographs, don't overlay it.
  - Let the face occupy nearly the whole sheet — bleed to the edges.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`, `david-carson` (deconstruction), `stefan-sagmeister`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Magazine covers about identity / media critique, fashion editorial, cultural-studies book jackets, visual essays.

### Waldemar Świerzy — "Jimi Hendrix" (music poster), 1974

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waldemar_%C5%9Awierzy (biography)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.projekt26.com/shop-polish-posters/p/waldemar-swierzy-jimi-hendrix (Projekt 26); https://www.liveauctioneers.com/item/65617329_advertising-poster-jimi-hendrix-waldemar-swierzy-1974 (AntikBar record); https://polishpostergallery.com/poster/swierzy_jimy_hendrix
- **Country**: Poland
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph from painted original (gouache)
- **Format**: ~97 × 67 cm (B1)
- **Palette**: Prussian blue field `#0B2F5C`, cadmium yellow `#F3B820`, ivory highlight `#F4E8C6`, deep shadow black `#0A0E14`. 3 saturated tones + black.
- **Composition**: Hendrix as a painted, expressionist head — afro and guitar neck wrapping into one silhouette. Brushwork visible; gestural, loose. The rock-star-as-icon, but hand-painted in gouache rather than reproduced from a photograph. Świerzy's series of great jazz and rock musicians (Coltrane, Armstrong, Morrison, Hendrix) all follow this formula.
- **Typography**: "HENDRIX" hand-lettered, unpolished — matches the brushstroke energy of the portrait. No manufactured typeface.
- **Signature move**: Painterly musician portraits that refuse the glossy photographic treatment Western music posters standardized. A hand-made protest against celebrity merchandising.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Personality comes from brush pressure — digital gradients can't fake it.
  - A portrait doesn't need likeness; it needs gesture.
  - Very saturated background + very desaturated face = icon treatment.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `josan-gonzalez` (painterly), `paula-scher`, `yoji-shinkawa` (ink + figure).
- **Pair with briefs**: Music posters, celebrity tributes, jazz / rock / hip-hop biographies, concert tour art, book-jacket portraits.

### Franciszek Starowieyski — Theater posters ("Polish Baroque" period, late 1960s–1980s)

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/5607 (Starowieyski at MoMA)
- **Alt URL**: https://polishpostershop.com/en/franciszek-starowieyski-teatr-rysowania/ (Pigasus feature); https://blog.inyourpocket.com/poland/2018/09/the-power-of-polish-posters-franciszek-starowieyski/; https://designobserver.com/starowieyskis-graphic-universe-of-excess/ (Design Observer essay); https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franciszek_Starowieyski
- **Country**: Poland
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph from hand-drawn / hand-painted originals (sepia, ink, chalk)
- **Format**: B1, approx 97 × 67 cm
- **Palette**: Parchment `#E8DCC2`, sanguine / burnt sienna `#8E3A1C`, bone white `#F2EBD9`, ink black `#111111`. A near-monochrome, manuscript palette.
- **Composition**: Anatomical bodies fused with bulls, birds, and baroque ornament. Skulls and ribcages rendered in the style of Renaissance anatomical plates. The body is never whole — it is split, winged, grafted, or dissected. Ornament is excessive by design.
- **Typography**: Hand-lettered, often scratchy — as if scrawled by the same hand that drew the figure. Starowieyski famously signed and dated his posters with future years ("2031") as a memento-mori prank.
- **Signature move**: "21st-century Baroque" — grotesque eroticism, corporeal fragments, a drawing-theater sensibility where the poster reads as a plate from an alchemical manuscript. In 1986 he became the first Polish artist to have a solo show at MoMA.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Drawing technique from medical / alchemical illustration carries cultural weight digital rendering cannot.
  - Grotesque is not ugly — it is beauty plus transgression.
  - Signatures matter: the author's mark is part of the composition, not an after-thought.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `yoji-shinkawa` (ink work), `peter-saville`, `josan-gonzalez` (painterly density).
- **Pair with briefs**: Literary horror, theater, perfumery / fashion with dark romance, opera, memento-mori themed work, luxury editorial.

### Jan Młodożeniec — "Gremlins" (Polish release), 1985

- **URL**: https://polishposter.com/designer/jan-mlodozeniec.html
- **Alt URL**: https://posteritati.com/artist/127/jan-mlodozeniec (Posteritati); https://polishpostergallery.com/gallery/?q=mlodozeniec_jan; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_M%C5%82odo%C5%BCeniec
- **Country**: Poland
- **Medium**: Offset lithograph from painted original (flat gouache)
- **Format**: B1, ~97 × 67 cm
- **Palette**: Cobalt ground `#2B4BA0`, tomato red `#E04A35`, butter yellow `#F6D24E`, black outline `#1A1A1A`. Three primaries + outline.
- **Composition**: Naive-childlike flat color masses with heavy black outlines, built as if cut from construction paper. The film title and a cartoon-simple gremlin face occupy the full frame. No perspective; pure pattern. Młodożeniec studied under Tomaszewski at the Warsaw Academy of Fine Arts and pushed the master's playfulness into folk-primitive directions.
- **Typography**: Chunky hand-drawn display caps with uneven spacing — a child's handwriting amplified. The poster's "font" is deliberately technically crude.
- **Signature move**: Folk-art flatness applied to American commercial cinema — disarms the marketing and makes the film strange again. The Polish film-poster tradition routinely did this with Hollywood titles (the 1981 Polish "Apocalypse Now" by Świerzy is another famous example).
- **What it teaches**:
  - Studied naivety (adult designer using child-drawn language) is a powerful subversion of slick Hollywood marketing.
  - 3–4 flat color zones only. No gradient, no shading.
  - Let the outline carry all the linework.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`, `josan-gonzalez`, `kenya-hara` (for oppositional calm).
- **Pair with briefs**: Indie film reissues, children's programming (with edge), festival posters, counter-cultural reinterpretation of mainstream IP, picture-book identities.

### Henryk Tomaszewski — "Cyrk" (Polish State Circus series), 1960s

- **URL**: http://www.posterpage.ch/exhib/ex318tom/ex318tom.htm (René Wanner's Poster Page exhibition record)
- **Alt URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/5894 (MoMA); https://a-g-i.org/user/henryktomaszewski/ (AGI); https://en-m-wikipedia-org.translate.goog/wiki/Henryk_Tomaszewski_(poster_artist)
- **Country**: Poland
- **Medium**: Offset from gouache/ink painting
- **Format**: B1, ~97 × 67 cm
- **Palette**: Primary red `#D92B2E`, chrome yellow `#F6C82B`, ultramarine `#2337A0`, ink black. A kindergarten-bright palette repurposed for high art.
- **Composition**: A single animal or acrobat rendered as a loose gestural silhouette — often only a few strokes. The rest of the sheet is clean color field. "Cyrk" appears in hand-drawn letters, often unified with the drawing. The series (which ran 1962–1989) drew in dozens of top Polish designers — every one treated the same word differently.
- **Typography**: Hand-lettered, bouncy, circus-bill energy but stripped of Victorian clutter. Letters sit inside or beneath the figure, not above.
- **Signature move**: The Polish state-circus poster series forced many top designers to treat one word ("Cyrk") as the unifying brand — a decade-long exercise in stylistic range within a tight constraint. Every designer's Cyrk poster is instantly recognizable as their own.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Brand consistency can come from a single repeated word, not a logo.
  - Gestural drawing (one brushload, no redrawing) reads as joy — finish reads as cold.
  - A series format lets each piece live in the shadow of all the others; participation is the point.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`, `kenya-hara`, `josan-gonzalez`.
- **Pair with briefs**: Festival series identity, performing-arts seasons, typographic mark-as-brand projects, children's museum identity.
