# Photo-Based Posters — Catalog

*Posters where a photograph is the hero and typography is subservient — sometimes whispered, sometimes absent. The image has to carry the idea; type exists to credit, caption, or locate. Use when the subject has a face, a body, a scene, or a moment that the viewer needs to recognize or feel before they read.*

## When this technique wins

- Film and entertainment where a star, a character, or an iconic frame must sell the product
- Editorial covers and magazine posters where a portrait is the content
- Fashion and lifestyle where the photograph IS the product (clothing on a body, skin, texture)
- Social and political campaigns where witness-photography shocks or indicts
- Architecture, travel, and documentary subjects where place/person must be seen

## When NOT to use it

- Institutional identity where voice matters more than face (use type-only instead)
- Abstract or conceptual subjects that no photograph can show without being literal
- Briefs where rights, model release, or ethical use of photographic subjects is contested

## Canonical works

### Peter Saville, Martyn Atkins; photograph by Bernard Pierre Wolff — Joy Division "Closer" album & promotional poster, Factory Records FACT-25, 1980

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/189342
- **Country**: UK (photograph taken in Genoa, Italy)
- **Medium**: offset lithography on matte stock; Wolff's original 1978 gelatin silver photograph
- **Format**: 12 x 12 in sleeve; associated promotional poster approx A2
- **Palette**: warm duotone — bone white (#EDE6D3), stone gray (#8A857A), near-black (#1F1C18)
- **Composition**: the photograph — the Appiani family tomb at Staglieno cemetery in Genoa, showing mourners at a sculpted deathbed — fills the entire sleeve. Saville added a thin classical border and set the band name and album title in a Roman capital at the top, almost tomb-inscription small. The image speaks; the type is the chiseled label.
- **Typography**: Bodoni / Roman capital, set tight and small at the top. Type occupies less than 3% of the sheet. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: treating the sleeve as a carved stone — Roman inscription typography at small scale turns the photograph into a monument.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A photograph of existing statuary or architecture can do work that studio photography can't — it carries history
  - Small Roman capitals at the top or bottom turn any photograph into a "document"
  - Duotone or warm monochrome reads as solemn where four-color reads as commercial
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`
- **Pair with briefs**: serious music releases, memorial/commemorative posters, documentary film, literary fiction covers

### Eiko Ishioka (art direction) & Haruo Takino (painting) — "Apocalypse Now" Japanese B0 poster, 1980

- **URL**: https://mubi.com/en/notebook/posts/movie-poster-of-the-week-the-posters-of-eiko-ishioka-and-haruo-takino
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: two painted posters at 58 x 40 in (hyper-realist, commissioned by Ishioka from Takino), reproduced as large-format offset B0
- **Format**: Japanese B0 (48 x 57 in)
- **Palette**: jungle green (#2E4A2C), burnt orange (#D4521C), black (#111111), gold (#C9A14B), smoke gray (#736B63)
- **Composition**: the Japanese release posters show a hyper-real painted scene — helicopters against napalm sunrise for one, a surfer cutting across a red ocean for the other — occupying the full sheet. Japanese title characters run vertically at one edge; Latin credits compress to a band at the bottom. The painting behaves as a photograph: lit and framed cinematically, photographic in fidelity.
- **Typography**: hand-lettered Japanese kanji vertical; Latin credit block in a clean sans at the base. Subservience: **3**.
- **Signature move**: commissioning a hyper-realist painting that behaves like a still photograph — letting "photographic" mean "cinematic" rather than "captured."
- **What it teaches**:
  - For film posters, the image must read as a movie frame, not as a portrait
  - Vertical title treatment (Japanese convention) can compress a lot of information into a thin column, leaving the image uninterrupted
  - Commissioning paintings that look photographic gives you total compositional control — no location, no model availability, no weather
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Ishioka — consider adding*
- **Pair with briefs**: international film releases, epic-scale drama, genre films that need atmosphere rather than character close-up

### Stefan Sagmeister; photograph by Tom Schierlitz — AIGA Detroit lecture poster ("Skin poster"), 1999

- **URL**: https://sagmeister.com/work/aiga-detroit/
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: four-color offset from original photograph; original "type" cut into Sagmeister's skin by intern Martin Woodtli
- **Format**: AIGA speaking-engagement poster, approx 24 x 36 in
- **Palette**: skin tones (#E4B59C through #7A4A3A), blood red (#A31C1F), background flat gray (#8D8D8D)
- **Composition**: a tightly cropped photograph of Sagmeister's torso shows the entire lecture details — date, venue, title — cut into his skin as welts and scabs. The photograph fills the frame; the type IS the photograph because the type is physically on his body. Negative space is skin.
- **Typography**: the text is carved, not set — a literal hand-cut letterform on flesh. Any "digital" type is limited to a small AIGA Detroit credit. Subservience: **1** — type exists only as body inscription.
- **Signature move**: collapsing the type/image distinction by making the body the medium — the poster's message and the poster's wound are the same mark.
- **What it teaches**:
  - When the lecture topic is "pain and design," the execution has to commit to the premise, not represent it
  - Collapsing type and image into a single act can be the whole concept
  - Shock photography works once; plan for the poster to live as a historical artifact, not a template
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `stefan-sagmeister`
- **Pair with briefs**: lectures/speaking events where the speaker has a provocative point of view, art-book covers, performance art documentation

### Oliviero Toscani — Benetton "United Colors" campaigns (1991–2000), including "Newborn" (1991), "David Kirby/AIDS" (1992), "Bosnian Uniform" (1994), "We, On Death Row" (2000)

- **URL**: https://www.benettongroup.com/en/media-press/image-gallery/institutional-communication/historical-campaigns/
- **Country**: Italy
- **Medium**: four-color offset on billboard and bus-shelter scale; commissioned documentary photographs and set-ups
- **Format**: billboards, bus-shelter posters, full-page magazine — scales from A3 to outdoor
- **Palette**: full-color documentary — no brand palette override. The only consistent element is the green "United Colors of Benetton" lockup dropped in a corner.
- **Composition**: a single documentary or near-documentary photograph — a blood-smeared newborn still attached to its umbilical cord; a man dying of AIDS surrounded by his family; a blood-soaked Bosnian soldier's uniform; portraits of death-row inmates. Photograph fills the entire sheet. The only text is the Benetton lockup, treated as a watermark more than a caption. Deliberately no product, no model, no fashion.
- **Typography**: Benetton green box lockup only — a corner mark sized to be ignored. Subservience: **1**.
- **Signature move**: using retail advertising real estate to stage documentary photography — the commercial frame as host for images that refuse commerce.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A photograph will carry the poster alone if you trust it — resist the urge to add explanatory type
  - Controversy is a tool, not a goal; Toscani's campaigns that endured are the ones tied to real events and real subjects
  - The corner-lockup pattern works when the photograph can't be ignored; it fails when the photograph is generic
  - Note: ethical concerns around Toscani's use of unconsenting subjects and shock-as-retail are ongoing — include in reference material with that caveat
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug*
- **Pair with briefs**: social campaigns, NGO work, fashion brands with a documentary position, museum photography exhibitions

### Neville Brody — The Face magazine covers, art direction 1981–1986

- **URL**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neville_Brody
- **Country**: UK
- **Medium**: offset magazine covers reproduced as subscriber and newsstand posters
- **Format**: magazine cover; shop posters at A2
- **Palette**: issue-specific, typically a flat saturated background color (pink, yellow, cyan, black) with a studio portrait
- **Composition**: a studio portrait of a cultural figure — musician, model, actor — crops face-and-shoulders against a flat color field, with Brody's custom hand-drawn "The Face" logotype at the top. A single cover line interrupts the portrait with aggressive typographic asymmetry. Photograph is hero; type slashes through.
- **Typography**: custom-drawn "The Face" masthead (evolving over issues), Franklin Gothic Condensed and custom display faces for cover lines. Subservience: **4** — photograph dominates, but Brody's cover type is loud enough to share billing.
- **Signature move**: using a single over-scaled photographic portrait against flat color, crossed with custom display type that fights the photograph on scale — the tension is the design.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Flat color background + portrait makes the photograph graphic — it separates the subject from any context
  - Custom masthead drawing gives a publication a voice no licensed type can
  - Cover lines should fight the photograph, not float above it
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `david-carson` (adjacent sensibility — next-generation anti-grid)
- **Pair with briefs**: cultural magazines, music covers, editorial portraits, fashion publications

### Annie Leibovitz — "More Demi Moore" (pregnant portrait), Vanity Fair, August 1991

- **URL**: https://100photos.time.com/photos/annie-leibovitz-demi-moore
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: commercial offset magazine cover; large-format subscriber and newsstand poster
- **Format**: magazine cover; poster reproductions at A2/A3
- **Palette**: warm skin tones (#D9B299), chestnut hair (#4F2C1B), off-black studio background (#0F0F10)
- **Composition**: seven-months-pregnant Demi Moore, nude, in profile-three-quarter turn, hands cradling breast and belly, lit sculpturally against a near-black studio backdrop. The figure occupies the central 70% of the frame. The Vanity Fair masthead sits across the top, smaller cover lines on the left margin. The photograph's silhouette does the composition.
- **Typography**: Vanity Fair masthead (Bodoni-derived display) at top; cover lines set in Bodoni small caps. Subservience: **3**.
- **Signature move**: a single studio portrait, classically lit, that is content rather than illustration — the image is the story, not a decoration for it.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A studio portrait holds a cover if the lighting and pose are specific enough to be the news
  - Magazine mastheads should be small enough not to compete with a strong photograph
  - Negative space around a figure (dark, clean studio) reads as editorial gravity
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Leibovitz*
- **Pair with briefs**: magazine covers, portrait-driven campaigns, artist/author monographs, fashion editorial

### Wolfgang Tillmans — Tate Modern 2017 exhibition posters ("Greifbar 29", "astro crusto, a")

- **URL**: https://shop.tate.org.uk/wolfgang-tillmans-greifbar-29-exhibition-poster/19768.html
- **Country**: Germany / UK
- **Medium**: offset on uncoated, reproduced from C-print originals
- **Format**: A2 exhibition posters
- **Palette**: per-photograph — "Greifbar 29" is a wash of turquoise, magenta, and indigo abstraction; "astro crusto, a" is a close-up of crustacean shell with deep red, amber, and ocean blue
- **Composition**: Tillmans' photographs fill the sheet edge-to-edge. Exhibition credits (title, Tate Modern wordmark, dates) compress into a narrow strip at the bottom. The photograph is not cropped to make room for type; type is sized to fit the remaining strip.
- **Typography**: Tate sans (custom) for institutional lockup and show credits. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: treating the exhibition poster as a reduction of the installation — the photograph is the work, the credits are the wall label.
- **What it teaches**:
  - When the artist's name is the draw, the photograph should not share real estate with it — put the name small, put the work big
  - Uncoated stock respects photographic color more honestly than gloss for abstract/chromatic subjects
  - Exhibition posters should feel like a page from the show, not a promotion for it
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Tillmans*
- **Pair with briefs**: solo-artist museum shows, photography exhibitions, gallery openings, contemporary art fairs

### Jeff Wall — "A Sudden Gust of Wind (after Hokusai)", 1993; exhibition poster (Tate and touring)

- **URL**: https://shop.tate.org.uk/jeff-wall-a-sudden-gust-of-wind-poster/28414.html
- **Country**: Canada
- **Medium**: original large-scale transparency in lightbox (250 x 397 cm); exhibition poster is offset reproduction
- **Format**: poster 50 x 70 cm with white border
- **Palette**: muted field-green (#7F8B5A), earth brown (#6A5240), sky gray (#B8B8B2), paper-white bursts (#F1EED9)
- **Composition**: Wall's staged tableau — four figures in a windswept landscape, papers exploding into the air mid-gust, restaging Hokusai's "Ejiri" woodcut — fills the image area. A generous white border surrounds the photograph like a museum mount; all type (artist name, title, year, institution) sits below the image in a clean credit line.
- **Typography**: institutional sans (Tate / Beyeler house type), small caption at bottom only. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: the museum-mount treatment — a generous border that says "this is an artwork, not an advertisement," and a caption that behaves like a wall label.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A photograph staged from a historical image deserves the museum-mount treatment — the border is the design
  - Constructed (composited, staged) photography can carry a poster the same way documentary photography can, if the scale is committed to
  - Let the image breathe; the border is part of the composition
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug*
- **Pair with briefs**: museum retrospectives, photography biennials, art book covers, gallery posters

### Annie Leibovitz — Rolling Stone / Vanity Fair portraits, "John Lennon & Yoko Ono" (December 1980) and related magazine covers

- **URL**: https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/photographer-annie-leibovitz-on-the-most-famous-photo-of-her-career-239743/
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: commercial offset magazine cover; later poster reproductions
- **Format**: Rolling Stone cover; poster reprint at A3/A2
- **Palette**: warm neutrals (#E5D8C3, #B8987B), deep black (#101014), pale blue (#8FA6B2), skin tones (#D5B29C)
- **Composition**: the famous December 1980 cover shows a nude John Lennon in fetal curl around a fully-clothed Yoko Ono — the Rolling Stone masthead sits in the top third, the photograph fills the rest. The intimacy is the whole composition; no cover lines distract. Leibovitz's portraits consistently use the full cover bleed, flat seamless studio backdrop, and dramatic single-source directional light.
- **Typography**: Rolling Stone masthead (custom), minimal cover lines at the edge. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: a portrait that reads as a private moment recorded in the moment before/after a tragedy — the photograph dated itself by accident when Lennon was shot hours after the session. The poster became a memorial.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single portrait at full-cover scale with no cover lines is the strongest possible use of a cover — it requires a photograph that can hold that weight
  - Seamless studio backdrop behind a portrait reads as timeless; environmental portraits date faster
  - Celebrity intimacy photographed without irony can become memorial — understand what you're committing to
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Leibovitz*
- **Pair with briefs**: celebrity/cultural figure covers, memorial editions, music magazine posters, portrait-driven campaigns

### Benetton "David Kirby" campaign — specific call-out, 1992

- **URL**: https://www.benettongroup.com/en/media-press/image-gallery/institutional-communication/historical-campaigns/
- **Country**: Italy / USA (photograph by Therese Frare, originally for LIFE magazine, 1991)
- **Medium**: four-color offset at billboard scale
- **Format**: outdoor billboard and magazine full-page
- **Palette**: domestic lamp-warm (#D9A96A), hospital-gray bedding (#C7C2B3), deep shadow (#1B1915), flesh tones (#C08F76)
- **Composition**: a single photograph — David Kirby in his final moments, surrounded by his family in a hospital room — frames the entire sheet. Warm directional lamp-light falls on the central figures; the rest of the room recedes into shadow. The Benetton green corner lockup is the only piece of typography. The image is journalism repurposed for retail real estate.
- **Typography**: Benetton green box lockup in a lower corner. Subservience: **1**.
- **Signature move**: importing a World Press Photo-winning documentary image into a commercial campaign — the ethical violence of the recontextualization is the campaign.
- **What it teaches**:
  - The same photograph can be journalism, advocacy, or advertising depending only on the frame it's placed in — and the ethical implications are real
  - Found / existing photography (licensed or appropriated) can produce work commissioned photography cannot
  - A lockup in a corner is not neutral — it claims the image for the brand

## Technique-specific rules learned from the canon

- **Trust the photograph.** If the image cannot carry the poster alone, don't add type to fix it — find a better photograph. Type-added-for-support usually signals a weak image. Saville's Closer, Leibovitz's covers, Tillmans' Tate posters all operate on the assumption that the photograph is sufficient.
- **Shrink the text.** Saville's Roman cap at Closer, Tillmans' Tate credit line, Toscani's Benetton lockup — the best photo-based posters use type at 2–4% of the sheet. If type creeps past 10% of area, you're drifting toward type-led. The exception is Brody-style editorial where the masthead is itself a designed artifact meant to share billing.
- **Decide the frame: full-bleed or museum-mount.** Two legitimate modes. Full-bleed (Toscani, Brody, Saville) says "this is the world." Museum-mount with border (Wall, gallery posters) says "this is art." A half-bleed with a clumsy border in between looks unresolved.
- **Match the photographic register to the subject.** Studio portrait (Leibovitz), documentary (Toscani), staged tableau (Wall), constructed/painted realism (Ishioka/Takino), body-as-surface (Sagmeister) — each has a different emotional vocabulary. Don't mix them in one campaign.
- **Color comes from the image, not the brand palette.** Photo-based posters almost never impose a brand palette over the photograph. The image brings its color; type and lockup accommodate. A green Benetton lockup on a blood-red image works because the green is a tiny corner flag, not a dominant wash.
- **Consider the ethics of found imagery.** When you appropriate a documentary photograph (Toscani/Kirby, Saville/Wolff cemetery) into a commercial or promotional context, the power relation between subject and author matters. Credit photographer explicitly; confirm rights; be prepared to answer for the reframing.
- **Type placement is not neutral.** Top-of-image masthead (Brody, Leibovitz) says "publication." Bottom-of-image caption (Wall, Tillmans) says "artwork." Corner lockup (Benetton) says "brand." Roman inscription at top (Closer) says "monument." Choose based on the register you want.
- **Uncoated paper respects color; coated paper flatters.** Tillmans' Tate posters print on uncoated stock because abstract chromatic photography needs the paper's tooth. Fashion and editorial typically print on coated because skin and product want shine. Stock is a register choice.

## Cross-reference

- **Style profiles in library**: `peter-saville` (austere photo-led treatment), `stefan-sagmeister` (conceptual body/skin photography), `david-carson` (related anti-grid sensibility for Brody context), `beeple` (contemporary digital hybrids where the line between photograph and render blurs)
- **Book principles**: Barthes, *Camera Lucida* (punctum, the arresting detail); Sontag, *On Photography* (ethics of the photographic gaze); Heller, *The Graphic Design Reader*; Shore, *The Nature of Photographs*; Berger, *Ways of Seeing*
- **When to use photo-based vs. related techniques**: photo-based is the correct choice when a face, a body, a moment, or a scene must be witnessed. If the subject is imaginative or otherworldly, move to illustration (see `illustration-posters.md`). If the subject is institutional or idea-based, move to type-only (`type-only-posters.md`).
