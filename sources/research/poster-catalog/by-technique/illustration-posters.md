# Illustration Posters — Catalog

*Posters built around hand-drawn or painted illustration as the hero. The image is authored, not captured — brush, pen, digital brush, or painted-then-scanned. Typography serves the illustration, often integrated into the picture plane. Use when the subject is imaginative (genre film, fantasy, science fiction, editorial metaphor, music/band imagery) or when the emotional register of a drawn line beats the emotional register of a photograph.*

## When this technique wins

- Genre cinema (sci-fi, horror, fantasy, animation) where the photograph can't deliver the world the film promises
- Music posters and alternative movie posters (Mondo, Bottleneck Gallery, Spoke Art culture)
- Editorial illustration — magazine covers and feature illustrations that argue rather than report
- Video game key art where character design is the product
- Literary covers, surrealist work, and subjects that live in metaphor

## When NOT to use it

- Documentary subjects where witness value matters (use photography)
- Serious institutional identity where drawing reads as decorative or juvenile (use type-only)
- Briefs where the subject must be recognized on sight by a mass audience unfamiliar with the illustrator

## Canonical works

### Kilian Eng — "Blade Runner 2049" screen print, Bottleneck Gallery / Mondo, 2024

- **URL**: https://bottleneckgallery.com/blogs/news/blade-runner-2049-by-kilian-eng-godzilla-minus-one-by-greg-ruth-on-sale-info
- **Country**: Sweden
- **Medium**: digital illustration screen-printed (24 x 36 in, edition of 375); foil variant (edition of 150); acrylic-panel edition
- **Format**: 24 x 36 in (standard Mondo one-sheet)
- **Palette**: smog orange (#E27140), pale cyan (#A6D8D4), pink dust (#E8A9B8), deep navy (#0F1B2E), bone (#E5DFC9)
- **Composition**: Eng uses the lone tree from Sapper Morton's farm as a central vertical axis, stacking the film's world above and below — sprawling industrial horizon receding into the smog sky, K in the foreground as silhouette. The illustration is strictly symmetrical, every element subordinated to the central tree; titles are reduced to a single-line credit at bottom.
- **Typography**: Mondo's standard bottom credit line (Akzidenz-Grotesk-family sans), film title as a small logotype lockup. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: worldbuilding via vertical compression — stacking multiple zones of the film into one symmetrical architectural illustration.
- **What it teaches**:
  - For sci-fi/dystopian films, illustration beats photography because the viewer has already seen the stills — the poster's job is to show what the stills can't
  - A strong central vertical axis lets you stack three zones (ground, middle, sky) into one composition
  - Spot-color screen printing enforces palette discipline: 4–6 colors max
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kilian-eng`, `syd-mead`
- **Pair with briefs**: dystopian sci-fi, retro-futurist, environmental/climate work, architectural/urban subjects

### Kilian Eng — "Oblivion" Mondo screen print, 2013

- **URL**: https://mondoshop.com/
- **Country**: Sweden
- **Medium**: screen print, Mondo edition
- **Format**: 24 x 36 in
- **Palette**: cold gunmetal (#3E4A52), radioactive teal (#4FB0A8), solar white (#F0EEDE), sand (#D4B68A), accent red (#C33A2E)
- **Composition**: a geometric, near-symmetrical aerial view of the film's bubble-ship and tower architecture, organized around a central horizon band. Eng's line work is tight, all shape, no texture — the composition reads as a technical diagram made luminous.
- **Typography**: bottom credit line; film title in the film's logotype. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: aerial geometric symmetry — treating the sci-fi set as an architectural elevation, not a dramatic scene.
- **What it teaches**:
  - When a film has iconic architecture or vehicles, draw them in elevation, not in narrative
  - Teal + gunmetal + sand is a canonical sci-fi palette — it reads "future" without reading period
  - Central horizon symmetry works for landscape-scale subjects better than asymmetric compositions
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kilian-eng`, `syd-mead`
- **Pair with briefs**: architectural sci-fi, clean-future tech brands, aviation/aerospace subjects

### Kilian Eng — "Heavy Metal" Mondo print

- **URL**: https://mondoshop.com/products/heavy-metal-kilian-eng-poster
- **Country**: Sweden
- **Medium**: screen print
- **Format**: 24 x 36 in
- **Palette**: hot magenta (#E0197A), sulfur yellow (#F4D03F), indigo (#2B1B5E), acid green (#5AB450), red (#C0322E)
- **Composition**: psychedelic space-opera stack — central figure (helmeted rider or craft) framed by radiating celestial geometry, alien architecture filling top and bottom thirds. Saturated pulp-magazine palette. The frame reads like a 1970s magazine cover enlarged.
- **Typography**: Heavy Metal logotype; bottom credit line. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: quoting 1970s pulp sci-fi illustration (Jean Giraud / Moebius lineage) without pastiche — the palette and compositional density are period, the line and color separations are contemporary.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Genre illustration rewards period quotation — the audience is literate in the tradition
  - High-chroma clashing palette (magenta + sulfur + indigo) reads "pulp" where muted palette reads "prestige"
  - Radiating symmetry delivers spectacle better than perspective
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kilian-eng`, `syd-mead`
- **Pair with briefs**: fantasy/sci-fi genre work, psychedelic music, comics/graphic novels, festival posters

### Josan Gonzalez (Death Burger) — "The Future Is Now" series illustrations (Citadel 9 / ArtStation)

- **URL**: https://www.artstation.com/josan
- **Country**: Spain
- **Medium**: digital illustration (Photoshop), offered as prints and poster-scale editions
- **Format**: typically A1/A2 print editions
- **Palette**: acid green (#3AEFA0), hot pink (#FF2D95), cyan (#00D4FF), deep black (#0A0A0A), caution yellow (#FFEA2B)
- **Composition**: dense maximalist cyberpunk scenes — rooftops, alleys, interiors stuffed with dozens of characters, signs, wires, and machinery. The picture plane is compressed; foreground/background hierarchy is flattened into pattern. Type, where it appears, is integrated as signage inside the illustration (shop signs, tattoos, billboard copy), not overlaid.
- **Typography**: no external type layer; typography is inside the scene as diegetic signage. Subservience: **0** — type is part of the illustration's world.
- **Signature move**: diegetic typography only — refusing poster-over-image type in favor of type that exists inside the fiction as signage.
- **What it teaches**:
  - In worldbuilt illustration, external title type breaks the spell — integrate text as signage, graffiti, tattoos, billboards
  - Maximalist density works when every element has internal logic; if the density reads as decoration it fails
  - High-chroma neon palette signals "cyberpunk" directly — don't over-explain with type
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `josan-gonzalez`
- **Pair with briefs**: cyberpunk and near-future subjects, comics/graphic novels, music videos, editorial features about technology and urbanism

### Yoji Shinkawa — Metal Gear Solid key art and promotional posters (1998–present)

- **URL**: https://www.creativeuncut.com/artist-profile-yoji-shinkawa.html
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: Pentel brush pen and ink on paper; later digital (Photoshop, Corel Painter) finishing
- **Format**: game cover art reproduced as promotional posters; exhibition prints
- **Palette**: bone white ground (#E8E2D0), ink black (#0C0C0C), chalk gray (#7D7A74), one accent — crimson (#B22A24) or khaki (#8B8760)
- **Composition**: a single character (Snake, Raiden, Ocelot) at near-full-body scale, framed by wet brush-ink expressive gesture. Empty paper-white ground carries most of the sheet. A second figure or mechanical element often ghosts in behind, rendered at 30% opacity. The brush mark is visible — drips, splashes, mis-strokes left on the sheet.
- **Typography**: Metal Gear Solid logotype (custom) and Kojima Productions credit at small scale. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: leaving the brush evidence visible — drips, splashes, and uneven strokes stay on the finished art. The "quick sketch" quality is the finished quality.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single character at poster scale on empty ground reads more heroic than a composed multi-character scene
  - Visible brushwork / ink-splash adds kineticism that clean vector can't
  - One accent color against black-ink-on-paper is the canonical samurai/martial aesthetic — use sparingly
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `yoji-shinkawa`
- **Pair with briefs**: character-driven video games, martial/military/samurai subjects, action films, manga-inflected editorial

### Tadanori Yokoo — "Koshimaki-Osen" poster, 1966

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7957
- **Country**: Japan
- **Medium**: silkscreen on paper
- **Format**: 41 1/2 x 29 3/8 in (105.4 x 74.6 cm)
- **Palette**: Japanese flag red (#D51C29), sun yellow (#F4C430), cobalt (#1B4B9C), black (#111111), flesh (#E8B899), white (#F9F4E8)
- **Composition**: vertical sheet densely collaged with ukiyo-e figures, a rising-sun ray pattern, a large graphic peach in the lower center, and a silhouetted hanging figure at the top. Japanese type (both traditional and modern) scatters through the image as integrated element. There is no single focal point — the eye moves through the surface as if reading a pachinko machine.
- **Typography**: hand-drawn and photo-composed Japanese type integrated into the image; no external caption. Subservience: **3** — type is part of the collage, not a layer on top.
- **Signature move**: Pop-collage that fuses American pop art, ukiyo-e, and advertising graphics into a single dense surface — the image is both poster and painting.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Multiple simultaneous focal points can work if each zone has internal color and line logic
  - Integrating display type as collaged element rather than as overlay gives the illustration unity
  - Cultural quotation (ukiyo-e, national flag motifs) charges an image with layered reading — be deliberate about what periods/traditions you quote
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Yokoo — consider adding*
- **Pair with briefs**: avant-garde theater, experimental film, subcultural events, fashion with cultural subtext

### Phil Noto — Star Wars / Marvel covers and poster illustrations (Poe Dameron series, Black Widow, Rogue One promotional, 2015–present)

- **URL**: https://philnoto.com/
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: digital illustration with painterly finish
- **Format**: comic-cover scale reproduced as prints; one-sheet promotional posters
- **Palette**: muted mid-century palette — burnt orange (#CA6B3E), teal (#4A8A8F), cream (#EDE4CB), burgundy (#7E2A2B), charcoal (#2E2E30)
- **Composition**: a single character in three-quarter portrait, often against a flat muted color field or a minimal environmental element (a cockpit, a rainy window, a doorframe). Negative space is generous. Painterly edges — no clean vector lines, no photo-real rendering — the drawing is visibly drawn.
- **Typography**: Marvel / Star Wars franchise lockups; Noto's compositions often keep type to top or bottom band. Subservience: **3**.
- **Signature move**: muted cinematic palette — refusing saturated comics color in favor of a palette that reads as 1970s Polaroid or mid-century magazine illustration.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Muted painterly palette reads as adult and cinematic where saturated comics palette reads as juvenile
  - A single character against flat color is enough — resist adding plot-illustrative background
  - Painterly edges (visible brush/pencil) signal authorship in a sea of photo-composited posters
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Noto — consider adding*
- **Pair with briefs**: character-led film and TV promotion, adult graphic novels, editorial portraits, prestige-franchise merchandise

### Tomer Hanuka — New Yorker covers and editorial illustration ("Perfect Storm", "Spring Awakening", "Take the L Train", 2014–present)

- **URL**: http://thanuka.com/
- **Country**: Israel / USA
- **Medium**: digital illustration (often with ink-drawing roots), reproduced at cover and poster scale
- **Format**: New Yorker cover format; gallery prints 18 x 24 in
- **Palette**: per-illustration — typically a 6–8 color vignette with strong complementary accents. "Perfect Storm" uses cold blue snowscape (#6B8CB0, #D8E4F1) against warm interior amber (#E7A45B, #C75E2E). "Take the L Train" uses subway-tile green (#7BA88C) with orange seat accents (#DE7F39).
- **Composition**: intimate urban narrative moments — two people at a window, a hipster and an orthodox Jew on the same subway car, a street scene under flowering trees. Figures are stylized but specific; perspective is shallow, theatrical. Lighting is directional and cinematic — one warm source against a cool field.
- **Typography**: The New Yorker masthead (Irvin typeface) at top, no other type. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: treating the illustration as a staged moment — characters positioned like a theater director blocked the scene, with cinematic light carrying the emotion.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Editorial illustration is storytelling by implication — show two people and an object and let the viewer write the scene
  - Warm-cool complementary lighting carries emotion more reliably than facial expression
  - A single moment at intimate scale beats a "capture the whole subject" panorama for editorial impact
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug for Hanuka*
- **Pair with briefs**: editorial magazine covers, short fiction promotion, urban subjects, relationship/intimacy themes

### Mondo poster series — general movement (2004–present; Rockwell, Taylor, Moss, Stout, Eng, Ruth, etc.)

- **URL**: https://mondoshop.com/
- **Country**: USA (Austin, TX)
- **Medium**: screen-printed editions (typically 150–400 units per artist variant), licensed through film studios
- **Format**: 24 x 36 in standard one-sheet; occasional 18 x 24 in and 36 x 24 in landscape
- **Palette**: per-artist; Mondo imposes no house palette, only a finishing standard (heavy ink, crisp registration, signed/numbered edition)
- **Composition**: Mondo's editorial logic is "reinterpret the film, don't illustrate the existing poster." Each artist brings a personal style — Tyler Stout maximalist character-stack, Jay Shaw minimalist conceptual, Kilian Eng symmetrical worldbuilding, Laurent Durieux Magritte-inflected mid-century. The shared trait is that type is always minimal and the illustration is always the authored reading.
- **Typography**: bottom-strip credit line consistent across editions; film title in the film's lockup or bespoke logotype. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: editorial curation — Mondo's value is not one style but the matching of artist-to-film. The poster is a reading, not a reproduction.
- **What it teaches**:
  - An alternative poster's job is interpretation — if it looks like what the studio would have approved, it has failed
  - Screen print edition culture forces palette discipline (4–8 colors)
  - A consistent house format (credit line, size, signing) lets wildly different styles live on the same wall
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kilian-eng`, `syd-mead` (for sci-fi context)
- **Pair with briefs**: film collectibles, genre film promotion, festival posters, repertory cinema

### Kilian Eng — "Koyaanisqatsi" Mondo print variant

- **URL**: https://www.worthpoint.com/worthopedia/kilian-eng-koyaanisqatsi-art-print-1981954091
- **Country**: Sweden
- **Medium**: screen print, Mondo variant edition (signed/numbered)
- **Format**: 24 x 36 in
- **Palette**: desert ochre (#C98A3E), clay red (#9C3A2B), sky pink (#E4B3A4), deep blue-black (#0E1B2A), bone (#EDE2C8)
- **Composition**: Eng's homage to the Reggio/Glass film stacks mesa, cloud, and city into a single vertical column — geological layers give way to concrete layers, each zone a flat color band with line-work detail. The composition refuses narrative and focuses on system/pattern, matching the film's philosophy.
- **Typography**: bottom credit line; film title in the film's original lockup. Subservience: **2**.
- **Signature move**: replacing characters with geological/architectural strata — treating the subject as pattern rather than story.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Philosophical or non-narrative films demand poster compositions that refuse character — use strata, systems, pattern
  - A vertical layered composition reads as "earth-to-sky" without needing to name it
  - Ochre + clay + deep blue is the canonical American-Southwest palette
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `kilian-eng`
- **Pair with briefs**: documentary and experimental film, environmental subjects, philosophical/conceptual content

### Sergey Timofeyev (Orbita) / Russian contemporary poster — illustrated poetry and arts posters, 2010s

- **URL**: https://www.arcpublications.co.uk/writers/sergey-timofeyev
- **Country**: Latvia (Russian-language Baltic arts milieu)
- **Medium**: mixed — commissioned illustration, screenprint, digital print for poetry-video and cultural events
- **Format**: A2/A1 cultural event posters
- **Palette**: limited two- or three-color — typically Constructivist-quoted red/black/white with one accent
- **Composition**: illustrated posters for the Orbita poetry-video collective and Latvian/Russian cultural events draw on Russian avant-garde tradition (Rodchenko, Lissitzky) — a single bold illustrated motif (a figure, an eye, a machine) against a flat geometric field, with Cyrillic type integrated at a strong diagonal or stacked on the margin.
- **Typography**: Cyrillic display type, often custom-drawn or Constructivist-quoted, integrated diagonally or as geometric block. Subservience: **4** — illustration and type share billing more equally than in Western illustration posters.
- **Signature move**: Constructivist quotation — Russian/Baltic illustration tradition uses the historic avant-garde vocabulary (diagonal composition, primary colors, photomontage-adjacent illustration) as continuing language, not pastiche.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Regional illustration traditions carry political/historical charge — they are not neutral stylistic choices
  - A two-color Constructivist poster commits the reader to a specific cultural position
  - Cyrillic integrated as a compositional element (not overlaid) respects the script's verticality and block character
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug*
- **Pair with briefs**: poetry and literary events, avant-garde theater, post-Soviet cultural programming, activist poster work

## Technique-specific rules learned from the canon

- **Integrate type into the illustration, don't layer it on top.** The best illustrated posters (Josan, Yokoo, Shinkawa, Hanuka) treat type as part of the picture plane — either diegetic (signage inside the fiction), collaged (pasted in as element), or compressed to a single credit band. Floating external type breaks the spell. The Mondo bottom-credit-line is the compromise that works because it's a shared house convention, not an aesthetic intrusion.
- **Commit to a finite palette.** Screen-print culture (Mondo, Eng) enforces 4–8 colors per poster. Even digital-output illustration (Noto, Hanuka) benefits from palette discipline — 6–8 colors, not 40. The palette choice is itself a genre signal: acid neon reads cyberpunk, muted mid-century reads prestige, high-chroma pulp reads psychedelia.
- **Show the hand.** Shinkawa's brush drips, Eng's screen registration, Bankov's paint edges, Hanuka's visible pencil under the color — authorial evidence separates illustration posters from stock graphic design. Clean vector without human evidence reads as anonymous. Even in digital work, simulating the press texture (halftone, offset misregistration, screen-print trap) gives the image a body.
- **Match the illustration register to the subject's fiction.** Cyberpunk wants maximalist density (Josan); prestige sci-fi wants architectural symmetry (Eng Oblivion); character-driven action wants solo-figure-on-empty-ground (Shinkawa); editorial wants intimate cinematic moment (Hanuka); pulp/heroic-fantasy wants radiating clash-color composition (Eng Heavy Metal). Don't default to one mode.
- **Respect the tradition.** Mondo's value is its literacy — artists quote Moebius, Hokusai, Rodchenko, 1970s pulp magazines. Illiterate pastiche (generic "retro") reads as decoration; literate quotation reads as conversation. Know what era and tradition you're sampling from, and why it fits the subject.
- **Composition is genre.** Central vertical symmetry = sci-fi worldbuilding (Eng). Dense flattened surface = cyberpunk (Josan). Solo figure + empty ground + brush gesture = martial/action (Shinkawa). Staged narrative moment + cinematic light = editorial (Hanuka). Radiating diagonal from center = psychedelia/pulp. Composition is not a neutral container; it carries genre signals.
- **Single character beats ensemble.** Character-driven illustration posters (Shinkawa, Noto) almost always isolate one figure. Ensembles dilute focus; the audience reads a crowd as "busy" rather than "epic." When you need to show multiple characters, stack them (Mondo Tyler Stout style) with one figure dominant and others as ghosts.
- **Hand-drawn type inside the illustration is better than overlaid logotype.** When the subject is imaginative and drawn, the typography should be drawn too. Shinkawa's titles are custom; Yokoo's Japanese characters are inseparable from the collage; Josan's signage is diegetic. A clean sans-serif credit line at the bottom is fine; a clean sans-serif title overlaid on the middle is not.

If the subject must be witnessed or recognized by face, move to photo-based (`photo-based-posters.md`). If the subject is institutional or abstract, move to type-only (`type-only-posters.md`).
