# Type-Only Posters — Catalog

*Posters where typography is the entire design — no photograph, no illustration, no hero image. The letterforms, the grid, the color field, and the negative space carry the full expressive load. Use when the subject is an idea, an institution, an identity system, or a cultural posture that must be argued through language itself.*

## When this technique wins

- Institutional and cultural identity (museums, theaters, record labels, transit authorities) where a consistent voice matters more than a single image
- Information-first posters: schedules, manifestos, signage, public notices where every word has to read
- Music releases that want to feel austere, serious, or conceptually loaded — type-only signals "we are not selling you a face"
- Typographic exhibitions, design lectures, and industry events where the medium is the message
- Subject matter that resists imagery — abstract concepts, political positions, protest, philosophy

## When NOT to use it

- Entertainment products that need a face, a star, or a spectacle to sell the emotional promise (most film, most pop music)
- Consumer brands whose product is visual (fashion, food, travel) where the thing itself must be pictured
- Briefs that need instant mass appeal across literacy/language gaps — type-only assumes the viewer will read

## Canonical works

### Massimo Vignelli, Bob Noorda, Joan Charysyn (Unimark International) — New York City Transit Authority signage & map system, 1970–1972

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/89300
- **Country**: USA (Italian-led studio)
- **Medium**: offset printed poster/map; signage system printed on porcelain enamel
- **Format**: subway map approx 57 x 45 in; station signs modular
- **Palette**: black (#000000), white (#FFFFFF), MTA line colors — Red (#EE352E), Blue (#0039A6), Green (#00933C), Orange (#FF6319), Yellow (#FCCC0A), Purple (#B933AD)
- **Composition**: the 1972 diagrammatic map reads as pure type-and-line composition — Helvetica station names clipped flush to colored 45/90-degree track lines, white field carrying the whole diagram, no illustration of geography. Stations are dots, names are labels, rivers are beige fields with the word "BROOKLYN" or "BRONX" sized as territory labels.
- **Typography**: Helvetica (originally specified Standard/Akzidenz-Grotesk, later rationalized to Helvetica). Set flush with the track. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: treating geographic information as pure typographic diagram — the line color IS the wayfinding, the station name IS the place. No decorative element.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A typographic system scales from pocket map to six-foot station sign if the grid, weight, and color logic are absolute
  - When type must carry wayfinding, one family at one or two weights beats a rich palette
  - Naming hierarchy (line color > station name > street exit) is the whole design problem
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli`
- **Pair with briefs**: transit / civic signage, museum identity, institutional wayfinding, schedule-heavy events

### Paula Scher (Pentagram) — "Bring in 'Da Noise, Bring in 'Da Funk" for The Public Theater, 1995

- **URL**: https://www.pentagram.com/work/the-public-theater
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: offset litho, commercial poster edition
- **Format**: ~ 46 x 30 in theater one-sheet
- **Palette**: black (#111111), red (#D62828), yellow (#F2C230), white (#FFFFFF) — later variants add green and pink
- **Composition**: American wood-type words stacked edge-to-edge with no margin, each word rendered in a different face and scale, reading top-to-bottom like a 19th-century broadside crossed with a subway car. Colors block the words into zones. Negative space is the thin slivers between letters — almost none.
- **Typography**: mixed American wood type (Morgan Foundry sources), layered with Akzidenz-Grotesk for credit type. Subservience: **10** — the type IS the noise, IS the funk.
- **Signature move**: shouting with mismatched historical wood faces — each word sized to fight the next, no quiet zone, the margin is the street.
- **What it teaches**:
  - When a show is about rhythm and volume, type can imitate a drum kit: big / small / block / italic / color / color
  - Historical wood type reads as street, protest, marquee — use it when the subject is vernacular, not corporate
  - Fill the frame. Margin is a choice, and the Public posters choose none.
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`
- **Pair with briefs**: theater, live music, festival, protest, any subject where the energy is "loud and in public"

### Paula Scher (Pentagram) — Public Theater season campaign (ongoing 1994–present)

- **URL**: https://www.pentagram.com/work/the-public-theater
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: offset, later digital large-format
- **Format**: bus-shelter, street-pole, one-sheet — the system scales
- **Palette**: rotating seasonal palettes anchored by a black/white/red base; each season introduces 2–3 secondary colors
- **Composition**: Scher treats the season as a typographic system — show titles set in a shared display family, spaced into a grid that reads as the Public's house voice. Individual posters foreground one title at near-poster-edge scale; campaign posters stack many titles as a single typographic field.
- **Typography**: the Public wordmark (custom, wood-type-derived), plus seasonal display faces. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: a programmatic typographic identity that stays recognizable across 30+ years while re-inventing the display voice each season.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Identity systems win by setting two or three fixed rules (wordmark, anchor color, one grid) and letting everything else vary
  - Season posters and campaign posters are two scales of the same system — don't design them separately, design the system
  - A type-only identity can carry 30 years if the rules are about voice, not about a specific typeface
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`
- **Pair with briefs**: performing arts seasons, festival identities, publisher seasons, any multi-title program

### Peter Saville — Joy Division "Unknown Pleasures" album & promotional poster, Factory Records FAC-10, 1979

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/collection/works/186051
- **Country**: UK
- **Medium**: offset lithography on textured card (album); offset poster (tour promo)
- **Format**: 12 x 12 in album; 22 x 44 cm and larger promotional posters
- **Palette**: black (#000000), white (#FFFFFF) — no third color
- **Composition**: centered on a black field, the CP 1919 pulsar plot sits as 80 stacked white lines in the upper-middle. No title, no artist name, no text on the front of the album. The brief was so austere that the diagram reads as a typographic glyph more than as an image. Promotional tour poster applies the same mark with tour dates set small in modernist sans.
- **Typography**: tour posters set dates in a tight sans (Helvetica / Univers family). Front cover: no type at all. Subservience: **8** — conceptually type-led because the pulsar diagram functions as a wordmark; actual typography appears only on sleeve back and posters.
- **Signature move**: refusing to put the band name or album title on the front — letting an austere diagram become the whole identity. Promotional posters amplify by keeping the diagram at the same scale and treating dates/venues as tiny captions.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Silence is a typographic choice — leaving text off the front is the loudest move
  - A found scientific/technical diagram can function as a typographic mark if the grid is absolute
  - When the artwork is already severe, tour-date text should get smaller, not more decorative
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`
- **Pair with briefs**: serious music releases, conceptual art shows, any brief where restraint is the pitch

### Experimental Jetset — Stedelijk Museum CS posters and printed matter (2004–2008)

- **URL**: https://www.jetset.nl/archive/smcs-posters
- **Country**: Netherlands
- **Medium**: offset lithography
- **Format**: 116 x 171 cm (grand opening poster) and smaller program posters
- **Palette**: black (#000000), white (#FFFFFF), airmail red (#E63946), airmail blue (#1D3557)
- **Composition**: the grand-opening poster is built like a page torn from a block calendar — the opening date at maximum possible scale, filling nearly the entire sheet, with institutional credits reduced to a single line. Program posters work the same calendar logic with show dates as the hero, stacked diagonals of red/blue airmail striping as the only non-typographic element (and arguably even that is treated as pattern-type).
- **Typography**: Univers (occasionally Helvetica), one weight at multiple sizes. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: date as hero. Instead of showing what's opening, show when — treating the calendar block as the most urgent piece of information.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A single typographic idea repeated across a season creates institutional voice faster than a logo does
  - One typeface + one color rule + a disciplined grid can carry an entire museum program
  - "The boring information is the design" — dates, venues, times treated at poster scale become iconic
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug — consider adding `experimental-jetset`*
- **Pair with briefs**: museum programs, exhibition openings, institutional calendars, modernist lectures

### Non-Format (Kjell Ekhorn + Jon Forss) — The Wire magazine redesign and covers, 2002–2010s

- **URL**: https://eyeondesign.aiga.org/the-first-thing-i-ever-designed-jon-forass-of-non-formats-modernist-grid-for-the-wire/
- **Country**: UK / Norway
- **Medium**: offset lithography (magazine), occasional screen-printed promotional posters
- **Format**: standard magazine format with poster-scale covers printed for subscriber and shop use
- **Palette**: issue-to-issue palette, typically 2–3 colors against white; frequent fluorescent inks
- **Composition**: Non-Format established a modernist grid at the base of the magazine, then reinvented a custom headline treatment for each issue — each cover is a standalone typographic experiment operating inside the grid. The feature name becomes a custom type composition; once used, it's abandoned, never reused.
- **Typography**: Neue Haas Grotesk / Akzidenz-Grotesk / Univers as grid type; custom drawn display type per issue. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: the disposable headline — a bespoke typographic construction that exists for one issue and is then retired. The grid is permanent; the type is ephemeral.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A strong grid earns the right to break the rules in the headline
  - Custom type drawn for a single use reads as more committed than a set typeface
  - In music design, refusing to illustrate genre with imagery (refusing to show a guitar, a DJ, a crowd) is the point
- **Pair with designer profiles**: *no current slug*
- **Pair with briefs**: music press, avant-garde cultural publications, lecture series, experimental festival programs

### Lars Müller Publishers — Helvetica Forever: Story of a Typeface, 2009

- **URL**: https://www.lars-mueller-publishers.com/helvetica-forever-0
- **Country**: Switzerland
- **Medium**: offset; publication and promotional posters for book launches
- **Format**: 19 x 26 cm book, with associated A2 and A1 launch posters
- **Palette**: black (#000000), white (#FFFFFF), Helvetica-red (#E1352C) used sparingly on launch materials
- **Composition**: launch posters present the word "Helvetica" at full bleed, set in Helvetica, with the book title and publisher credit as a single small line below. The self-referential move is the whole design: a poster about Helvetica, set in Helvetica, showing Helvetica.
- **Typography**: Helvetica and Neue Haas Grotesk only. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: tautology as poster design — let the subject be the form and the form be the subject.
- **What it teaches**:
  - When the subject is a typeface, setting the poster in that typeface is not lazy, it's honest
  - Publishing posters should feel like the book's interior logic scaled up
  - Red-on-white-on-black is the Swiss default for a reason: it always reads at distance
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli` (Swiss modernist lineage)
- **Pair with briefs**: book launches, academic lectures, typography conferences, design school promotions

### Build (Studio.Build / Michael C. Place) — typographic identity and poster work, 2001–present

- **URL**: https://www.michaelcplace.com/about
- **Country**: UK
- **Medium**: offset and screen-printed posters; frequent self-initiated print work
- **Format**: A1/A2 self-promotional posters and client identity pieces
- **Palette**: studio trademark: high-contrast black/white with one saturated spot color (often fluorescent pink, yellow, or cyan) per poster
- **Composition**: Build's posters stack tightly-kerned custom display letterforms with layered grids, data-like tick marks, and process annotations. The negative space is exaggerated — type often occupies less than 40% of the sheet, with the rest as a charged white or colored field.
- **Typography**: custom drawn display + Akzidenz-Grotesk / Neue Haas Grotesk for supporting copy. Subservience: **9–10** (occasionally a single abstract shape appears, but never an image).
- **Signature move**: treating typography as engineering — showing the construction lines, baselines, and measuring marks as part of the final composition. The "how this was made" is visible on the poster.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Exposing the grid and construction marks communicates craft and intentionality
  - A single fluorescent color against black/white reads as contemporary where four-color reads as retail
  - Custom display drawing beats font selection for identity work
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli` (grid tradition), `james-white-signalnoise` (contrasts — ornamentation vs. exposed grid)
- **Pair with briefs**: design studio self-promotion, engineering/architecture firm identity, technical/editorial journals

### Peter Bankov — poster series (Heroes of the Russian Avant-Garde; Neofuturism; Poster Exhibition), 2010s–2020s

- **URL**: https://www.moma.org/artists/48799-peter-bankov
- **Country**: Belarus / Czechia (Moscow / Prague based)
- **Medium**: screen print, digital print, riso; frequently hand-drawn typography reproduced
- **Format**: A1–A0 exhibition posters, designed in series
- **Palette**: saturated primary red, black, white, occasional bright yellow or cyan — one-color floods against hand-brushed textures
- **Composition**: Bankov paints or draws his letterforms by hand, then photographs or scans them — the poster is the hand-rendered word, often single-subject, filling most of the sheet with paint-drip edges and raw brush marks as evidence of the hand. Negative space is abundant and charged — 40–60% of the sheet is often flat color.
- **Typography**: hand-painted / hand-drawn Cyrillic and Latin display. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: painting the word. Bankov's posters are hand-lettering at poster scale — refusing digital type, refusing photographic reference, letting the letter be an object.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Hand-painted type carries political and emotional charge that digital type cannot
  - Post-Soviet / Russian / Eastern European design tradition leans into poster-as-protest — the type-only poster becomes an act
  - One word, one color, full bleed can be the whole brief
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher` (vernacular typographic energy)
- **Pair with briefs**: cultural festivals, activist/protest posters, exhibition series with an authorial voice, contemporary art

### Neue Haas Grotesk release campaign — Christian Schwartz / Font Bureau, 2010

- **URL**: https://www.fontbureau.com/fonts/neuehaasgrotesk/
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: offset launch posters and digital specimen book
- **Format**: specimen posters A1 and A2
- **Palette**: black (#000000), white (#FFFFFF), Swiss red (#EE2E24) as accent — mirrors the original 1957 Haas promotional material
- **Composition**: the revival specimen poster deliberately echoes mid-century Haas type-foundry advertising — large numeral or letterform as hero, face name set beneath at small scale, spec data arranged in clean columns on the lower third.
- **Typography**: Neue Haas Grotesk Display and Text at multiple weights. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: the single giant numeral or letterform — the sample that is also the subject. Shows the shape of the type at poster scale, answering "why should you license this?"
- **What it teaches**:
  - Specimen posters should show the type doing what it does, not describe it
  - A single character at massive scale beats a specimen grid for emotional impact
  - When reissuing a historical typeface, the promotional design should quote the period without pastiche
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `massimo-vignelli`
- **Pair with briefs**: typeface launches, foundry specimens, design magazine covers, typography conferences

### Paula Scher (Pentagram) — "The Tempest" and "Troilus and Cressida" Public Theater posters, 1995

- **URL**: https://www.pentagram.com/work/the-public-theater
- **Country**: USA
- **Medium**: offset litho
- **Format**: 46 x 30 in theater one-sheet
- **Palette**: black (#0A0A0A), bone (#EDE2C8), burnt orange (#D66A2B), cobalt (#1F3E8E)
- **Composition**: the play's title fills the sheet in stacked American wood type, each word a different weight and face, arranged as a column rather than a line. Subtitle credits ("Joseph Papp Public Theater / Shakespeare in the Park") run as thin strips between the title words. No image, no ornament.
- **Typography**: American Wood Type (Morgan foundry sources), overlaid with Akzidenz-Grotesk for credit blocks. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: treating Shakespeare as street — the wood-type broadside tradition applied to canonical drama, refusing the gilded-frame visual language theater usually gets.
- **What it teaches**:
  - Stacking the title vertically (rather than horizontally) lets each word be its own scale decision
  - Credit blocks woven between title words function as both information and rhythm device
  - Shakespeare plus wood type is a legible cultural position — making the canon feel like the street
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `paula-scher`
- **Pair with briefs**: classical theater, literary festivals, public-program identity

### Peter Saville — Factory Records "Section 25" / "A Certain Ratio" promotional posters, 1980–1982

- **URL**: https://factoryrecords.org/cerysmatic/fact10plus4.php
- **Country**: UK
- **Medium**: offset, sometimes with metallic ink
- **Format**: A2/A1 shop and gig posters
- **Palette**: silver (#C7CACF), graphite (#2A2D33), black (#0B0C0E), occasional blood red (#8C1A1A) on select releases
- **Composition**: severe typographic structure — catalog number (FAC-00) set at the top or as watermark, release title in a clean modernist sans occupying the upper half, release details stacked in a narrow column at the base. Large flat fields of silver or black carry most of the sheet.
- **Typography**: Helvetica / Univers, occasionally ornamented with Bembo or Bodoni Roman caps for contrast. Subservience: **10**.
- **Signature move**: Factory's "catalog-number-as-identity" move — giving a gig poster a FAC number and setting the number at display scale beside the band name, treating every release as an archived object.
- **What it teaches**:
  - A catalog-numbering system can function as a typographic identity device — the number gives every poster a place in the series
  - Large flat silver or metallic fields read as "austere and expensive" where chromatic fields read as "promotional"
  - Quoting classical Roman type against modernist sans creates a critical tension rather than a decorative one
- **Pair with designer profiles**: `peter-saville`
- **Pair with briefs**: record label identity systems, archival/research posters, serious music releases

## Technique-specific rules learned from the canon

- **Pick one family and one grid and commit.** Vignelli, Experimental Jetset, and Lars Müller all win by refusing to mix families. One typeface at multiple weights reads as voice; three typefaces reads as confusion. If the brief demands historical reference, choose wood type (Scher) or classical Roman (Saville) — just don't mix both with a neutral sans in the same poster.
- **Decide the subservience level on purpose.** Is the poster 100% type (Scher's Public Theater, Bankov) or is there one conceptual element that functions as a mark (Saville's pulsar, Experimental Jetset's airmail stripe)? The decision should be conscious, not a compromise. "Type with a small decorative flourish" is the failure mode.
- **Treat information as the visual.** Dates, show titles, line names, ticket prices — when these are the hero, set them at poster scale. The viewer reads them as the image. Experimental Jetset's grand-opening poster sets the date at 80% of sheet height because the date IS the message.
- **Exploit scale differential.** Type-only posters live on the contrast between the hero word (80% of the frame) and the supporting credit type (2–4% of the frame). A middle ground of moderately sized type reads as corporate. If everything is 18pt, nothing is heroic.
- **Earn decoration by exposing the grid.** If you add tick marks, baselines, or construction lines (Build, Non-Format), show them as craft. Purely decorative ornament without grid logic weakens a type-only poster. Ornament must be either structural (the grid made visible) or semantic (e.g., the airmail stripe referencing the museum's "in-transit" temporary building).
- **Hand-drawn beats licensed for authorial work.** Bankov's painted letterforms, Non-Format's bespoke headline drawings, Build's custom display — when the poster is about the designer's voice (studio promo, artist exhibition, activist work), custom drawing reads as commitment where licensed type reads as service work.
- **Color reads as institutional before it reads as decorative.** Type-only posters at 2–3 colors (Swiss red/white/black; Vignelli MTA line colors; Factory silver/black) function as identity systems. Color palettes over 5 colors in type-only work collapse into pattern and lose their institutional grip.
- **Margin is either absolute or abolished.** Either follow a strict modernist margin (Müller, Vignelli) or commit to zero margin with full bleed (Scher). The middle ground — a half-inch hesitation — signals indecision.

## Cross-reference

- **Style profiles in library**: `massimo-vignelli` (Swiss modernist discipline), `paula-scher` (vernacular wood-type energy), `peter-saville` (austere post-punk minimalism), `james-white-signalnoise` (useful contrast — ornamentation and retro-futurism against these austere examples)
- **Book principles**: Müller-Brockmann, *Grid Systems in Graphic Design*; Ruder, *Typographie*; Kinross, *Modern Typography*; Heller & Fili, *Typography Sketchbooks*; Lupton, *Thinking with Type*; Bringhurst, *The Elements of Typographic Style*
- **When to use type-only vs. related techniques**: type-only is the correct choice when the subject is an institution, an idea, or an information system. If the subject is a person, a moment, or a world, move to photo-based or illustration (see `photo-based-posters.md` and `illustration-posters.md`).
