# Ray Gun, Bryan Ferry Interview Spread (1994)

**Designer:** David Carson
**Year:** 1994 (Ray Gun magazine, issue approx. #20)
**Source URL:** davidcarsondesign.com (archived work section)
**Project type:** Editorial magazine spread
**Client:** Ray Gun magazine (Carson's own art direction)

## Brief
Lay out a Bryan Ferry (of Roxy Music) interview for *Ray Gun*. Carson, the art director, found the interview boring and decided to communicate that assessment visually rather than editorially. Result: the most legendary "design as commentary" move of the 1990s — Carson set the entire interview body text in **Zapf Dingbats** (a font of pictograms, not letters).

## Palette
- `#0E0E0E` — black (text, 40%)
- `#F5F5F0` — off-white / newsprint (background, 50%)
- `#8B2418` / `#A33020` — occasional oxblood red (accent elements, 5-10%)
- Inconsistent across full Ray Gun tenure — each spread chose its own minimal palette
- **Typical Carson:** monochrome + one accent, but not a hard rule

## Typography
- **Body text: Zapf Dingbats throughout** — the entire interview rendered as symbol/pictogram patterns. Completely illegible as text.
- **Headline:** distressed serif or custom grunge lettering (varies)
- **Bryan Ferry's name:** prominent but treated as photographic/textural element
- **Pull quotes:** occasional readable passages at random scales
- **Page numbers and credits:** standard-looking but oddly placed

## Composition
- **Two-page spread** disrupting expected magazine conventions
- **Photograph of Ferry** dominant on one side (often moody, low-contrast)
- **Text "column" of dingbats** occupying expected body-copy area
- **No visual hierarchy in text** because text is not text
- **White space weaponized** — places you'd expect content are empty
- **Tilted/rotated elements** common in surrounding pages

## Techniques observed
- **Typographic substitution** — font choice IS the content message
- **Reader participation required** — spread forces engagement with the meta-joke (you notice it's dingbats, you question it, you get it)
- **Magazine convention violation** — violates "spreads must communicate" doctrine Carson famously rejects
- **Photograph treated as texture** — image is part of layout, not subservient to it
- **Intentional illegibility** — this is the "end of print" moment: Carson proves communication doesn't require legibility

## Visual hierarchy (top to bottom importance)
1. The VIOLATION — reader immediately notices text is wrong
2. Ferry's photograph — anchors the spread visually
3. Headline — tells you who the piece is about
4. The meta-realization — "is this dingbats? is that the point?"
5. Any readable elements become precious / foregrounded by contrast

## What's stolen-worthy for other projects
- **Design as editorial commentary** — design can have a POV, not just execute a brief
- **Convention violation as rhetorical device** — breaking rules deliberately communicates something words cannot
- **Reader participation over broadcast** — spreads that ask reader to COMPLETE the meaning
- **Intentional friction** — hard-to-read can be a feature if the difficulty itself communicates
- **"The medium is the message" applied to type** — font choice AS content
- **Monochrome + one accent** on aggressive layouts — keeps rule-breaking focused on structure, not color

## Caveats
- This move **only works ONCE per context** — if you always set things in dingbats, you're no longer commenting
- Requires editorial authority — Carson could do this because he was art director with license
- Context is everything — this would be inappropriate for, say, medical instructions or legal documents
- Easy to misapply — "make it illegible" without a reason is just bad design, not Carson-level commentary
- The joke requires a reader smart enough to get it — plays well with Ray Gun's audience, wouldn't with a supermarket magazine

## Cultural impact
This spread became a shorthand for "design with a point of view" in design education. Still cited decades later. Launched a thousand imitations, most of which missed the point (illegibility without commentary). The genuine move: a considered, specific, editorial judgment rendered typographically.
