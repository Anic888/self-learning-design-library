# Visual Design Trends by Decade, 1900–2020s

*Quick-reference cheat-sheet. For each decade: dominant signatures, palette notes, key names. Use when a brief says "1940s" or "1970s flavor" and you need to conjure the look in under 30 seconds. Deep dives live in `sources/history/eras/`.*

## 1900s — Art Nouveau peak

- **Vocabulary**: whiplash curves, floral/botanical ornament, sinuous female figures, peacocks, dragonflies, ivy, lilies.
- **Layout**: organic asymmetry, hand-lettered type integrated with illustration.
- **Typography**: custom hand-drawn sans with swashes (proto–Art Nouveau display faces). Bernhard, Arnold Böcklin, Eckmann.
- **Printing**: chromolithography — up to 12+ spot colors, flat areas, fine linework.
- **Palette**: muted jewel tones. Ivory #F5E6D3, muted gold #C9A869, olive #8A8540, deep teal #2A5A6A, aubergine #4A2C44. Never pure black, never full saturation.
- **Key names**: Alphonse Mucha, Aubrey Beardsley, Koloman Moser, Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec, Louis Comfort Tiffany, Jules Chéret.
- **Spirit**: decorative, sensual, symbolic, slightly decadent.

## 1910s — Reduction and propaganda

- **Vocabulary**: Plakatstil (poster style) reduction, bold single-image posters, WW1 propaganda urgency, futurist speed lines.
- **Layout**: object-word-color; one dominant image, minimal type.
- **Typography**: heavy sans slabs, condensed gothics, thick German grotesks.
- **Printing**: fewer plates, flat color blocks, high-contrast silhouettes.
- **Palette**: restricted (wartime economy). Red #CC1F2A, black #1A1A1A, cream #F2E8D4. WW1 propaganda: khaki #8A7A4A, navy #2A3A5A. Futurist: mechanical grays + blood red.
- **Key names**: Lucian Bernhard, Ludwig Hohlwein, James Montgomery Flagg (Uncle Sam), Alfred Leete, Fortunato Depero, Umberto Boccioni.
- **Spirit**: urgent, reduced, propagandist, mechanized.

## 1920s — Constructivism + Bauhaus + Art Deco

Three simultaneous movements. Know which you're channeling.

- **Constructivism** (Soviet, 1917–1930): red #DC143C + black #000000 + off-white, diagonals, photomontage, "fist" imagery, Cyrillic sans. Rodchenko, El Lissitzky, Stenberg Brothers.
- **Bauhaus** (German, 1919–1933): primary red/yellow/blue + black, sans-serif grotesks (Akzidenz, Futura after 1927), geometric rigor, functional typography, asymmetric grids. Herbert Bayer, László Moholy-Nagy, Jan Tschichold.
- **Art Deco** (global, 1920s–30s): streamlined geometry, metallics (gold/chrome/silver), sunrise motifs, stepped forms, black lacquer. A.M. Cassandre posters = summit. Palette: ivory #F8EED2 + black + gold #D4AF37 + jade #00A388.
- **Spirit**: modernity declared, machine age embraced.

## 1930s — WPA regionalism + streamline

- **Vocabulary**: WPA poster program (1935–1943) = silkscreen, 2–4 spot colors, posterized gradients, National Parks imagery, workers/agriculture.
- **Streamline Moderne**: aerodynamic curves, speed lines, chrome, Zephyr-train aesthetic.
- **Pulp magazine illustration** (also 30s peak): lurid saturation, action poses, cover-dominant type.
- **Typography**: Futura, Kabel, hand-cut sans. WPA posters: chunky blocky sans.
- **Palette (WPA)**: deep forest #2A4A3A, sky #5A8AB0, mustard #C9A040, burnt orange #C85A2A, cream #F0E8D0. Posterized, limited, saturated.
- **Palette (Deco/Streamline)**: black + chrome silver + deep red + bottle green.
- **Key names**: WPA artists (Chet Crowell, Frank S. Nicholson), Norman Rockwell (starts), Bauhaus emigrants bring ideas to US.
- **Spirit**: optimistic through hardship, patriotic, industrial.

## 1940s — Wartime restraint

- **Vocabulary**: limited-color posters (paper and ink rationed), stencil typography, bold rationed design, "Loose Lips Sink Ships" propaganda.
- **Layout**: clear hierarchy, large typographic headlines, supporting illustration.
- **Typography**: stencil faces (Allied stencil), condensed sans, hand-lettered script for warmth.
- **Palette**: khaki #8A7A4A, olive #6B6A3A, navy #1E3A5F, rust #A84428, cream #F4E8D0, blood red #C8272A. Economy palette.
- **Post-war (late 40s)**: palette lightens, ad illustration returns (full color), pin-up art, Norman Rockwell Saturday Evening Post peak.
- **Key names**: J.C. Leyendecker, Norman Rockwell, Gil Elvgren (pinup), Jean Carlu, Abram Games.
- **Spirit**: first half — wartime discipline. Second half — rebuilding warmth.

## 1950s — Swiss modernism + mid-century playful

Split the decade: corporate Swiss = clean, cool. Consumer America = warm, playful.

### Swiss / International Typographic Style
- **Grid-driven**: Müller-Brockmann, Hofmann, Ruder.
- **Typography**: Helvetica (1957, née Neue Haas Grotesk), Akzidenz, Univers (1957). Sans-serif only. Often left-aligned.
- **Layout**: rigorous grids, generous whitespace, photographic imagery, red spot accents.
- **Palette**: black + white + single red accent (#D43A26 canonical).

### Mid-century consumer
- **Vocabulary**: atomic motifs, boomerangs, starbursts, Eames furniture, molded plastics.
- **Typography**: ITC Cooper Black, scripts, geometric sans, Saul Bass custom lettering.
- **Palette**: mustard #E2B53C, teal #4A9090, salmon #E88A75, avocado #6B8C3E, cream #F5EEDC. Warm plus cool pops.
- **Key names**: Saul Bass, Paul Rand, Alvin Lustig, Charles & Ray Eames.
- **Spirit**: optimistic futurism, post-war prosperity, nuclear family, jet-age.

## 1960s — Psychedelic + Pop + Polish

Three streams operating side by side.

### Psychedelic (1966–1969, San Francisco peak)
- **Vocabulary**: acid-trip lettering (vibrating, near-illegible), mandala symmetry, Victorian revival, blacklight posters.
- **Typography**: custom bulbous hand-letter (Wes Wilson, Victor Moscoso).
- **Palette**: vibrating complementaries — hot pink #FF2D92 + lime #00FF7F + orange #FF6B00 + purple #A020F0. Full saturation everything.
- **Key names**: Wes Wilson, Victor Moscoso, Stanley Mouse, Rick Griffin.

### Pop Art
- **Vocabulary**: commercial imagery appropriated, comic Ben-Day dots, flat bold colors, celebrity portraits.
- **Palette**: primaries — red #E71D36 + yellow #FFD700 + blue #0077BE + black outline.
- **Key names**: Andy Warhol, Roy Lichtenstein, Peter Blake, Richard Hamilton.

### Polish Poster School
- **Vocabulary**: surreal painterly posters for films/theatre, hand-painted, unconventional.
- **Palette**: ochre / burnt sienna / deep red / black, muted and painterly.
- **Key names**: Jan Lenica, Jan Sawka, Roman Cieślewicz, Waldemar Świerzy.

### Spirit of 60s generally
Counterculture, revolution, consumer irony, global design explosion.

## 1970s — Earth tones + airbrush + disco

- **Vocabulary**: handmade hippie holdovers (70–73), airbrush illustration peak (73–79), disco glam, album cover art golden age, punk emerges (77–79).
- **Typography**: ITC typefaces (Avant Garde, Souvenir, Bookman), Herb Lubalin, hand-script revival, Letraset dry-transfer era.
- **Palette**: earth tones dominant — mustard #C29430, harvest gold #DAA520, avocado #568203, burnt orange #C45410, brown #7A4A24, cream #F5E8C8. Secondary: disco chrome + hot pink.
- **Photography**: soft warm film, golden-hour aesthetic, Hipgnosis album covers (Pink Floyd etc.).
- **Disco aesthetic**: chrome #C0C0C0, magenta #D02080, gold #D4AF37, black mirror backgrounds.
- **Punk (late 70s)**: DIY photocopy, ransom-note lettering, safety pins, Jamie Reid Sex Pistols covers. Black + hot pink + xerox gray.
- **Key names**: Herb Lubalin, Milton Glaser (I ♥ NY, Bob Dylan), Push Pin Studios, Peter Saville (late), Jamie Reid, Storm Thorgerson (Hipgnosis).
- **Spirit**: first half — mellow organic. Second half — glitter and rupture.

## 1980s — Memphis + New Wave + early digital

- **Vocabulary**: Memphis Group pastels + patterns (1981–87), new wave grids, MTV graphics explosion, early Macintosh pixel art, airbrush still hanging on, anime arrives in West late decade.
- **Typography**: Emigre magazine (1984) launches digital type revolution, April Greiman layers, Neville Brody at The Face, punk → post-punk Factory records (Peter Saville).
- **Palette (Memphis)**: neon pink #FF3DA5, cyan #00D4FF, lemon #FFE156, mint #A8E6CF, lavender #D4A5E8, black. Pastel + loud simultaneously.
- **Palette (New Wave / MTV)**: neon magenta #FF1493 + electric blue #0066FF + black + chrome.
- **Palette (corporate 80s)**: mauve #B08AA8, teal #008B8B, peach #FFCBA4, gray #9B9B9B, black — corporate gym / dentist office.
- **Key names**: Ettore Sottsass (Memphis), April Greiman, Neville Brody, Peter Saville, Roger Dean, Patrick Nagel (Duran Duran covers).
- **Spirit**: maximalism returns, technology optimism, excess, first desktop creativity.

## 1990s — Grunge + rave + first digital chaos

- **Vocabulary**: David Carson deconstruction (Ray Gun magazine 1992–95), grunge distress, rave flyers, rave acid colors, first web aesthetics, anti-design reaction to 80s corporate.
- **Typography**: FUSE magazine experiments, Emigre peak, distressed fonts (Trixie, Suburban, Dead History), layered semi-legible type.
- **Palette (grunge)**: desaturated earth + black + rust #8B3A1A + moss #4A5D23 + cream, muddy.
- **Palette (rave/acid)**: acid green #00FF00, hot pink #FF00FF, cyan #00FFFF, yellow #FFFF00. Full saturation, neon, glow. Smiley face icon.
- **Palette (corporate 90s)**: teal #008B8B, mauve, peach, beige — carpet pattern, dental office, mall food court.
- **Photography**: heroin chic (Corinne Day, Juergen Teller), Kate Moss emaciation, flash-on-camera flat. Counter: Herb Ritts glamour bw.
- **Web (1995+)**: GeoCities under-construction aesthetic, animated GIFs, Times New Roman, blue #0000FF hyperlinks, Comic Sans (1994).
- **Key names**: David Carson, Stefan Sagmeister, Designers Republic, Me Company, Tomato, Jamie Hewlett (Tank Girl, Gorillaz later).
- **Spirit**: rupture, slackerism, anti-corporate, rave-hedonic, first global digital.

## 2000s — Y2K + flat web + homogenization

- **Vocabulary**: Y2K chrome / translucent plastic (1998–2003), iPod silhouettes (2003), Web 2.0 gradients + gloss + rounded corners (2005–2009), hipster Brooklyn aesthetic (Lomography, fixie bikes, beards).
- **Typography**: Helvetica documentary (2007) revives Helvetica cult, Gotham (2000) and Gotham-style geometric sans dominate corporate, Obama 2008 campaign (Hope poster, Shepard Fairey).
- **Palette (Y2K chrome)**: silver #C0C0C0, iridescent pastel holograms, clear plastic, baby blue #A4CFFF.
- **Palette (iPod)**: white #FFFFFF + black silhouette + single-color saturation accent.
- **Palette (Web 2.0)**: gradients glossy blue→sky, green "go" #2ECC40, orange CTA, drop shadows, reflections.
- **Palette (corporate/homogenized)**: navy #1A365D + slate gray + white. "Safe" everywhere.
- **Photography**: clean product shots on infinity white, 5D Mark II (2008) launches indie cinema bokeh obsession, early Instagram-style filter nostalgia (Hipstamatic 2009).
- **Key names**: Shepard Fairey, Stefan Sagmeister (peak), Pentagram (Paula Scher, Michael Bierut), Apple design team (Jonathan Ive).
- **Spirit**: optimism + branding rationalization + first smartphone.

## 2010s — Flat + geometric sans + pastels

- **Vocabulary**: flat design reaction to skeuomorphism (iOS 7, 2013), long-shadow fad (2013), geometric sans everywhere (Gotham, Circular, Proxima Nova), Instagram pastels, Airbnb/Uber "startup look," pattern overlays, lifestyle photography with lens flare.
- **Typography**: Circular, Gotham, Avenir Next, Proxima Nova, Inter (2017), Söhne (2019). Geometric sans total domination. Marker Felt banished. Serif revival late decade for "premium" (2018+).
- **Palette (startup flat)**: flat pastels — mint #BAE1D3, blush #F4C2C2, periwinkle #B8C5D6, cream #F5F1E8, charcoal #2D2D2D. Dunn's pastel.
- **Palette (Apple iOS 7+)**: pure white #FFFFFF + accent single color (iOS blue #007AFF), minimal.
- **Palette (Instagram 2015)**: filtered washed — faded teal, muted peach, warm highlight, crushed blacks.
- **Palette (neon return late 2010s)**: hot pink #FF006E + cyan #3A86FF + lime #06FFA5, Glossier-style.
- **Pattern overlays**: geometric/tropical repeats (flamingos, tropical leaves 2016–18), Memphis revival.
- **Key names**: Apple design team post-Ive, Stefan Sagmeister & Jessica Walsh, Pentagram current, Bruno Maag (type), Kris Sowersby (type).
- **Spirit**: reduction, democratization of design, startup aesthetic homogenizes globally.

## 2020s — AI era + dreamcore + anti-design

- **Vocabulary**: AI-generated imagery (Midjourney 2022+, Nano Banana 2025+), bento layouts (Apple 2022), anti-design brutalism / low-fi web (Are.na aesthetic), dreamcore muted pastels (A24, Sofia Coppola palette), aurora gradients (Stripe, Figma, Linear), Y2K 2.0 revival (2022–24), retro-futurism, editorial serif revival (Canela, PP Editorial Old), cinematic color grading dominant on video.
- **Typography**: Variable fonts mainstream, editorial serifs (PP Editorial, Migra, Ogg, Canela), Söhne, Inter updates, custom type commissions boom, monospace "technical" aesthetic (JetBrains Mono, Commit Mono).
- **Palette (dreamcore / A24)**: dusty lavender #C4B5D0, peach #F5C9B0, sage #A8C09A, sand #DDD0B8, midnight blue #1B2A47, blush #E8B7B7. Muted, nostalgic, vintage-film.
- **Palette (aurora gradient)**: pink #FF6B9D → purple #A259FF → blue #3E8BFF → cyan #00E5FF, soft gradient flow.
- **Palette (brutalist / anti-design)**: harsh — default system fonts (Arial, Times), raw HTML aesthetic, single accent color (red #FF0000 or lime #00FF00), minimal CSS, intentional "ugly."
- **Palette (Y2K 2.0)**: chrome + iridescent holograms + baby blue + hot pink, updated with better gradients.
- **Layout**: bento grids (multiple framed tiles), full-bleed imagery with overlay text, asymmetric magazine returns, sticky-scroll horizontal, 3D CSS accents (Spline, Three.js).
- **Photography**: AI-gen "feels off" aesthetic — unreal but convincing, color-graded, cinematic. LUTs from Frank Ocean / Sofia Coppola / A24. Film revival (Portra 400, Ektar 100).
- **Key names**: Linear design team, Figma design, Stripe design, Ilya Sutskever era AI (Midjourney, OpenAI), independent typographers (Pangram Pangram, Grilli Type, Displaay).
- **Spirit**: maximalist pluralism, algorithmic saturation, anti-AI craft revival running parallel to AI-gen tsunami, retro-futurism, blurred past/present/future.

## Quick validation checklist (for "make this feel like [decade]")

1. Is the palette era-correct? No anachronistic hex codes (e.g. no neon in 1940s, no earth-tones in 1980s).
2. Is the typography era-correct? No Helvetica pre-1957, no Gotham pre-2000, no Futura pre-1927.
3. Is the printing/production plausible? No full-bleed photo in 1900s (chromolitho only), no CMYK bleed before 1950s.
4. Is the layout era-correct? No Swiss grid pre-1950s, no geometric sans dominance pre-1980s in popular use.
5. Does the photography / illustration style match the era's technology (airbrush peak 70s, film grain era-appropriate)?
6. Palette saturation level right? Pre-war muted; 60s full sat; 90s muddy or acid.
7. Cultural signifiers correct? (No Memphis in 1970s; no psychedelic in 1950s.)
8. Is the key name you're channeling actually from that decade?
